## 4. Control Structure

| Feature | Detail | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Dual-class / super-voting | **None.** Single class of registered shares, $0.01 par, **one vote per share**. No super-voting or founder shares. | `FY25 10-K, cover p.1 l.93`; `FY25 10-K, l.129` |
| Controlled-company status | **Not a controlled company** in the >50% sense. No single holder controls a majority. But a **~34% Viterra-seller bloc (Glencore ~17% / CPP ~14% / BCI ~3%)** is a material concentration created July 2, 2025. | `FY25 10-K, Risk Factors l.1579–1597`; `Note 2 l.5507` |
| Shareholder bloc with board rights | **Yes — contractual board-nomination rights.** Under shareholder's agreements (closing of the Viterra acquisition), **Glencore and CPP Investments EACH** may designate **two** directors at ≥10% ownership, and **one** at 5–10%. At current stakes that is potentially **four** bloc-nominated board seats. BCI (~3%) has a lock-up but no nomination right disclosed. | `FY25 10-K, Risk Factors l.1582–1591`; `Exhibits 4.3/4.4 (Glencore/CPP Shareholder's Agreements) l.3898–3901` |
| Supermajority / blocking thresholds | Statutory mergers and **withdrawal of preemptive rights for new-share issuance** require a **two-thirds (2/3)** majority of votes represented at the general meeting. A ~34% bloc voting together can **materially influence, and in low-turnout meetings potentially block,** such 2/3 actions. The 10-K states this concentration "could discourage" a change-of-control transaction. | `FY25 10-K, Risk Factors l.1591–1597` |
| Swiss-law capital constraints | Board may issue up to ~16% (capital band, 33.6M shares, expires Oct 19, 2028) and ~15% conditional capital; buybacks limited to 10% of par unless shareholder-authorized for cancellation. Preemptive rights protect minorities against dilution unless withdrawn by the 2/3 vote above. | `FY25 10-K, Risk Factors l.1638–1661` |

**Read on control:** The vote is **one-share-one-vote with no dual-class** — structurally fair on its face. The real minority-shareholder issue is the **~34% Viterra-seller bloc with up to four contractual board seats and the swing weight to influence 2/3 supermajority votes** (mergers, dilutive issuances). This is **disclosed, contractual, and not itself a red flag**, but it is a genuine governance overhang: a single aligned group of former sellers — one of which (Glencore) is also a global commodity-trading competitor/counterparty — can shape board composition and corporate-action outcomes in a way that did not exist before July 2025. Whether their interests stay aligned with minority holders on a future transaction is the open risk.

## 5. Ownership / Insider Read

Skin-in-the-game is **unproven and almost certainly thin**: management is professional, not owner-operator (no founder, no controlling stake), the beneficial-ownership table is missing, and every insider transaction visible is **granted/comp-mechanical — no open-market buying with cash and no discretionary selling**, so there is **no insider-conviction signal in either direction**. The defining ownership fact is **control concentration, not alignment**: a ~34% Glencore/CPP/BCI Viterra-seller bloc that **received** (not bought) its stock now holds up to four contractual board seats and enough swing weight to influence the 2/3 supermajority votes that govern mergers and dilutive issuances — the single biggest structural risk to minority holders, sharpened by Glencore simultaneously being a trading competitor. Pledging is clean (none disclosed) and there is one share class with one-share-one-vote, so the governance machinery is not abusive on its face — but until the 2026 proxy supplies the ownership table, insider holdings, and AGM voting results, this read is **capped and incomplete (Shareholder-friendliness max 60)**.
