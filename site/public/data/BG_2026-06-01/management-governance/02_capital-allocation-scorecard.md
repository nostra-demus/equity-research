# Capital-Allocation Scorecard — BG

*Bunge Global SA (NYSE: BG). Swiss-domiciled (Geneva), US-SEC domestic filer. Reporting currency **USD millions** unless noted; fiscal year ends Dec-31. FCF = CFO − total capex (CLAUDE.md). Net debt = total debt − cash (CLAUDE.md). "Adjusted Total EBIT"/"adjusted EPS" are the company's non-GAAP measures, labeled as such wherever used. Window: **FY2023–FY2025** (the 10-K presents three years of income and cash-flow statements; no five-year selected-data exhibit), plus Q1 FY2026 where the deal-financing and capital-return framework are clarified. Primary sources: FY2025 10-K (filed 2026-02-19), Q1 FY2026 10-Q, Q1 FY2026 transcript (2026-04-29). Cross-module anchors: `business-model/11_capital-allocation-governance`, `earnings/01_historical-financials`, `earnings/99_earnings-synthesis`. The 2026 DEF 14A is NOT in the pool, so insider participation in buybacks/dividends and option-dilution detail are out of reach (owned by agent `04`).*

*This module runs BEFORE valuation. Buybacks are therefore judged ONLY on observable evidence (net share-count change, price vs the trading range, leverage at the time, SBC offset, insider benefit) — NOT against intrinsic/fair value. Intrinsic-value discipline is "not assessable from this module."*

**One fact dominates this scorecard — flagged once, applies throughout:** the **Viterra business combination closed July 2, 2025** ($10,617M total consideration), the largest deal in company history. It lifted share count ~44% in one year and roughly tripled total debt. Every use-of-capital figure below straddles that mid-2025 line, so the 3-year flows are part pre-deal capital return and part deal financing.

**Read on M&A.** The defining capital decision of the window is the **$10,617M Viterra deal, and it is not yet judgeable on returns.** The only delivered, dated evidence is the **>$70M year-1 cost-synergy print** that management calls "ahead of plan" [`FY25 10-K, CEO letter, p.1`] — a down-payment, not a full-cycle result. The portfolio pruning (corn milling, Spanish stake) is coherent and cash-positive, but it is rounding error next to Viterra. This is a **large acquisition whose rationale is stated but whose per-share payoff is unproven** — it is logged against RF-CAP-001 as a watch item (rationale is disclosed and specific, so it does not trip the "vague rationale" trigger to Red), and re-scored when a full post-Viterra year and the Investor-Day synergy target are in hand.

**Read on buybacks.** On the one signal that matters most — **did buybacks reduce the share count** — the answer for FY2025 is **no.** The company repurchased 6.75M shares for $551M at ~$81.64, but issued 65.6M shares for Viterra, so diluted/outstanding share count rose ~44% (133.96M → 193.41M) [`FY25 10-K, F-14`]. The buyback offset roughly **10%** of the deal issuance; the 12.38M treasury-share cancellation is a Swiss capital-band mechanic, not a net reduction. That trips **RF-CAP-002** (buybacks not reducing share count) as a flag, though here the cause is a disclosed, deliberate stock-funded acquisition rather than buybacks silently funding option grants. On **price discipline by the only observable yardstick**, the picture is two-sided: the FY25/Q4'25 buying ($81.64 avg; $79.77 in October) was done **below** the program's inception-to-date average ($94.64) — i.e., recent tranches were cheaper than historic ones, which is the right direction — but I cannot test it against fair value (out of module). On **leverage timing**, management did the prudent thing: it **paused** the buyback after October 2025 (zero in Nov–Dec, $249.4M left on the authorization) with leverage elevated, and for FY2026 has only committed to finishing the remaining **$250M "Viterra-portion" buyback**, gating further repurchases on the new "return ~50% of discretionary cash flow" framework and the leverage trajectory [`Q1 FY26 transcript, l.636–660`]. **Insider participation cannot be assessed** — no proxy. Net: a small, opportunistically-priced, prudently-paused buyback that was structurally swamped by deal dilution — not a per-share-reducing program in this window.

**Incremental ROIC (Δ NOPAT / Δ invested capital).** *Inference, not from filings — and heavily contaminated by the partial-year Viterra consolidation and the spread down-cycle; presented for direction only.* Using adjusted Total EBIT × (1−24% tax) as a NOPAT proxy, NOPAT fell from ~$2,304M (FY23) to ~$1,546M (FY25) — a **−$758M** change — while invested capital (total debt + total equity incl. NCI) rose from ~$17,183M (FY24) to ~$31,420M (FY25), a **+$14,237M** change. Δ NOPAT / Δ invested capital ≈ **−5%**. The honest reading is not "−5% returns" — it is that **the capital base grew by ~$14.2bn in one year while NOPAT declined**, so on a same-year basis the incremental capital earned nothing visible yet. This is exactly what you expect from a stock-and-debt-funded acquisition that closed mid-year into a soft crush-spread cycle; it is **not proof of value destruction**, but it is the opposite of evidence of incremental value creation, and it will only become a clean read after a full post-Viterra year.

**Read on dividends & reinvestment.** The dividend is **modest and growing** (raised to $2.80, ~37% of adjusted / ~57% of GAAP EPS) but in FY2025 it was **not covered by free cash flow** — CFO ($844M) did not even cover capex ($1,723M), so FCF was −$879M before a dollar of the $459M dividend [`earnings/01 §1`]. That trips **RF-CAP-003** (dividends not covered by FCF) for FY2025. Three mitigants keep it Amber, not Red-Critical: (1) FCF was positive and covered the dividend in FY23 (5.7×) and FY24 (1.4×), so this is a one-year, deal-and-working-capital-driven gap, not a structural shortfall; (2) FY2025 CFO was depressed by a commodity-trader working-capital build on the enlarged combined book, not by an earnings collapse [`business-model/11`]; (3) the company retains $9bn+ of unused committed liquidity [`Q1 FY26 transcript, l.185–188`]. On **organic reinvestment**, the harder problem is structural: capex is running at ~2.5× depreciation in a deliberate growth phase, but the company's **own disclosed headline ROIC of 6.7% sits below its own 7.2% cost of equity** (computed ~5.1%) [`Q1 FY26 transcript, l.190–202`; `business-model/09`]. Only the CIP-/cash-adjusted 8% clears the hurdle, and that figure strips out the very projects still under construction. Reinvesting heavily at or below the cost of capital is the central per-share concern, independent of Viterra.

## 5. Capital-Allocation Read

Over FY23–FY25 capital was deployed into one transformational, stock-and-debt-funded acquisition (Viterra, $10,617M, 175% of three-year CFO) on top of a routine return program ($3,471M of buybacks + dividends, 57% of CFO), and the per-share verdict is **not yet earned**: buybacks did not reduce the share count (it rose ~44% on the deal), FY2025 dividends were not covered by free cash flow, and the company's own disclosed ROIC (6.7%) sits below its own cost of equity (7.2%) — so on observable evidence this is reinvestment at or below the cost of capital, not demonstrated per-share value creation. The **best-judged use** was the disciplined, opportunistically-priced **buyback pause** (recent tranches at $79.77–$81.64 vs a $94.64 inception-to-date average, then halted with leverage elevated) and the cash-positive portfolio pruning (corn milling, Spanish stake); the **worst** is the unproven Viterra bet — ~44% dilution plus a $7.8bn debt step-up for a >$70M year-1 synergy down-payment and $2.66bn of still-preliminary goodwill. The single biggest capital-allocation signal is that **the defining decision of the period dilutes shareholders today and must convert into synergies, deleveraging, and EPS accretion before it can be called value-creative — and that conversion is unproven**. Verdict: **mixed**.
