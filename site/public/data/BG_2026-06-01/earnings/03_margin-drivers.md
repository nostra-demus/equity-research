# Margin Drivers — BG

**Company:** Bunge Global SA (NYSE: BG). **Reporting currency:** US$ millions unless noted. **Fiscal year:** ends Dec 31. **FY0 = FY2025** (10-K); **latest quarter Q0 = Q1 2026** (10-Q, quarter ended Mar 31, 2026).

**Three caveats inherited from `01_historical-financials.md` — they govern every table below:**
1. **Viterra acquisition closed mid-2025** (registered shares issued July 2, 2025). It roughly doubled quarterly revenue from ~$12–13bn to ~$22–24bn from Q3 2025, lifted D&A and interest expense, and makes year-over-year margin comparisons not like-for-like [FY25 10-K, Note 2].
2. **Segment redefinition (Q3 2025) and Sugar & Bioenergy exit** moved discontinued results into Corporate and Other, enlarging the corporate drag [FY25 10-K, MD&A, lines 2153–2169]. In Q1 2026 the "Other Oilseeds Processing and Refining" segment was renamed **"Tropical Oils and Specialty Ingredients"** (same segment, name only) [Q1 2026 10-Q, Note, line 376].
3. **GAAP margins swing on non-cash mark-to-market timing.** Bunge marks commodity/freight/FX contracts and readily-marketable inventory to fair value through Cost of goods sold. Q1 2026 GAAP Total EBIT $184M vs adjusted $561M — a $377M gap, of which $336M is mark-to-market timing [Q1 2026 release, p.1]. GAAP single-period margin moves are an unreliable read of operating performance; both GAAP and adjusted series are shown and labeled.

**Pricing-power context (from business-model module):** Bunge is rated **"Squeezed" — a price-taker on inputs AND outputs** [06_value-chain.md, §4]. Raw-material cost tracks net sales nearly one-for-one; what Bunge keeps is the *spread* (crush margin / merchandising margin), set by commodity exchanges, not by any contractual escalator Bunge controls. This is the central fact for margins: Bunge has almost no discretionary pricing lever, so margin is a spread it captures, not a price it sets.

## 1. Segment Decomposition Status

The business-model `03_segment-map.md` exists and was read. Bunge reports **four segments** plus Corporate and Other [FY25 10-K, Note 26]. This is **not** a single-segment business — the top segment (Soybean Processing and Refining) is 51.6% of revenue and 52.6% of reportable Segment EBIT, below the 85% single-segment threshold [03_segment-map.md, §2].

**Segment-level P&L IS disclosed in granular form.** Note 26 gives, for each segment, a full cost decomposition — Raw materials cost, Industrial expenses (fixed and variable), Depreciation, SG&A, Other segment items, and Segment EBIT — for FY2023/2024/2025 [FY25 10-K, Note 26, lines 8360–8431], and the same lines for Q1 2026 and Q1 2025 [Q1 2026 10-Q, Note, lines 1660–1700]. This is unusually detailed and **allows margin drivers to be decomposed per segment** (Section 6).

Three structural points govern the decomposition:
- The four segments are economically similar — all thin-margin (2.5%–4.6% EBIT) commodity oilseed/grain origination-processing-merchandising businesses. Treat them as one commodity-processing complex with the soybean crush spread as the single largest swing factor [03_segment-map.md, §2].
- The four reportable segments earned $2,329M Segment EBIT in FY2025, but **Corporate and Other consumed −$796M** (up from −$367M in FY2024), leaving Total EBIT of $1,533M [FY25 10-K, p.37]. The corporate drag is now more than half the size of total EBIT and must be tracked as its own margin driver.
- Inter-segment revenue is large (FY25 eliminations ~$4.2bn) and margin can move *between* origination, processing, merchandising and refining inside a segment ("the power of the total system") [Q1 2026 transcript, line 449], so within-segment line attribution is directional, not precise.

| EBITDA margin (Total EBIT + D&A) | 3.18% | 4.26% | **−108 bps** | Same spread compression, partly masked because D&A rose 50% on Viterra assets (a smaller EBITDA fall than EBIT) | FY25 10-K, p.37; CF line 4830 |

**Cleaner operating read (adjusted, strips mark-to-market and one-offs):** Adjusted Total EBIT margin fell from 3.80% (FY24, $2,017M) to 2.89% (FY25, $2,034M) — **−91 bps** — a milder compression than the GAAP −119 bps, because GAAP FY25 carried the pension settlement, impairment and integration charges [adjusted Total EBIT from Capital IQ normalized series, data as of 2026-05-09; per `01_historical-financials.md` §4]. Label: adjusted figures are management-defined.

**Latest quarter, GAAP:** Q1 2026 gross margin **3.50%** vs Q1 2025 **5.13% = −162 bps** — the only clean standalone-quarter GAAP comparison in the data pool [Q1 2026 10-Q, lines 117–119; Q1 2025 10-Q]. But Q1 2026 GAAP is depressed by $336M of mark-to-market timing; on an **adjusted** basis Q1 2026 segment EBIT was $661M vs $406M a year earlier — adjusted segment-EBIT margin ~3.02% vs ~3.49%, only −46 bps, i.e. the operating spread held far better than the GAAP gross margin implies [Q1 2026 release, p.1].

**Pass-through and lag (input costs are a material driver, so stated explicitly):** Input-cost moves wash through **almost fully and within ~1 quarter or less**. Both legs (crop bought, meal/oil sold) are priced off the same commodity exchanges; ASC 606 customer contracts settle "generally 30–60 days," and ~76% of FY25 net sales are ASC 815 commodity contracts marked-to-market continuously [FY25 10-K, revenue policy lines 5403–5405; 06_value-chain.md, §2]. There is **no contractual margin-on-top escalator** — Bunge does not keep a fixed margin when input costs rise; it keeps the market-set spread. So a rise in the *bean* price is near-neutral to margin if the *meal + oil* price rises with it; margin only moves when the **spread between the legs** changes. Crucially, management "doesn't lock in the margin until all three legs are priced" [Q1 2026 transcript, line 582] — the spread, not the lag, is the risk.

## 4. Margin Walk — Which Margin Level Matters Most?

**The primary margin metric for Bunge is adjusted Segment EBIT margin, with consolidated gross margin as the closest GAAP proxy.** Justification: (1) Bunge is a capital-meaningful manufacturer-trader where D&A is real and rising (FY25 $703M, guided ~$975M FY26), so a pure gross-margin view would ignore a step-change in depreciation from Viterra and growth capex — gross margin alone overstates the durable margin. (2) Bunge's own CODM measure and the metric management guides and that analysts model is **Segment EBIT / Total EBIT**, not EBITDA or net margin [FY25 10-K, Note 26, lines 8449–8456]. (3) GAAP gross margin and GAAP EBIT are corrupted quarter-to-quarter by mark-to-market timing (Q1 2026 GAAP gross margin −162 bps YoY but adjusted segment EBIT *up* 63%), so the **adjusted** Segment EBIT series is the only stable operating read — but it must be labeled management-defined each time. Net debt/EBITDA also makes EBITDA relevant for the balance sheet (5.78x GAAP-built at FY25-end), but for *operating* margin the EBIT level is the one that matters. EBITDA margin is reported above for completeness but is the least useful of the three here because the D&A add-back is exactly the line that is structurally rising.

## 8. The Single Biggest Margin Driver

**The crush / processing spread — specifically the soybean crush spread — is the single biggest margin driver, and if it moved adversely it would compress margins the most.** It is the gap between the exchange-set soybean cost and the exchange-set (meal + oil) price, and Bunge controls neither side [06_value-chain.md §4]. The evidence of magnitude is in the segment numbers, not adjectives: Soybean Processing EBIT moved $2,222M → $872M → $1,225M across FY2023–2025 (≈±60% swings) on a near-stable asset base, and the Soybean segment is ~80% of total company EBIT once the corporate drag is netted [FY25 10-K, p.37; 03_segment-map.md §2]. A compression of this spread has no offset — Bunge has "no contractual lever, supplier concession, or customer price increase to defend its margin" [06_value-chain.md §5]; management literally cannot lock a margin "until all three legs are priced" by the market [Q1 2026 transcript, line 582]. **Current direction: Tailwind.** As of Q1 2026 the spread is improving — soybean and softseed processing "primarily drove" the beat, FY2026 adjusted-EPS guidance was raised to $9.00–9.50 from $7.50–8.00 mostly on these two segments, and a favorable EPA RVO is expected to draw down soy-oil stocks and support the oil leg into H2 [Q1 2026 transcript, lines 95–96, 104–106, 210, 498–509]. The risk is the symmetry: the same spread that doubled guidance in two months can compress just as fast, the forward curves are "heavily inverted" with limited H2 visibility, and the spread is hostage to weather (El Niño risk), the Middle East conflict's effect on freight and fuel, and the durability of biofuel policy [Q1 2026 transcript, lines 320–321, 461–475]. One driver, both directions, no contractual defense.
