# External Dependency Check — HCG

## 3. Classification

**Partly externally driven.**

HCG is a rupee-in, rupee-out domestic oncology hospital operator with structural (cancer-incidence-driven), largely non-discretionary demand. Financial market variables are minor-to-moderate: FX and commodity exposure are immaterial, and interest-rate exposure — while the largest disclosed sensitivity and rising with a leveraging, expansion-funding balance sheet — is partly within management's control (rights-issue-funded debt repayment, fixed/floating mix). The genuinely material and uncontrollable forces are government healthcare policy (DPCO drug price control, margin capping, the licensing/accreditation regime) and payer behaviour (insurance/TPA/government-scheme reimbursement and disallowances). These set a ceiling on pricing and a floor under bad-debt risk. But management retains real levers: it has shifted payer mix favourably (+100bps), is deliberately replacing low-margin business to lift average revenue per patient, and controls bed additions, clinical-talent mix and cost. So this is neither a pure pass-through nor company-controlled — it sits in the middle, with policy/regulation as the dominant exogenous overhang.

## 4. External Dependency Risk Score

**45 / 100** (INVERTED scale — higher = worse).

Band: 41–60 — "Material external exposure, mixed mitigation."

Rationale: two High-dependency variables (government policy, regulation) that management cannot control and that directly cap pricing and licensing, pulling the score above the "partly externally driven / hedgeable" band (21–40). Offsetting this: immaterial FX and commodity exposure, non-cyclical demand, demonstrated pricing/mix and deleveraging levers, and India-domestic simplicity, which keep it well below the "mostly externally driven" band (61–80). The score is consistent with the "Partly externally driven" classification.

## 5. The Single Biggest Lever

**Government healthcare pricing policy (DPCO drug price control and margin capping).** A 20%-adverse tightening — for example, deeper margin caps on medicines/consumables or expanded price ceilings on oncology drugs and devices — would compress the 25.72%-of-revenue cost-of-consumption spread and squeeze margins directly, with no hedge available; management itself flags that "price control or capping of margins on medicines has been stepped up" and is "impacting industry margins" [FY24-25 AR, MD&A "Risks and concerns", p.149, p.166].
