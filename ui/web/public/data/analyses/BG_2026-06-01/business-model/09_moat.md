# Moat — BG

Bunge Global SA (NYSE: BG). This report inherits the named competitors from competitive-map (`08_competitive-map.md`) and tests whether anything makes it hard for *those specific* rivals to take Bunge's profits. Upstream competitive-map and segment-map were both present; no degraded-confidence flag is needed. The four reportable segments are treated as one commodity oilseed/grain-processing complex, consistent with segment-map. Scoring bands per CLAUDE.md: 0–20 very weak, 21–40 weak, 41–60 mixed, 61–80 strong, 81–100 very strong. Higher = stronger moat (not inverted).

## 1. Named Competitors

(Inherited verbatim from competitive-map; none invented here. Source for all: [FY25 10-K, Item 1, Soybean Processing and Refining segment, p.3 / lines 307–309].)

- **ADM (Archer Daniels Midland, NYSE: ADM)** — the most directly comparable public oilseed processor-trader; competes head-to-head in all four Bunge segments; FY2025 revenue ~$80.3bn, ~1.1x Bunge's $70,329M net sales.
- **Cargill Incorporated (private, US)** — largest named rival, FY2025 revenue ~$154bn, but broader than Bunge (includes meat/protein); a partial, not pure, crush comparable; discloses no segment economics.
- **Louis Dreyfus Company B.V. (private, Netherlands)** — closest pure-play oilseed/grain-trader by business mix; FY2025 net sales $53.2bn (~0.76x Bunge), more weighted to merchandising.
- Secondary named rivals: **Wilmar International (SGX-listed, Asia-Pacific palm/oilseed)** and **COFCO International (private, China state-owned)**.

## 3. Competitive Economics

For the private competitors and ADM there are no margin or ROIC figures in the data pool. The single Louis Dreyfus datapoint is an EBITDA margin from an unverified web release, not an EBIT margin, so it is not comparable on a like-for-like basis. No invented numbers.

Anchor read on Bunge alone: gross margin fell 8.14% (FY23) → 6.39% (FY24) → 4.85% (FY25) [07_business-quality §1]; Total EBIT fell $3,333M → $1,792M → $1,533M [FY25 10-K, line 2373]; headline ROIC of 6.7% sits **below** the company's own stated 7.2% cost of equity, and only the adjusted 8% (which strips out Viterra construction-in-progress not yet earning) clears it [Q1 FY26 call, lines 190–192, 201]. A moat is supposed to produce returns *above* the cost of capital; Bunge's disclosed returns are at or below it.

## 4. Where The Company Sits

**Insufficient data to compare against named peers** — the data pool contains no ADM, Cargill, or Louis Dreyfus margin or ROIC figures, so Bunge cannot be ranked top / median / bottom of its named peer set on capital efficiency. On its own absolute economics, Bunge earns a thin, falling, sub-cost-of-equity return (gross margin 4.85%, headline ROIC 6.7% vs 7.2% cost of equity) [FY25 10-K, lines 4690–4692; Q1 FY26 call, lines 190–192].

## 5. Moat Verdict

**Narrow moat.**

The strongest moat is **scale (50/100)** — Bunge's global, 200-year, 50-country processing and logistics network, reinforced by the July 2025 Viterra acquisition that removed one independent ABCD competitor [FY25 10-K, lines 254–258; 08_competitive-map §4]. That scale is real and hard to replicate, and it is what keeps the industry a four-player oligopoly. But it is a *narrow* moat, not a strong one, for two evidenced reasons: (1) the same scale-and-logistics advantage is shared by ADM, Cargill and Louis Dreyfus, so it does not specifically wall off Bunge's profits from those rivals; and (2) it has not translated into observable margin or ROIC superiority — gross margin is 4.85%, EBIT margin 2.18%, and headline ROIC of 6.7% sits below Bunge's own 7.2% cost of equity [FY25 10-K, lines 2373, 4690–4692; Q1 FY26 call, lines 190–192]. The durability test it must pass over the next five years: the post-Viterra integrated network must lift sustained, cycle-average ROIC clearly above the cost of capital (the company's adjusted 8% needs to become a through-cycle reality, not a CIP-adjusted figure), *and* the crush/refining spread must hold despite competitors adding capacity and petroleum entrants bidding for renewable-diesel feedstock [FY25 10-K, lines 1030–1032]. If returns stay at or below cost of equity through the next cycle, the scale advantage is a moat in structure but not in economics, and the verdict would fall to "No moat proven." A direct peer margin/ROIC export (ADM, Louis Dreyfus) would be the single most valuable input to confirm or break this read.
