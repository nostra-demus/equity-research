# Unit Economics — BG

Dominant segment taken from segment-map: **Soybean Processing and Refining** — 52.6% of reportable Segment EBIT and 51.6% of net sales in FY2025, the value driver on both measures [analyses/BG_2026-06-01/business-model/03_segment-map.md, §2]. Segment-map also flagged that the four segments are economically one commodity oilseed-processing complex and that the single largest company-wide swing factor is the soybean crush spread. This report builds the unit table on that dominant segment.

## 1. Natural Unit

The natural economic unit for Soybean Processing and Refining is **one metric ton of soybeans processed (crushed)** — Bunge's own stated unit of measure for the segment is the metric ton [FY25 10-K, MD&A, line 2145, 2206]. Secondary unit types in the same segment are the merchandised ton (soybeans traded to third parties, not crushed) and the refined-oil ton (a downstream, partly overlapping volume); the company reports all three but only the processed ton represents the core conversion economics [FY25 10-K, MD&A, lines 2158–2161, 2398–2401].

Caveat carried from segment-map: Bunge does NOT disclose a standalone soybean crush margin per ton. Segment revenue and raw-material cost combine the processing book and the lower-margin merchandising book, so per-ton figures below are blended segment economics, not a pure crush spread. Management states the margin "can move around between origination, processing, merch and refining" and is not locked "until we have all three legs" [Q1 2026 earnings call transcript, Q&A, lines 442–443, 582].

## 3. Value Creation Read

**Unclear, leaning marginal value creation at the segment level.** Each processed ton carries a thin slice of profit: ~$30 of segment EBIT per processed ton on ~$885 of revenue (3.4% EBIT margin), and the segment earns a ~7.5% pre-tax return on its $16.3bn of assets [FY25 10-K, Note 26, lines 8366–8381]. That is above zero and rising (EBIT +40% in FY25), so a processed ton is not destroying value. But the whole-company headline ROIC of 6.7% sits below the company's own stated 7.2% cost of equity, and only the *adjusted* 8–9% figures clear it — and those adjustments strip out the large Viterra-related construction-in-progress that has not yet started earning [Q1 2026 call, lines 190–192, 201]. The classic unit-economics test (contribution margin × unit lifetime > acquisition cost) cannot be run cleanly: there is no per-ton acquisition cost, no plant payback disclosure, and the contribution figure ($72/processed ton) is contaminated by the lower-margin merchandising book. The single most valuable missing disclosure is a **standalone soybean crush margin (or processing margin) per ton, processing-only, split from merchandising** — without it, the per-unit profit cannot be separated from commodity price pass-through, and management explicitly declines to lock or break it out [Q1 2026 call, lines 442–443, 582].

## 4. Sensitivity

The input that would most change the read is the **crush/processing margin per ton (price spread)**, not volume. A 20% move in the per-ton spread swamps the model: segment EBIT is only $30/processed ton on $885 of revenue, so the profit-to-revenue gap is ~3.4%; a 20% swing in the realized crush spread (well within historical range for this commodity-processing book) would move segment EBIT by a multiple of any plausible 20% volume change, because raw-material cost ($32,852M) is ~90% of sales and is largely a pass-through that the spread sits on top of. Volume matters less: the segment already runs near ~64% of nameplate capacity, and a 20% volume change moves revenue ~20% but leaves the thin spread intact, so EBIT scales roughly linearly rather than explosively. Acquisition/build cost is the least sensitive near-term lever — capex is ~64% of segment EBIT and affects future capacity, not the current per-ton economics. On historical volatility, the segment's own three-year EBIT swing is the evidence: $2,222M (FY23) → $872M (FY24) → $1,225M (FY25), a >60% drop then a 40% rebound on single-digit volume changes [FY25 10-K, line 2410]. That confirms the spread, not the ton count, is the volatile variable that decides whether the unit creates value in any given year — and makes this a commodity-conditional, not a company-specific, unit-economics profile.
