# Value Chain Position — BG

Bunge Global SA (NYSE: BG). Primary sources: FY2025 Form 10-K / Annual Report (year ended Dec 31, 2025) and Q1 FY26 10-Q / earnings call in the data pool. Upstream input read: `02_business-identity.md`. Bunge is a global oilseed-crush and grain-merchandising processor-trader earning thin spreads on very high physical volumes [02_business-identity.md, §3].

## 1. Stages Occupied

Bunge sits in the *middle* of the agricultural value chain. It buys crops from farmers (upstream), and sells meal, oil, grain and refined products to food/feed/biofuel businesses (downstream) [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.3–5]. It is not a raw-material owner (it does not, materially, grow crops — exception: a portion of Brazilian sugarcane via leased land [FY25 10-K, p.5, line 412–413]) and only a thin slice is end-consumer-facing brand. "Bargaining Power vs Upstream" = power over farmers/intermediaries it buys from. "Bargaining Power vs Downstream" = power over the customers it sells to.

Bargaining power bands:
- **Strong:** Company sets price, dictates terms, has alternatives
- **Mid:** Negotiated outcomes, no extreme leverage either way
- **Weak:** Price-taker, terms imposed, few alternatives

The pattern: Bunge has **Mid** power upstream (it buys from a fragmented base of farmers, so no single supplier dictates to it — but farmers control their own selling timing and the price is set by exchanges, not by Bunge) and **Weak-to-Mid** power downstream (it sells global commodities that are "highly price competitive" and "subject to product substitution," so it is a price-taker on the bulk of volume). Only the specialty/branded and milling slices reach Mid downstream.

## 2. Input Cost Pass-Through

Bunge passes input-cost changes through almost completely, by construction — but pass-through is not the same as pricing power. Raw-material cost is the dominant expense and tracks net sales nearly one-for-one: Q1 FY26 net sales of $21,861M produced cost of goods sold of $21,095M and gross profit of just $766M, a ~3.5% gross margin [Q1 FY26 10-Q, condensed income statement, line 117–119]. Mechanically, the pass-through is automatic because both the crop Bunge buys and the meal/oil it sells are priced off the same commodity exchanges: all of Bunge's readily-marketable inventory is carried at fair value using "commodity futures exchange quotations, broker or dealer quotations, or market transactions," and fair-value changes flow straight through Cost of goods sold [FY25 10-K, Note 1, line 5201–5204; Note, line 3249]. There are no disclosed long-term contractual escalators or indexed price clauses giving Bunge a margin on top — instead, both legs float with the market. The lag is short: ASC 606 customer contracts settle "generally 30-60 days," and ~76% of FY25 net sales are ASC 815 commodity contracts that are marked-to-market continuously [FY25 10-K, revenue policy line 5403–5405; 05_customer-geography.md, §1]. So input cost moves wash through quickly and almost fully — but what Bunge keeps is the *spread* between input and output, and that spread is set by the market, not by a pass-through clause Bunge controls.

## 3. Customer Pricing Power

Bunge cannot raise price independently of the commodity market without losing volume on the bulk of its book. Its own 10-K states the products are "global commodities, the markets for our products are highly price competitive, and in many cases also sensitive to product substitution" [FY25 10-K, Item 1A, line 1034]. Competition across every core segment is "principally based on price, quality, product and service offerings, and geographic location" [FY25 10-K, p.3–5, lines 293–294, 330–331, 429–430]. The clearest evidence that Bunge does not set the price is management's own description of how it captures margin: "for us, we don't lock in the margin until we have all three legs priced" — meaning Bunge waits until exchange prices for seed, meal and oil are all observable, then captures the spread between them; it does not name a price to either side [Q1 FY26 call, Q&A, line 582]. There were no discretionary list-price increases disclosed in the last 24 months that held volume; instead, the relevant pricing actions are market-driven. Management noted refining premiums "while they're not—" available at will [Q1 FY26 call, Q&A, line 428], and described biofuel-blending economics as set by crude/diesel prices and the EPA RVO, not by Bunge: the EPA RVO decision swung Q1 FY26 adjusted-EPS guidance from ~$0.85 to ~$2.00 in two months — a >100% move on a policy decision, not on any pricing action Bunge took [10_external-dependency.md, §3; Q1 FY26 call, Q&A]. The narrow exceptions are the specialty/branded oils, soy protein concentrate and milling lines, where competition adds "brand recognition, product innovation, technical support" [FY25 10-K, p.4, line 377–379] — there Bunge has some negotiated pricing power, but these are a minority of revenue (Other Oilseeds 6.6% + Milling ~2% of net sales) [03_segment-map.md, §1].

## 4. Economic Control Verdict

**Squeezed** — price-taker on inputs AND on outputs.

Both the crop Bunge buys and the products it sells are exchange-priced commodities Bunge does not set: it buys "directly from farmers or indirectly through intermediaries" at market and sells products whose markets are "highly price competitive" and "subject to product substitution" [FY25 10-K, p.3, line 292; Item 1A, line 1034]. Its margin is a *spread* it captures only once all legs are priced by the market — "we don't lock in the margin until we have all three legs priced" [Q1 FY26 call, line 582] — confirmed by a 3.5% gross margin in which raw-material cost tracks revenue nearly one-for-one [Q1 FY26 10-Q, line 117–119]. The pressure runs both ways: farmers can withhold supply (on-farm storage "allows producers to retain commodities for extended periods and increase price pressure on purchasers such as us" [FY25 10-K, Item 1A, line 1037–1038]) and Bunge must advance $835M to Brazilian farmers just to secure supply [FY25 10-K, line 1313], while downstream customers buy fungible commodities with low switching cost and 30-60 day settlement [FY25 10-K, line 5403]. What partial control Bunge has comes from scale, asset position and logistics ("the power of the total system" [Q1 FY26 call, line 449]) and from the specialty/branded/milling slices — but those do not change the dominant economics: Bunge is a spread-taker squeezed between an exchange-priced input and an exchange-priced output.

## 5. The Single Biggest Bargaining Risk

The crush/processing spread itself — the gap between exchange-set soybean/softseed cost and exchange-set meal+oil prices — because Bunge controls neither side of it: if the spread compresses (as Soybean Processing EBIT did from $2,222M in 2023 to $872M in 2024, −61% [10_external-dependency.md, §3]), Bunge has no contractual lever, supplier concession, or customer price increase to defend its margin.
