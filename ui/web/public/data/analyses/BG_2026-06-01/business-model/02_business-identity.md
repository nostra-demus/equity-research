# Business Identity — BG

## 1. What The Company Actually Does

Bunge buys oilseeds and grains from farmers, processes them, and sells the resulting meal, oil and grain products to food makers, animal-feed and livestock producers, and biofuel refiners around the world [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.3–5]. Its single biggest activity is crushing soybeans and softseeds (canola/rapeseed and sunflower): it turns the seed into protein meal (for animal feed) and vegetable oil (for food and biodiesel/renewable diesel), and refines some of that oil into food-grade and specialty products [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.3–4]. Alongside processing, it runs a large grain-merchandising business — originating, storing, shipping and trading corn, wheat, barley, cotton, pulses and sugar — and mills wheat and sugar, primarily in South America [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.5]. Customers are mostly other businesses: feed mills, livestock and biofuel producers, food processors and bakeries; a smaller slice is branded and private-label packaged oils sold through grocery and food-service channels [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.3–5]. It operates in more than 50 countries across North and South America, Europe and Asia-Pacific [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.3]. What customers pay Bunge for is access to crops and processed ingredients in the right place, at the right time and quality, with the price and supply risk managed for them — Bunge's role is to physically connect where crops are grown to where they are consumed [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.3]. On July 2, 2025 Bunge closed its stock-and-cash acquisition of Viterra, which materially enlarged its soybean processing, softseed processing, and grain-merchandising operations [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.5; Q1 FY26 10-Q, MD&A Segment Overview].

## 2. How The Company Makes Money

Bunge is a spread business, not a price-of-the-commodity business. It books large gross revenue from selling physical commodities but keeps a very thin margin: Q1 FY26 net sales of $21,861m produced gross profit of just $766m, a gross margin of roughly 3.5% [Q1 FY26 10-Q, condensed income statement, p.~3 (line 117–119)]. Raw-material cost is the dominant expense and tracks net sales almost one-for-one (e.g., Soybean Processing raw-material cost of $8,662m against $9,552m of external net sales in Q1 FY26) [Q1 FY26 10-Q, Note 19 Segment Information]. The economics differ by segment:

- **Soybean Processing and Refining** (largest by net sales, $9,552m external in Q1 FY26): `Profit ≈ crush volume × crush/processing spread + refined-oil margin + merchandising margin`. Spread is the gap between the cost of soybeans and the combined selling price of meal and oil [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.3; Q1 FY26 call, prepared remarks].
- **Softseed Processing and Refining** ($3,904m external in Q1 FY26): same structure on canola/rapeseed and sunflower — `Profit ≈ crush volume × softseed crush spread + refining + merchandising margin` [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.4; Q1 FY26 10-Q, Note 19].
- **Grain Merchandising and Milling** ($7,177m external in Q1 FY26): `Profit ≈ merchandised volume × origination/trading margin + milling spread + freight & financial-services fees` [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.5; Q1 FY26 10-Q, Note 19].
- **Tropical Oils and Specialty Ingredients** ($1,228m external in Q1 FY26): `Profit ≈ sales volume × (refined/specialty-product price − input oil cost)` on palm, palm kernel, coconut, shea and soy protein concentrate [FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.4 (renamed from "Other Oilseeds" in Q1 FY26); Q1 FY26 10-Q, Note 19].

Volume is driven by crop size, processing-capacity utilization and origination footprint — all three rose year-over-year largely from the added Viterra capacity in Argentina, Canada and Europe [Q1 FY26 call, prepared remarks]. Spreads are driven by the relative prices of seed, meal and oil, which in turn are heavily influenced by biofuel demand and policy (the US EPA RVO, plus Brazil, Indonesia and EU blending rules) [Q1 FY26 call, prepared remarks]. Margin also swings on mark-to-market timing on hedges, which is non-cash and reverses over time — Q1 FY26 reported EPS of $0.35 carried a $1.28 unfavorable mark-to-market timing difference versus adjusted EPS of $1.83 [Q1 FY26 call, prepared remarks; Q1 FY26 10-Q, MD&A].

## 3. Business Type Classification

Global oilseed-crush and grain-merchandising processor-trader earning thin spreads on very high physical volumes, with profitability gated by crush margins and biofuel policy.

## 4. What Drives Variance

When results move, the cause is almost always spread and volume, not headline commodity price. Crush margins (the seed-versus-meal-and-oil gap) and origination/trading margins set profit per unit; biofuel policy and demand for renewable feedstocks move the oil side of that spread, and Bunge's management cited the EPA RVO decision as a current tailwind [Q1 FY26 call, prepared remarks]. Volume swings with crop size and capacity — the Viterra acquisition lifted both processed and merchandised volumes in Q1 FY26 [Q1 FY26 call, prepared remarks]. Two further swing factors are largely non-operational: mark-to-market hedge timing, which can move reported EPS by more than a dollar in a quarter without changing cash economics, and FX, which produced a $94m net foreign-exchange loss in Q1 FY26 from a stronger US dollar and South American hedging costs [Q1 FY26 10-Q, MD&A; Q1 FY26 call, prepared remarks].
