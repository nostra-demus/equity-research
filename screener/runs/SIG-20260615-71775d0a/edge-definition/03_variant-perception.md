## 1. The Variant

The consensus SOTP (sum-of-the-parts) math values Honeywell Aerospace at roughly 29x EV/EBITDA — the peer median for listed aerospace suppliers — and assumes the stand-alone listing is enough to earn that multiple. But the businesses that trade at 27–30x (TransDigm, HEICO) grow organic revenue at 8–13% per year. Honeywell Aerospace delivered only 3% organic growth in Q1 2026. A company growing at 3% belongs in the mid-range of the aerospace peer set, not at the top. The consensus has applied the right peer roster but the wrong tier of that roster. We believe HONA will settle at 18–20x EV/EBITDA — consistent with lower-growth aerospace suppliers — which implies an equity value roughly 23–31% below the consensus SOTP figure for HONA.

## 3. Mechanisms Missing from Consensus

**1. mechanism_1 — Organic growth tier mismatch in the peer multiple** (attacks consensus assumption #2: "Honeywell Aerospace's 25%+ margins and $37B backlog are durable and will compound to at least $6.5B in earnings by 2030, meaning the pure-play premium is earned rather than gifted by investor enthusiasm.")

Consensus models HONA against the full peer roster for pure-play aerospace — a set that is dominated by high-growth, highly aftermarket-levered names. But multiple compression across the aerospace peer set is tightly linked to organic growth: HEICO's 30x+ multiple reflects 13%+ annual organic growth; TransDigm's premium reflects consistent 8%+ growth and a near-monopoly aftermarket mix. HONA's Q1 2026 organic aerospace revenue grew only 3% year over year. [Honeywell Q1 FY2026 8-K press release, April 24, 2026, SEC EDGAR; EBC Financial Group article, June 2026, web unverified]. A 3%-growth aerospace supplier — still meaningful in scale — belongs in the mid-range of the peer set alongside Curtiss-Wright or Woodward, which trade at 17–21x EV/EBITDA, not at the 29x peer-median that anchors the SOTP. The consensus has modelled the right comparable companies but applied the median of the full distribution rather than the median of the sub-group whose growth matches HONA's actual Q1 delivery. This is a modellable error: if analysts re-weight the peer comparable group to filter out companies growing organic revenue above 6%, the applicable multiple compresses by 8–11 turns.

**2. mechanism_2 — Cash conversion deficit as a re-rating brake** (attacks consensus assumption #2 again, and indirectly assumption #3: "separation costs are manageable and will not permanently impair HONA's margin profile.")

High-multiple aerospace suppliers earn their premium partly because they convert EBIT to free cash flow (FCF = cash from operations minus capital spending) reliably at 85–95%+ per year. FCF conversion is what underpins the "compound value" argument that justifies paying 27–30x. HONA's most recent disclosed performance: Q1 2026 combined HON free cash flow was $56M, down from $191M in Q1 2025, with operating cash flow from continuing operations at negative $650M. [Honeywell Q1 FY2026 8-K press release / financial tables, April 24, 2026, SEC EDGAR; investor.honeywell.com, April 2026]. Management attributed the miss to spin-off separation payments, a Flexjet litigation settlement, and collection timing — which are partly one-off. But a new standalone aerospace company starting its public life with negative operating cash flow in its most recent disclosed quarter cannot immediately claim the FCF-conversion premium the market is applying at 29x. The consensus SOTP uses $4.75B EBITDA as the valuation base but does not adjust the multiple for the demonstrated FCF-conversion shortfall. Even if HONA ultimately achieves 90%+ FCF conversion, it will need two or three quarters of reported standalone data to prove it. Until then, investors who care about cash (as most do for a 29x-multiple business) will pay less, not the consensus median.
