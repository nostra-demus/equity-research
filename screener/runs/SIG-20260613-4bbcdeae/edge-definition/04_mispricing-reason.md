# M0.6.4 Mispricing Reason — SIG-20260613-4bbcdeae

## 1. Primary Category

- **primary_category:** timing

- **primary_category_rationale:** The mechanism driving the variant — Intel's CFO disclosing on the Q1 2026 earnings call (2026-04-23) that the 41% non-GAAP gross margin included ~650 basis points of non-recurring legacy inventory liquidation that "would not repeat in Q2" — is not hidden from the market. It is in the primary source (Intel Q1 2026 8-K, SEC EDGAR, 2026-04-23) and in management's own Q2 gross margin guidance of 39%. The information is public. What stops correct pricing is model revision cadence: sell-side models are rebuilt quarterly, shortly after a print, and then anchored until the next data point forces a re-run. BofA's double-upgrade note (2026-06-11) and Citi's gross margin revision to 42% (2026-05-18) — both published after the Q1 print — absorbed the 41% headline figure as the new structural baseline. Neither available public summary of these notes references a back-out of the non-recurring inventory component. The next forced model revision is Q2 2026 earnings (2026-07-23, approximately 5–6 weeks from the date of this report), when the guided 39% Q2 gross margin will appear in the print and analysts will have to reconcile their 42% full-year estimates against a 39% Q2 actuals. Until that forcing function arrives, the models are locked on a Q1-derived baseline that management already told the market is not repeatable. This is the textbook timing mispricing: information already disclosed, but market models update on a known quarterly lag, leaving a window where the consensus EPS path (anchored to the BofA $6.24 / 2030 destination) reflects a starting gross margin 300–500bps too high. The runner-up category is behavioral: recency bias and narrative momentum (a decade of Intel underperformance reversing, the first clean DC+AI beat in years) explain why analysts did not back out the non-recurring item at the moment it was disclosed. But behavioral does not explain the persistence of the mispricing through mid-June — that persistence is timing. The information was visible on April 23; the models will not be corrected until July 23. The 13-week window is a known, bounded lag, not a permanent narrative failure.

Search: "Intel Q1 2026 earnings call transcript previously reserved inventory gross margin" on SEC EDGAR or TIKR; the CFO's prepared remarks and Q&A contain the quoted language.

2. **evidence_verifiable_fact_2:** Citi analyst Atif Malik raised Intel's full-year 2026 non-GAAP gross margin estimate from 37% to 42% following the Q1 2026 print (report dated approximately 2026-05-18, sourced via 24/7 Wall St., 2026-05-18, and Yahoo Finance). The Q2 2026 gross margin guide from Intel's own Q1 earnings release is 39% — 300 basis points below Citi's revised 42% full-year figure. A full-year estimate of 42% is arithmetically inconsistent with a Q2 guide of 39% unless H2 2026 margin is expected to be 43–45% — a range management never guided to. The inconsistency is visible by comparing the two public numbers side by side, and its persistence through mid-June (seven weeks after both data points were public) reflects the timing lag. — *verify via:* (a) Citi gross margin revision: 24/7 Wall St., "Intel Stock Can Rally 60%: Citi", 2026-05-18, unverified secondary (retrieved 2026-06-14); (b) Intel Q2 2026 gross margin guidance of 39%: Intel Q1 2026 8-K / earnings release, SEC EDGAR, 2026-04-23 (the Q2 gross margin outlook is in the company guidance table of the press release attached as Exhibit 99.1).

3. **evidence_verifiable_fact_3:** BofA issued its Intel double-upgrade and $135 price target (based on 25x a $6.24 2030 EPS destination) on 2026-06-11 — 49 days after Intel's Q1 2026 earnings release and Q2 gross margin guide-down. Available public summaries of the BofA note (Yahoo Finance, TheStreet, Benzinga, GuruFocus — all retrieved 2026-06-14) describe the 2030 EPS path without any reference to an adjustment for the non-recurring Q1 inventory benefit. The 49-day gap between the primary disclosure (2026-04-23) and the BofA model publication (2026-06-11) is itself evidence that model-update timing, not information availability, is the binding constraint. The next scheduled forcing function is Intel Q2 2026 earnings (2026-07-23 per Barchart.com, retrieved 2026-06-14), which is the first print where the guided 39% Q2 gross margin must appear as an actual result. — *verify via:* (a) BofA upgrade date and price target: Yahoo Finance article, "BofA Upgrades Intel to Buy", published 2026-06-11 (unverified secondary, retrieved 2026-06-14); (b) Q2 2026 earnings date of 2026-07-23: Barchart.com Intel earnings calendar (retrieved 2026-06-14); (c) Absence of non-recurring inventory adjustment in public BofA summaries: search "Intel BofA June 2026 upgrade $135 target $6.24 EPS model gross margin inventory non-recurring" on Google — no article in the top results references an inventory back-out (search logged in M0.6.3 coverage-gap evidence, 2026-06-14).

## 3. Secondary Categories

| Category | Rationale |
|---|---|
| behavioral | Recency bias and narrative momentum are a genuine second force. Intel entered 2026 priced near $30 on consensus expectations of near-zero earnings — a decade of underperformance. The Q1 2026 beat ($0.29 EPS vs $0.01 consensus, a near-3,000% beat) and the DC+AI revenue inflection ($5.1B, +22% YoY) triggered a regime-change narrative that made the 41% gross margin headline a symbol of structural recovery rather than a quarterly data point to be scrutinised line by line. Anchoring to the 41% headline, combined with the salience of the multi-year bull narrative now appearing to confirm, reduced the probability that any individual analyst stopped at the gross margin line item and asked "how much of this is the inventory one-off?" The behavioral layer explains why the non-recurring item was not backed out at the moment of disclosure (April 23); the timing layer explains why the mispricing has not been corrected in the seven weeks since. |
| complexity | A secondary complexity contribution exists: correctly adjusting the gross margin baseline requires reading the CFO commentary in the earnings call transcript, cross-referencing the Q2 guidance table in the 8-K (Exhibit 99.1), performing the arithmetic to isolate the non-recurring component (~650bps), and then propagating that correction through a multi-year EPS model all the way to a 2030 destination figure — a four-step, cross-document work chain. This is not unusually hard for a seasoned semiconductor analyst, but it is work that requires doing the transcript-to-model linkage rather than updating estimates from a published summary. The complexity penalty is small compared to the timing lag, which applies even to an analyst who did do the work — they still cannot force a market re-rating until Q2 confirms it. |
