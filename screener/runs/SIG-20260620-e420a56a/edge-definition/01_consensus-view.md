## 2. Consensus View (sympathetic)

The dominant market view holds that the Jio Platforms DRHP marks a structural positive for India's capital markets ecosystem and a clarifying moment for the telecom and technology sector. Here is that view in its strongest form.

**On Jio Platforms itself and its blast-radius valuation.** Analysts across investment banks place Jio's enterprise value at $131–180 billion — a range that reflects not a telecom company but a vertically integrated digital platform. Elara Capital, for instance, uses 13x FY28E EV/EBITDA (projecting 11% revenue CAGR and 14% EBITDA CAGR over FY26–29E) to arrive at a Rs 12–13 lakh crore valuation. Citi places the standalone enterprise value at $135 billion. The consensus view is that Jio deserves a premium above pure-play global telecom peers (which trade at 6–9x EV/EBITDA) because it is better described as a technology and AI platform: 268.5 million 5G users already, the world's largest VoLTE network outside China, a digital services layer (MyJio at 215.9 million monthly active users), an AI-infrastructure build-out under the "Reliance Intelligence" brand, and an EBITDA margin of 51.9% that global telcos cannot match. The bull case at $170–180 billion implies roughly 17x FY26 EV/EBITDA — expensive relative to telecom but considered reasonable by supporters as an entry price for a platform that will monetise data, enterprise cloud, and AI services over the next five years. [Business Standard, 22 Jun 2026, unverified; CMA Knowledge / Elara Capital cite, Jun 2026, unverified; Capital.com / Citi cite, Jun 2026, unverified]

**On Capital Markets & Investment Banking (DIR-001).** The consensus expects the Jio IPO to be the single largest fee event for India's investment banking industry in 2026. India's ECM underwriting fees rose 39% year-on-year to $84.3 million in Q1 2026 — the highest Q1 since 2018 — even before the Jio mandate was contracted. Goldman Sachs and Kotak Mahindra Capital have both forecast full-year 2026 IPO proceeds of as much as $25 billion (roughly 14% above 2025). The book-running lead managers, legal advisors, registrars, and rating agencies engaged on the ~Rs 37,700 crore issue stand to earn an estimated Rs 565–940 crore in total fees (at the typical 1.5–2.5% fee range for large Indian IPOs), making this the largest single-transaction fee event in recent Indian capital-markets history. The view is that Kotak Mahindra Capital, Axis Capital, ICICI Securities, and their co-managers will each capture material wallet share. [Whalesbook / LSEG data, Q1 2026, unverified; Business Standard, 2 Apr 2026, unverified; JPMorgan APAC ECM Outlook, 2026, unverified]

**On Equity Retail Brokerage & Wealth Management (DIR-002).** The consensus holds that a marquee IPO of this scale — the potential largest listing in Indian capital-markets history — will trigger another wave of demat account openings and peak the already-elevated subscription-brokerage revenue run-rate. India already had more than 21 crore (210 million) demat accounts by late 2025, with retail investors fuelling consecutive IPO subscription cycles. Zerodha and Angel One each serve roughly 67–69 million active clients (as of Feb 2026). The consensus expectation is that the Jio subscription window — likely a three-day open period — will generate platform-record ASBA/UPI application volumes, driving brokerage commissions, distribution fees, and new account openings. Wealth managers expect strong HNI and retail demand given Jio's consumer brand recognition. [Whalesbook demat data, 2025, unverified; SEBI / DD News, Oct 2025, unverified; Groww / Angel One broker data, Feb 2026, unverified]

**On PE & Unlisted Indian Tech/Telecom Funds (HARM-002).** The consensus view in the PE/VC community is that a Rs 37,700 crore IPO absorbs a significant share of the domestic institutional and retail capital pool for its subscription window, crowding out capital available for concurrent private placements and pre-IPO rounds. EY-IVCA data shows India PE/VC investments were subdued at $3.7 billion across 109 deals in February 2026 — already operating in a tighter capital environment. Commentary from EY and others notes that "large issues like Reliance Jio could absorb a significant share of investor liquidity and affect how much capital flows into other primary-market deals." The consensus view is that the crowding-out is temporary (the subscription window typically runs three days and then locked capital is returned or deployed), but the timing effect on competing pre-IPO rounds and secondary fundraises is real. [EY-IVCA Monthly Report, Feb 2026, unverified; Entrepreneur India / IPO Momentum piece, 2026, unverified; The Unlisted Intel, 2026, unverified]

**On Telecom Infrastructure & Tower Operators (IND-001).** The consensus view is constructive. Analysts expect Indus Towers to grow revenue, EBITDA, and profit after tax at CAGRs of 8.2%, 8.9%, and 11.1% respectively over FY26–FY28, driven by 5G densification spending from Jio and Airtel. Jio's IPO-enabled balance-sheet improvement (net debt already down 39.1% to Rs 27,579 crore as of March 2026) further strengthens the thesis. The industry has deployed approximately 5.2 lakh 5G base stations to date; Jio and Airtel together are planning 2+ lakh new 5G sites by 2028. Analyst consensus puts Indus Towers at Rs 390–450 in 12-month targets, implying 20–38% upside from the Rs 325 level as of recent trading. [Indian Infrastructure / Indus Towers mgmt commentary, Feb 2026, unverified; Univest analyst consensus, 2026, unverified; TradeBrains 5G build-out data, 2026, unverified]

**On Incumbent Indian Telecom Operators (HARM-001).** The consensus view is that the duopoly (Jio + Airtel) is now locked in and Vodafone Idea is structurally impaired — but that Airtel is positioned well enough to withstand Jio's IPO-amplified competitive firepower through ARPU leadership. Airtel's FY26 monthly ARPU of Rs 257 compares with Jio's Rs 214; Airtel holds roughly 38% wireless revenue market share (AGR basis) vs Jio's 43%. The consensus expects continued ARPU improvement for both leading operators as Vi continues to bleed subscribers. The incremental harm from the Jio IPO is seen as reinforcing an already-known competitive gap rather than creating a new one — Airtel's analyst consensus sits at a Rs 1,900 12-month target (25+ buy ratings, Motilal Oswal and CLSA at Rs 2,000–2,100), anchored to ARPU reaching Rs 280 by FY27. [S&P Global Market Intelligence, Aug 2024 (background), unverified; Business Standard telecom piece, 21 Jun 2026, unverified; Univest / Bharti Airtel analyst consensus, 2026, unverified]

- **numeric_anchor:** Jio Platforms consensus enterprise valuation range $131–180 billion, with Elara Capital at 13x FY28E EV/EBITDA (Rs 12–13 lakh crore); implied total IPO fee pool Rs 565–940 crore for capital-markets intermediaries; Indus Towers analyst consensus 12-month target Rs 390–450; Airtel analyst consensus 12-month target Rs 1,900 (25+ buys).

- **key_consensus_assumptions:**
  1. Jio Platforms deserves a technology/platform EV multiple (13–17x EV/EBITDA) materially above the 6–9x range at which global pure-play telecom peers trade, because its digital services, 5G user base, and AI infrastructure warrant reclassification away from a traditional carrier.
  2. The Jio IPO will proceed to listing within the 3–6 month post-DRHP window (Sep–Nov 2026) without a material SEBI objection or management withdrawal, locking in investment banking fee revenues and brokerage subscription volumes within the current thesis horizon.
  3. Jio's financial strength (51.9% EBITDA margin, accelerating net-debt reduction) will flow through to sustained 5G densification capex, benefiting tower operators through rising tenancy ratios and co-location volumes — not be redirected to AI infrastructure or spectrum acquisition instead.
  4. The capital crowding-out effect on PE/unlisted tech funds is temporary and bounded to the IPO subscription window, so it does not represent a structural shift in private-capital availability.

## 3. Contrary View

- **exists:** true

**Who:** BofA Securities (referenced in multiple analyst summaries post-DRHP filing, Jun 2026). Secondary: independent commentary from Hariprasad K., founder of Livelong Wealth.

**Evidence and argument:** BofA Securities warns of a holding company discount on RIL's stake in Jio — the structure means existing RIL shareholders do not gain direct exposure to the newly listed Jio entity, and the absence of a demerger plan means the Jio value sits behind a parent-level conglomerate discount. BNP Paribas takes a similar position, applying a 10% holding company discount in its RIL valuation. The market reflected this skepticism immediately: RIL's stock fell approximately 2.21% on the day Mukesh Ambani announced the IPO plan at the AGM (19 Jun 2026). The bear case on valuation contends that at $170–180 billion Jio would be priced at roughly 17x FY26 EV/EBITDA — "priced to perfection" with no margin for AI monetisation disappointment or ARPU growth shortfall — and that the absence of a free-float demerger limits the re-rating catalyst for existing RIL holders. On the tower and telecom side, the contrary view notes that 5G capital expenditure could be redirected to AI data-centre infrastructure rather than passive tower densification, removing the mechanism that underpins the tower-operator bull case. [Capital.com / BofA cite, Jun 2026, unverified; ScanX / Livelong Wealth cite, Jun 2026, unverified; Business Standard, 22 Jun 2026, unverified]

**Their numeric anchor:** BNP Paribas: 10% holding company discount applied to RIL's Jio stake. Bear-case IPO valuation: ~$130–133 billion (the low end of the analyst range, implying ~13x FY26 EV/EBITDA rather than the 17x bull case). [Capital.com / multiple secondary sources, Jun 2026, unverified]

## 4. Consensus Strength

- **unanimity_rating:** moderate — The core view that the Jio IPO is a net positive for Indian capital markets and that Jio deserves a premium above traditional telecom multiples is shared by a clear majority (Goldman Sachs, Jefferies, Citi, Elara Capital all in the $131–180 billion range), but BofA Securities, BNP Paribas, and a documented group of RIL shareholders are on record with a contrary position centred on the holding-company structure and valuation ceiling, preventing a "high" rating.

- **entrenchment_note:** The consensus is moderately entrenched for two structural reasons. First, Jio's 524.4 million subscriber base, 51.9% EBITDA margin, and the "platform premium" narrative have been in the analyst community for years (Meta, Alphabet, KKR, and others invested at private valuations above $50 billion in the 2020 funding rounds, anchoring expectations). Second, once a DRHP is filed and the SEBI review clock is running, institutional investors begin reserving allocations and investment banks begin accruing fee work — sunk institutional momentum makes a consensus reversal expensive before the observations letter arrives.
