## 2. Consensus View (sympathetic)

The dominant market view entering June 2026 is that the Qatar force majeure is a severe but ultimately time-bounded supply shock layered on top of a structural LNG oversupply cycle. Before Iran struck Ras Laffan in early March 2026, the standing consensus — held by Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, Drewry, and most LNG advisory shops — was that the global LNG market would tip into a modest surplus of roughly 6 million tonnes in 2026, driven by a wave of new US Gulf Coast liquefaction capacity coming online. That glut narrative was the organising principle of market positioning across all three industries. The Qatar disruption materially disrupted that narrative in the near term: Morgan Stanley acknowledged in a March 8, 2026 note that the outage "erases the 2026 supply surplus" and that any extension beyond one month "quickly brings a deficit" [Bloomberg, "Qatar LNG Outage Erases 2026 Supply Surplus, Morgan Stanley Says", 2026-03-09]. Goldman Sachs raised its first-half 2026 TTF forecast to €55/MWh from €36/MWh in the immediate aftermath [Investing.com, citing Goldman Sachs note, 2026-03 date]. Yet the consensus view — even after incorporating these revisions — is that the Qatar disruption accelerates a near-term price spike without fundamentally altering the medium-to-long-term surplus trajectory: LNG fleet deliveries of ~94 vessels are scheduled for 2026 [Drewry, Riviera Maritime, 2025-late/2026 reporting; unverified web, labelled], Qatar's surviving 12 trains retain export capacity, and US capacity additions of ~93 bcm through 2027 remain on track [ING Think, "Oil, gas and CO2 see an increasingly comfortable supply outlook", undated 2026; unverified web, labelled]. In LNG shipping, the pre-disruption consensus was explicitly bearish: Drewry described 2026 as "LNG Fleet > LNG Supply > LNG Demand" and expected only a modest rate recovery in the second half of 2026 [Drewry, "LNG shipping on a road to recovery, but 2026 marks just the beginning", Manifold Times, 2025-12; unverified web, labelled]. On European gas storage, the EU's own Gas Coordination Group confirmed in April 2026 that EU infrastructure can refill to ≥80% by November 1, 2026, provided LNG imports remain strong [EU Gas Coordination Group press release, 2026-04-09; unverified web, labelled; and ENTSOG Summer Supply Outlook 2026, 2026-04-09]. The consensus therefore holds that the price spike and shipping rate surge are a windfall for current-moment operators, but the underlying market dynamic — too many ships, too much capacity coming, prices trending lower — reasserts itself once the force majeure window closes.

- **numeric_anchor:** Morgan Stanley pre-disruption consensus for 2026 TTF: ~EUR 36/MWh ($~10–11/MMBtu); Goldman Sachs revised Q2 2026 average TTF forecast to EUR 45/MWh [$~13.4/MMBtu] and Q4 2026 at EUR 55/MWh [$~16.4/MMBtu] amid disruption; Goldman Sachs medium-term view: TTF falls below $10/MMBtu by Q4 2026 absent sustained disruption [Bloomberg/Investing.com, Goldman Sachs, 2026-03]. LNG shipping consensus before Qatar: FLEX LNG (FLNG) median analyst price target $25.50 with 100% Hold (2 analysts); Golar LNG (GLNG) median target $53.00, 7 Buy / 1 Hold / 0 Sell (8 analysts) [public.com / chartmill.com, retrieved 2026-06-12; unverified web, labelled].

- **key_consensus_assumptions:**
  1. The Qatar force majeure is temporary and bounded by the mid-August 2026 declared end date — the 12.8 mtpa shortfall does not represent a permanent structural loss of supply capacity; repair or restoration of Trains 4 and 6 is achievable within months and priced in by the market as a near-term aberration.
  2. The 2026 LNG fleet oversupply (94+ vessel deliveries, 42% orderbook-to-fleet ratio) is structurally intact and will reassert itself as the primary driver of shipping rates once the Qatar-related tonnage dislocation normalises; the $300,000/day spot rate spike is not a new equilibrium.
  3. European gas storage can reach the EU 80% target by November 1, 2026 if LNG import flows are sustained at high levels through the injection season — the 28% starting-point deficit is a refill challenge, not a supply-security crisis, because alternative LNG supply is available from the US and Australia to compensate for the Qatar volume.
  4. The medium-to-long-term LNG price trajectory (TTF below $10/MMBtu, JKM converging) is determined by the 50%+ global LNG export capacity expansion in 2025–2030, not by a single producer's multi-month outage; the disruption reprices near-term contracts but does not reset long-term price curves.

## 3. Contrary View

- **exists:** true

**Who:** IEA (International Energy Agency), Wood Mackenzie (Simon Flowers), and East Daley Analytics hold a documented minority view that the Qatar disruption is not merely a temporary aberration.

**Evidence and numbers:**

- The IEA's Gas Market Report Q2-2026 (published May 2026) states the Qatar facility damage "could reduce the country's LNG output by nearly 70 bcm by 2030, assuming a repair period of four years," and that the disruption "could result in a cumulative loss of around 120 billion cubic metres of LNG supply between 2026 and 2030, prolonging tight market conditions through 2026 and 2027" [IEA, "Middle East crisis disrupts international natural gas markets and delays global LNG supply wave", 2026-05; IEA Q2-2026 Gas Market Report summary; unverified web summary, labelled]. The IEA's contrary numeric anchor is that tight market conditions persist to **2030** — not to mid-August 2026.

- Wood Mackenzie's Simon Flowers (Energy Connects, June 2026): "No Gulf exports beyond four or five months will mean annual LNG supply falls, upward pressure on prices through 2026 and demand destruction, particularly in Asia." The Wood Mackenzie estimate is that "more than 80 million metric tons per annum of LNG supply has been affected by recent disruptions" [Energy Connects, "The market outlook for gas and LNG in Asia", 2026-06; unverified web, labelled].

- East Daley Analytics (Daley Note, undated March-April 2026): TTF surged 70% to $15.90/MMBtu and JKM jumped 96% in early March; the note argues US liquefaction operators (Venture Global, Cheniere) are structural winners as they hold uncommitted volumes that now command "much healthier" margins — implying the disruption is not being fully priced into beneficiary equities [East Daley, "What LNG Glut? Qatari Outage Puts US Projects in the Driver's Seat", 2026; unverified web, labelled].

- Goldman Sachs itself is the partial contrary voice within the consensus camp: even while revising its near-term TTF higher, it maintains a bearish 2027–2028 outlook ("prices falling to levels needed to curb marginal LNG supply") — meaning the Goldman view is that the disruption is fully temporary from a structural standpoint, but the IEA/Wood Mackenzie view is that it is at least medium-term structural [Goldman Sachs Commodities Outlook 2026, goldmansachs.com; unverified web, labelled].

**Contrary numeric anchor:** IEA estimates 120 bcm cumulative LNG supply loss 2026–2030, with tight conditions persisting to 2030 (versus the consensus assumption of normalisation by mid-August 2026 to end-2026).

## 4. Consensus Strength

- **unanimity_rating:** moderate — The market consensus that the Qatar disruption is temporary and the 2026 surplus thesis remains structurally intact holds among the major investment banks (Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs) and shipping analytics houses (Drewry) as of early-to-mid 2026. However, the IEA (a primary official source), Wood Mackenzie, and East Daley are documented dissenters who believe the disruption has medium-to-long-term structural consequences. The IEA's contrary view is from an official multilateral energy body, not a fringe opinion, and it carries meaningful weight with European policy-driven buyers.

- **entrenchment_note:** The pre-disruption surplus narrative is structurally entrenched because: (a) Drewry's fleet-oversupply data (94+ 2026 vessel deliveries) is objectively verifiable and does not depend on Qatar's output timeline; (b) most LNG shipping equity analysts had long-term charter models built on a 2026 rate floor assumption that predated the Qatar event; and (c) European gas buyers and utilities have historically treated LNG spot disruptions as transient and continued to structure procurement around pipeline and long-term LNG contracts. Index-level flows into energy infrastructure ETFs are indifferent to this specific disruption and rebalance mechanically, damping the signal.
