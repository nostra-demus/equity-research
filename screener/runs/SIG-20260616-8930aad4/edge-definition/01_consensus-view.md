## 2. Consensus View (sympathetic)

The market's dominant view is that the $70 million exceptional charge TCS must book in Q1 FY27 is a one-time, non-recurring event that will depress the headline reported net profit for a single quarter but leaves the operating earnings story intact. Analysts have known since at least the Fifth Circuit's January 2026 ruling that the $168 million award was likely to stand; TCS had already set aside $150 million, so the remaining $70 million gap — covering incremental damages, prejudgment interest, and legal costs — was not a surprise in direction, only in size and timing. The market's reaction confirms this read: TCS shares closed up 1.57% on BSE on the day of the disclosure (June 16, 2026, closing at Rs 2,196 vs prior close of Rs 2,162), treating certainty of closure as better than an open liability [Scanx Trade citing BSE data, 2026-06-16, unverified web]. Analysts who follow TCS value the company on a forward price-to-earnings multiple applied to operating (excluding exceptional items) earnings power; this charge sits below the operating line and will be stripped out of every normalized earnings model. With TCS generating roughly Rs 48,000 crore in free cash flow for FY26 [Inference from CEO reporter.com, 2026-06-16, unverified web], the $70 million cash outflow is roughly 1% of annual free cash flow — a rounding error on the balance sheet. The consensus FY27 revenue growth forecast of approximately 8.5% and profit growth of approximately 14.7% (both from 43-analyst Trendlyne/Investing.com consensus, 2026-06-16) are driven by AI-led deal wins, a $40.7 billion TCV in FY26, and North America recovery — none of which the litigation charge touches. The consensus 12-month price target of roughly Rs 2,940 (average of 43 analysts, Investing.com UK, 2026-06-16) implies the market continues to look through the Q1 headline miss to normalized earnings growth.

- **numeric_anchor:** Consensus 12-month price target Rs 2,940 per share (average of 43 analysts, Investing.com UK, retrieved 2026-06-16); FY27 consensus profit growth ~14.7% and revenue growth ~8.5% (Trendlyne, 43 analysts, retrieved 2026-06-16) — both on a pre-exceptional operating basis.

- **key_consensus_assumptions:**
  1. The $70 million charge is purely a one-time item — it will be stripped from every normalized earnings model and will not flow into forward P/E or price-target math for FY27.
  2. TCS's operating earnings trajectory (FY27 profit growth ~14.7%) is driven by AI-led demand and large deal conversion, and the DXC litigation result has no bearing on any of these growth drivers.
  3. The $150 million already provisioned since 2024 means the additional $70 million represents the tail end of a known risk; the market's +1.57% day-of reaction confirms the legal closure removed more uncertainty than the incremental charge added.
  4. TCS's balance sheet and free cash flow position (Rs 48,000 crore FCF in FY26, Inference) absorb a ~Rs 665 crore one-time cash payment without any impact on dividends, buybacks, or capital allocation, so no secondary earnings effect is expected.

## 4. Consensus Strength

- **unanimity_rating:** high — The day-of stock move (+1.57% on the disclosure) is the single most direct piece of evidence: the market collectively moved the other way from what a "new bad news" reaction would produce, confirming near-universal buy-side reading of the charge as non-recurring. No named dissenting analyst was found across all sources checked. The 43-analyst consensus from Trendlyne and Investing.com (67% Buy, 21% Hold, 12% Sell across 43 analysts) pre-dates the June 16 event but shows a strongly positive pre-existing orientation that is unlikely to be disrupted by a well-flagged exceptional item.

- **entrenchment_note:** TCS is the largest company in the Nifty 50 and Nifty IT by weight; passive funds tracking both indices must hold it at index weight regardless of the litigation outcome. Analyst coverage is heavily concentrated on buy-side IT generalists for whom a one-time charge below the operating line is a standard exclusion from their models — the construct of "normalized EPS" is so embedded in how the sell-side covers Indian IT that any exceptional item is almost automatically stripped. These two structural facts make the "strip and ignore" consensus view very hard to dislodge.
