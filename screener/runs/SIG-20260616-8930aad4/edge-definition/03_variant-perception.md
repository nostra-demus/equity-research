## 3. Mechanisms Missing from Consensus

1. **mechanism_1 — conduct-finding vendor-qualification risk:** The Fifth Circuit affirmed (January 14, 2026) that TCS acted with "willful and malicious" conduct and "through repeated deceit" — a specific legal characterization that goes beyond a commercial dispute settlement. Attacks consensus assumption #2: "the DXC litigation result has no bearing on any of TCS's growth drivers." In competitive RFPs for regulated US industries (BFSI, insurance, federal IT), procurement teams increasingly run vendor-integrity screens; a published "malicious misappropriation" finding is a different input to those screens than a settled or uncontested liability. Sell-side models assume zero contract-win impact. The mechanism is real but operates on a 6–18 month lag — contract cycles are long, and the finding has been public since January 2026 with no confirmed client loss attributable to it (the Transamerica termination was June 2023, predating the appeals court ruling). **This mechanism does not resolve within the Q1 FY27 horizon (July 9, 2026).** It is a valid medium-to-long-term risk that consensus has not modelled, but it is the wrong mechanism for this thesis's time window.

2. **mechanism_2 — FX and share-count precision on charge translation:** The consensus price-impact estimate of Rs 55–70 (from M0.6.2) uses an implicit FX rate and an unspecified share count. Our carry-through at Rs 83.5/USD and ~9,430M shares gives Rs ~86 — a modest upward departure. This is a mechanical difference, not a genuine variant perception insight. It does not attack any consensus assumption; it is an arithmetic refinement that a single analyst revision would close.
