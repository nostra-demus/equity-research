# Unit Economics — HCG

*HealthCare Global Enterprises Limited (NSE: HCG, BSE: 539787). Figures INR (₹) unless stated. ARPP = Average Revenue Per Patient (per in-patient, IP). Primary per-unit data is from the Q4/FY26 investor deck (May 19 2026) and the May 20 2026 earnings call; FY25 baseline cross-checks from the FY24-25 Integrated Annual Report. The FY26 annual report is not in the data pool, so FY26 per-unit figures are deck/management figures, not audited.*

Upstream segment-map was present and used. Per segment-map, HCG is effectively a **single-segment oncology hospital business** (~93–98% of revenue/EBITDA), and the value-driving split is **centre maturity**, not a product line. That directs the unit choice below.

---

## 1. Natural Unit

**The natural economic unit for HCG's oncology hospital network is the cancer centre (hospital), measured on a per-month revenue / maturity-cohort basis; the underlying revenue driver beneath the centre is the in-patient treated (priced as ARPP).**

A centre is the unit HCG actually builds, capitalises, and ramps, and the unit at which it discloses EBITDA margin and ROCE. Two finer units sit inside it and are also disclosed: the **bed** (the capacity HCG buys — ~₹45 lakh to build) and the **in-patient (ARPP × volume)** (the revenue HCG earns). I build the table primarily at the **centre** level because that is where value is created or destroyed, and add the bed and per-patient lines because the cost-to-build and revenue-per-unit are only meaningful there. Secondary unit types not built here: the International (Kenya) operation and the Milann fertility unit (being divested) — both immaterial (<3% of revenue each).

## 3. Value Creation Read

**Creates value — but only above the maturity threshold; a new centre destroys capital for years before it does.** The cleanest evidence is the cohort ROCE ladder: a mature centre earns **27% pre-tax ROCE** and a ramping centre **9%**, while an emerging centre runs at **−22% ROCE** — i.e., a freshly built centre destroys capital until it scales [Q4/FY26 Investor Deck, May 2026, "Path to Improve Profitability and Return on Capital"]. So the unit creates value *conditional on reaching maturity*, and the whole investment case is whether the 14 ramping centres (50% of revenue, 9% ROCE) climb into the >₹100Mn/27% bucket — which management says is already happening (centres migrated up buckets in FY26, and group ROCE rose 13%→14%) [same deck; May 20 2026 call]. At the network level the math is positive and improving: 14% blended ROCE on a 19% centre-EBITDA-margin base, with ARPP +3% and volume +12%.

The single most valuable missing disclosure is a **per-centre payback / years-to-mature curve** (or a vintage/cohort-by-opening-year table). With cost-to-build (~₹45 lakh/bed brownfield) disclosed but no time-to-breakeven and no contribution margin × lifetime, the formal "contribution × lifetime > acquisition cost" test cannot be closed from disclosure — the read rests on the cohort ROCE snapshot plus management's qualitative ramp claim, not on a computed payback.

---

## 4. Sensitivity

The input that would most change the read is **utilisation / ramp speed of the ₹50–100Mn cohort** — equivalently, the time a new centre spends in the value-destroying (<₹50Mn, −22% ROCE) phase. A 20% move in utilisation flows almost directly to contribution margin because the cost base of a built-out hospital is largely fixed (beds, LINACs, clinicians, and — post-IndAS — rent); the cohort table itself shows the leverage, with EBITDA margin jumping from 5% (emerging) to 18% (ramping) to 26% (mature) as throughput rises, far outpacing the ~3% annual ARPP move. ARPP is the more *stable* lever (only +2–3% YoY, and East's ARPP actually fell ~1% to ₹65,759 on Kolkata case-mix), so a 20% ARPP swing is historically unlikely; a 20% utilisation swing is both more achievable (West sits at 50% vs South's 68%, on the company's own blended metric) and far more consequential. Acquisition cost per bed (~₹45 lakh) is the least volatile input. Net: ramp/utilisation of the ramping cohort is the swing factor that decides whether the unit math compounds or stalls.
