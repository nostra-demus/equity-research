# Business Model Reality Check — HCG (Synthesis)

*HealthCare Global Enterprises Limited (NSE: HCG / BSE: 539787). India-domiciled comprehensive cancer-care (oncology) hospital network. Reporting currency INR; FY ends 31 March. Synthesis date: 2026-06-01. Built from the 13 upstream business-model specialist outputs only; raw data pool not re-read. Most recent audited base is the FY24-25 Integrated Annual Report; FY26 (year ended 31 Mar 2026) is covered by the 19-20 May 2026 deck and earnings call only — the FY26 audited annual report is not in the pool.*

## Abstract

HCG operates a chain of comprehensive cancer hospitals in India — 25 centres plus one in Kenya — selling diagnosis and the three oncology treatment modalities to patients, paid by cash, insurance/TPAs, and government schemes. It is effectively a single-segment business: oncology is ~97.6% of FY26 revenue, monetised through bed and equipment utilisation at each centre. The strongest positive is structurally rising, non-deferrable cancer demand: revenue compounded ~20% a year to ₹25,454m in FY26 at a stable 18.5% adjusted EBITDA margin. The strongest negative is that this demand has not yet earned its cost of capital — group Return on Capital sits at ~4.6%, the lowest EBIT margin in its listed peer set. Verdict: an average, capital-hungry business worth deeper work only if valuation is cheap; no disqualifier triggered.

## 3. Reconciliation

The specialists are largely consistent; HCG is a well-understood single-segment business and the reads converge. Items reconciled:

- **Revenue type vs deck "excluding fertility" presentation.** `segment-map` notes the FY26 deck reports FY25 revenue as ₹22,228.5m while audited Note 22 shows ₹22,181.36m — the ₹47.14m difference is a government-grant presentation item, not a discrepancy. **Reconciled view:** treat the audited Note 22 figure as authoritative and the deck figure as a presentation variant; both agents agree on substance.

- **Gross margin level.** `moat` shows HCG FY26 gross margin at 36.4% (Capital IQ basis) vs 53.6% FY25, and explicitly attributes the drop to a COGS reclassification (COGS +₹5,881m offset by "other operating expense" −₹2,591m), not a margin collapse — operating income still rose and EBITDA margin improved to 18.3%. Other agents (`business-quality`, `segment-map`) anchor on the more stable EBITDA-margin series (~14% FY22-25 → ~18.3-18.5% FY26). **Reconciled view:** the gross-margin line is partly an accounting-classification artifact and should not be read as deterioration; the EBIT-margin and ROC gaps versus peers are real and are the binding negative.

- **ROCE / ROC — two bases on the same company.** The company's deck shows "ROCE" of 14% (FY26, pre-Ind AS, post corporate cost, excl. fertility), while Capital IQ shows Return on Capital of ~4.6%. These are not contradictory — they are different definitions (pre-Ind AS centre/operating basis vs full statutory EBIT-on-total-capital). `business-quality` and `moat` both surface both. **Reconciled view (conservative default per CLAUDE.md §4):** for the cost-of-capital test, the statutory Capital IQ basis (~4.6%, below any plausible India cost of capital) governs the "does not earn its cost of capital" conclusion; the 14% deck figure is a management operating metric, useful for the maturity-migration story but not for the returns verdict.

- **Capital-allocation severity of the minority puts.** `capital-allocation-governance` recognised the Vizag put/forward as on-balance-sheet and benign (off-balance-sheet row severity 35), whereas `red-flags-sweep` re-sized the aggregate at ~₹3,318m with a self-disclosed bank-funding dependency management "cannot assure" (severity 55). **Reconciled view (conservative default):** adopt the red-flags-sweep reading — the obligation is larger and harder-committed than the capital-allocation row implied, and is carried forward as a material negative in Section 4.

No other material disagreements between specialists.

## 4. Note To The Final Synthesizer

- **Strongest business-model positive:** demand quality. Cancer care is non-deferrable and structurally rising (India incidence projected to ~1.77m cases by 2030); revenue compounded ~20% p.a. FY21→FY26 straight through COVID, with operating EBITDA margin stable at ~14% then lifting to ~18.5%. This is the rare healthcare niche where a downturn does not defer the demand — the top line is dependable in a way the returns are not.

- **Strongest business-model negative:** the demand has not converted into returns. Statutory Return on Capital has sat at ~4.5-5.0% for years (FY26 ~4.6%), below a ~12-13% India cost of capital, and HCG posts the lowest EBIT margin (8.7%) and net margin (0.5%) in its listed peer set while carrying the highest leverage (Total Debt/Capital 55.2% vs 28.5% median). The whole investment question is whether the 14 ramping centres (50% of revenue, 9% ROCE) climb into the mature, ~27%-ROCE bucket — that migration, not pricing, is the value lever.

- **Most important segment:** there is only one — India comprehensive oncology (~97.6% of revenue, ~98.5% of adjusted EBITDA). The economically meaningful split is centre maturity, not a product line. Milann fertility (~2.4%) is being divested, making the business cleaner, not more diversified.

- **Cleanest unit-economics read:** the natural unit is the cancer centre, on a per-month-revenue / maturity-cohort basis. The clean signal is the cohort ROCE ladder (mature 27% / ramping 9% / emerging −22%) — a centre creates value only after it scales and destroys capital for years before it does. What *cannot* be derived: a per-centre payback / years-to-mature curve is not disclosed, so the formal "contribution × lifetime > build cost" test cannot be closed — the read rests on the cohort snapshot plus management's qualitative ramp claim.

- **Where it sits vs named peers on margin / ROIC:** at the bottom. Versus the listed set (Max, Fortis, Narayana, Aster, Medanta), HCG has the lowest EBIT margin (8.7% vs 16.8% median), lowest net margin (0.5% vs 12.0%), highest leverage, and the weakest Credit Health operational and solvency rank (4 of 4). Its two truest oncology rivals — Apollo's oncology vertical and private AOI — do not disclose stand-alone oncology margins, so the "bottom of peers" verdict is anchored on the listed multi-specialty set, not a pure-oncology comparison.

- **Main external dependency:** government healthcare pricing policy — DPCO drug price control and margin capping — which management names first among its risks and which caps pricing on roughly a third of revenue (government-scheme payers) with no available hedge. Interest-rate sensitivity is the largest *quantified* market risk (+1% = −₹67.92m PBT) but is partly managed; FX and commodity exposure are immaterial.

- **Most important capital allocation / governance signal:** control and leadership instability. The controlling shareholder changed twice in five years (Founder → CVC 2020 → KKR May 2025) and almost the entire C-suite/board turned over in the same window (new KKR-nominee CEO from 30 Jun 2025; third CFO in ~3 years). Every step is disclosed and SEBI-LODR-compliant — this is orderly PE transition, not concealment — but the people and owner steering capital allocation are unproven in their current configuration, so any multi-year track record is reset.

- **Carry-forward red flags (severity ≥ 40, per mandatory propagation):**
  - **Minority buy-out funding obligation (severity 55):** ~₹3,318m of written puts (₹1,778m) + forward-purchase liability (₹1,540m) to acquire non-controlling stakes, of which ₹1,401.34m is already *current*, against ~₹3.2bn annual operating cash flow and ~₹9.1bn gross debt. Management's own risk factor states it depends on banks to fund these and "cannot assure" the funding arrives on planned timelines. This competes with expansion capex for the same constrained cash and is the sharpest near-term pressure point.
  - **Thin, heavily adjusted earnings (severity 40):** FY26 reported PAT ₹137.6m (0.5% margin) is ~4x below management's adjusted PAT ₹556.8m; one "normalisation," the new Labour Code cost (~₹100m post-tax), is a recurring forward cost, not a clean one-off, so adjusted run-rate earnings are overstated. The add-backs are one-time *charges* — the read is that reported earnings are fragile, not inflated.
  - *(Below the 40 threshold, summarised, not separately propagated: audit-trail edit-log gap on revenue/payroll books, severity 38; EPCG export-obligation contingency ₹490.81m, severity 30; profit leakage to minorities, severity 28. The cross-cutting thread across these and the two above: HCG's reported headline is cleaner than its underlying cash and control reality.)*

- **Whether any automatic disqualifier triggered:** No. All eight disqualifier checks clear on primary evidence.

- **Biggest missing data point:** the FY26 audited annual report (board-approved 19 May 2026) and a post-KKR-changeover detailed Reg-31 shareholding pattern / FY26 corporate-governance promoter-encumbrance line. Secondary: a per-centre payback / vintage-by-opening-year curve, which would close the unit-economics value test.

- **Whether the business deserves deeper work, and what would change the answer:** Yes, but conditionally — the demand and footprint are real, but the returns and governance do not clear a quality bar, so deeper work is justified only if valuation is cheap (it is a "buy the maturity migration at the right price" question, not a quality compounder). What would move it **toward high-quality:** statutory Return on Capital crossing ~12% and EBIT margin closing toward the ~16% peer median, driven by ramping centres migrating into the mature bucket, plus two-to-three years of stable leadership executing the stated 2.5x leverage ceiling. What would move it **toward low-quality:** a re-acceleration of debt-funded, goodwill-heavy acquisitions under the new team, another CEO/CFO change, a tight credit window forcing the minority puts onto a stretched balance sheet, or a tightening of DPCO/scheme tariffs that compresses the cost-of-consumption spread.

## 5. Simple Summary

- **What it does:** runs a chain of cancer hospitals in India (25 centres + one in Kenya), delivering radiation, medical, and surgical oncology under one roof to individual patients.
- **How it makes money:** patient revenue ≈ beds × occupancy × price per patient; ~56% from cash/insurance, ~33% from government schemes; margin expands as each centre matures.
- **Does each new unit create value:** only eventually — a mature centre earns 27% ROCE, but a new ("emerging") centre runs at −22% ROCE and destroys capital for years until it scales.
- **Which segment matters most:** essentially one — India oncology is ~97.6% of revenue; fertility is being sold off.
- **Moat, and against whom:** narrow and economically unproven — a clinical/technology and footprint edge over Apollo Cancer Centres, American Oncology Institute, and Max, but it has not produced peer-level margins or returns.
- **What it depends on externally:** Indian government healthcare policy (drug price controls, scheme tariffs) it cannot control, and the oncologists who both generate the revenue and are its largest cost.
- **Is capital allocated well:** mixed — clean accounting and recent deleveraging, but goodwill-heavy serial M&A, ~₹3.3bn of bank-funding-dependent minority buy-out obligations, no dividend (negative retained earnings), and an owner/leadership team that changed twice in five years.
- **Does it deserve deeper work:** yes, but only if the price is cheap — this is a bet on ramping centres maturing into profitability, not a proven high-return business; it earns below its cost of capital today.
