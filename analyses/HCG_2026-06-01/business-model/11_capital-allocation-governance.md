## 2. Classification

**Governance red flags** — driven specifically by control and management instability, not by accounting integrity.

The reasoning: most *accounting-integrity* rows are clean and low-severity (unmodified audit opinion by a KPMG-network firm with no auditor change, immaterial operating related-party transactions well under the 25% disqualifier threshold, no promoter pledge, no hidden off-balance-sheet vehicles, disclosed and shareholder-approved processes throughout). On those alone HCG would read as standard professional management. But two rows sit in red-flag territory and they are structural, not cosmetic: the company has changed controlling shareholder twice in five years (Founder → CVC 2020 → KKR May 2025) and has turned over almost its entire senior leadership (CEO, CFO twice, board chair role, both sets of PE nominee directors) in the same window, with a 26% open offer still being completed. A new controlling owner installing a new CEO and CFO resets strategy, capital-allocation philosophy, incentive design and the meaning of any multi-year track record. That is the defining governance feature of this name today, so the classification is set by it rather than averaged away against the clean accounting rows.

Two qualifiers keep this from being a worse call. First, every step is disclosed, SEBI-LODR/Companies-Act compliant, and shareholder-approved — this is orderly PE ownership transition, not concealment or self-dealing. Second, the earliest FY26 signals under KKR point toward tighter discipline, not looser: a rights issue used to cut net debt/EBITDA from ~2.2-2.3x to 1.4x, a stated leverage ceiling of 2.5x, and a portfolio-sharpening divestiture (sale of the Milann fertility business) that partially reverses the prior diversification roll-up [Q4/FY26 transcript, prepared remarks + Q&A, 20 May 2026]. The capital-allocation concerns (goodwill-heavy serial M&A, rising debt into FY25, a 36% receivable build) are real but secondary to the control/management-discontinuity flag.

## 3. Most Material Signal

The single signal that, if it deteriorated further, would harden this classification is **senior management turnover layered on the change of controlling shareholder**. HCG's verdict rests on a leadership team and an owner that are both new: KKR took sole control on 30 May 2025, a KKR-nominee CEO (Dr. Manish Mattoo) took over from 30 Jun 2025, and a new CFO (Sanjeev Kumar Saxena) was announced for FY26 — the third CFO in roughly three years. The reason this matters more than the goodwill or leverage rows is that those are quantifiable and currently improving (debt is falling, M&A pace is being reset toward oncology focus), whereas leadership/owner instability is what determines whether the FY26 deleveraging and divestiture discipline persist or prove to be transition-window optics. A further deterioration — for example a fast follow-on exit by KKR, another CEO/CFO change, or a re-acceleration of debt-funded acquisitions under the new team — would confirm that the company is run for sponsor timelines rather than durable operating discipline, and would lock the governance read firmly in red-flag territory. Conversely, two to three years of stable leadership executing the stated 2.5x leverage ceiling and oncology-focus strategy would, on its own, justify re-rating this toward standard professional management.

## 4. Capital Allocation Score /100

**52 / 100** (higher = better discipline and cleaner governance).

Derivation: the table averages to a mean severity of roughly 43 (higher = worse), which inverts to about 57 on a higher-is-better basis. That is then weighted down to 52 for what matters most in this specific case — the two highest-severity rows (control change at 55 and senior-management turnover at 62) are the load-bearing governance facts and are weighted above the clean accounting rows rather than averaged flat against them, consistent with not letting low-severity audit/RPT rows dilute a structural red flag. The score sits in the middle "mixed" band: accounting integrity, disclosure quality and the early FY26 deleveraging/focus moves pull it up, while two PE control changes in five years, full leadership turnover, goodwill-heavy serial M&A, and a 36% receivable build pull it down. It is not lower because nothing here impairs the integrity of the reported numbers (unmodified opinion, no auditor change, immaterial RPTs, no pledge, nothing hidden off balance sheet); it is not higher because the people and owner steering capital allocation are largely unproven in their current configuration.
