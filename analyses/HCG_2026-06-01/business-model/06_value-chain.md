# Value Chain Position — HCG

*HealthCare Global Enterprises Limited (NSE: HCG / BSE: 539787). Reporting currency INR, FY ends 31 March. Evidence base: FY24-25 Integrated Annual Report (filed Sep 2025); Q4/FY26 investor presentation (May 19, 2026); Q4/FY26 earnings call (May 20, 2026); Q3/FY26 earnings call (Feb 9, 2026); Capital IQ Suppliers / Customers relationship files. Upstream input: 02_business-identity.md (read).*

HCG is a single-specialty (oncology) hospital operator. In value-chain terms it is a **service provider** that sits between two groups it does not control: upstream input providers (drug/consumable distributors, imported-equipment makers, and — most importantly — the oncologists who bring patients in) and downstream payors (individual cancer patients, insurers/TPAs, corporates, and government health schemes). Because HCG runs an "asset-light" model — leased real estate, pay-per-use radiation/imaging equipment, and a largely visiting (fee-for-service) clinician roster — it has deliberately pushed two of its most important inputs off its own balance sheet and into supplier hands [FY24-25 Annual Report, "Asset-light model", p.46-47; "Total employees" panel, p.~28].

There is **no external supplier or customer concentration disclosed** in those vendor files — consistent with a business whose customers are tens of thousands of individual patients and whose inputs are spread across a 15,000+ supplier base [Capital IQ Suppliers file; Capital IQ Customers file; FY24-25 AR, "Total supplier base: 15000+", p.29/63].

## 2. Input Cost Pass-Through

HCG does **not** demonstrate clean, contractual pass-through of input costs; the filings show input costs rising faster than HCG offsets them. Cost of consumption (drugs and medical/non-medical consumables) rose to ₹5,805.99m in FY25 from ₹4,754.17m in FY24, lifting its share of total revenue to 25.72% from 24.64% — a ~108 bps drag, meaning consumable inflation outran realization on a like-for-like basis [FY24-25 AR, "Cost of consumption", p.169]. Two structural constraints sit on the upstream side: (1) HCG dispenses drugs that fall under India's Drugs (Prices Control) Order, 2013 (DPCO), so the government caps prices on scheduled essential cancer drugs — HCG cannot freely re-price the regulated portion of its pharmacy [FY24-25 AR, regulatory-compliance schedules, p.~178 and p.~250]; and (2) a large share of cost is medical consultancy charges (₹4,816.7m in FY25, the single biggest line in Other expenses) plus pay-per-use radiation-equipment fees, both of which rise with clinician hiring and volume rather than being passed through to patients [FY24-25 AR, Note 30, p.~228; MD&A "Other expenses", p.169]. The company's own framing is that realization grows roughly with "medical inflation" of about 5% plus mix, not via an input-indexed escalator: when asked to bridge 15% revenue growth, management split it into ~10% volume and ~5% ARPP, with ARPP improvement coming from replacing low-margin work with higher-margin work over time [Q3/FY26 call, Feb 9 2026, Q&A; Q4/FY26 call, May 20 2026, Q&A]. No contractual cost-escalator or indexed-pricing clause with patients or payors is disclosed in the pool.

## 3. Customer Pricing Power

HCG has partial, mix-dependent pricing power, not broad pricing power. On the cash/insurance/corporate side (~65% of oncology revenue, or ~56% excluding corporates per the business-identity read) it sets list prices and has lifted average revenue per patient (ARPP) — ARPP was ~₹84,000 in Q3 FY26, up ~5% year on year, and FY26 saw a 3% improvement in average revenue per patient — but management is explicit that this comes from case mix (more high-acuity surgical/radiation work) and payor mix, not from raising sticker prices, and it cites "pricing sensitivity around cancer care" as a reason it guides only ~5% ARPP growth [Q3/FY26 call, Feb 9 2026, prepared remarks + Q&A; Q4/FY26 call, May 20 2026, prepared remarks]. On the government-scheme side (~33% of revenue), HCG is a price-taker: scheme tariffs are set by the payor, and a transition in the Odisha state government scheme cut one cluster's ARPP by 3% year on year in Q3 FY26 — direct evidence that HCG absorbs scheme repricing rather than dictating it [Q3/FY26 call, Feb 9 2026, prepared remarks]. The clearest signal on pricing-power limits is behavioural: across the last 24 months HCG chose to *pare down low-margin business* and accept slower revenue growth rather than push price to defend volume, telling analysts the low-margin-to-high-margin transition would "normalize over the next couple of quarters" — a company with strong pricing power would raise price, not exit the work [Q4/FY26 call, May 20 2026, prepared remarks + Q&A]. There is no disclosed instance in the pool of HCG taking a broad list-price increase and holding volume; the volume reaction it does cite (paring low-margin cases) is a volume *sacrifice* it chose, which means pricing power on the patient side is real but bounded.

## 4. Economic Control Verdict

**Mixed.**

HCG sets prices to the majority cash/insurance/corporate payor base (~65% of oncology revenue) and has many alternatives on the supplier side (15,000+ suppliers, no disclosed single-vendor dependence), which gives it real economic control on parts of both ends [Q4/FY26 deck, payor-mix slide; FY24-25 AR, "Total supplier base: 15000+", p.29]. But it is squeezed on the two relationships that matter most for its economics: roughly a third of revenue is government-scheme work where the payor sets the tariff (Odisha scheme repricing cut a cluster's ARPP 3%), and its single largest cost is the oncologists themselves, whom the company concedes it is "highly dependent on" and pays largely on a fee-for-service basis — so the people who generate the revenue capture a large, growing slice of it (medical consultancy charges ₹4,816.7m in FY25) [Q3/FY26 call, Feb 9 2026; FY24-25 AR, Risk factor (e), p.~150; Note 30, p.~228]. Rising consumable costs (25.7% of revenue, up ~108 bps) and DPCO drug-price caps further limit upstream control. Strong on one side, weak on the other — this is a mixed position, not full control.

## 5. The Single Biggest Bargaining Risk

The clinician relationship: if HCG's high-volume oncologists (the 3,388 visiting/400+ specialist roster it depends on for patient flow) negotiated a larger share of economics or left for a competitor, both revenue and the largest cost line would move against HCG at once — it is the one value-chain link the company itself flags as a dependency [FY24-25 AR, Risk factor (e) "highly dependent on... key clinicians", p.~150; Note 30 medical consultancy charges ₹4,816.7m, p.~228; Q4/FY26 call, May 20 2026, growth attributed to "new clinician additions"].
