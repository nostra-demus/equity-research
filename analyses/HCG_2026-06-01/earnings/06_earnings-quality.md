# Earnings Quality — HCG

**Company:** HealthCare Global Enterprises Limited (NSE: HCG) — pan-India oncology hospital chain.
**Reporting currency:** INR million (₹ Mn), consolidated, post-Ind AS 116 (leases capitalised). FY ends 31 March (FY26 = year ended 31 Mar 2026).
**Basis note:** Two EBITDA constructs are kept separate throughout. **Reported EBITDA** = audited statutory operating result, pre other-income. **Adjusted EBITDA** = company-defined (Reported EBITDA + ESOP + one-time cost). FCF is defined per CLAUDE.md as **CFO − total capex**; the company does not publish its own FCF figure. Cash-flow capex = "Acquisition of PP&E and others" line in the audited investing section (FY23–FY25) and the deck capex figure for FY26.
**Data status:** Cash flow statement is **available and audited** for FY23, FY24, FY25 [2, p.314; 6, p.288]. FY26 cash flow / balance sheet / P&L are from the **board-approved audited results presentation dated 19 May 2026** [1, p.20-22], not a full statutory statement (standalone audited FY26 PDF not in pool). No partial-data cap applies — the cash conversion and EBITDA-to-FCF bridge are fully assessable. FY22 detail below the EBITDA line is not in the pool, so the bridge runs FY23–FY26 (four years).

## 2. Cash Conversion Assessment

CFO tracks EBITDA closely and has done so for four straight years: CFO/Reported EBITDA was 84.2% (FY23), 86.3% (FY24), 81.9% (FY25) and 74.5% (FY26) — every year comfortably above the 70% healthy threshold and never near the 50% red-flag line [2, p.314; 6, p.288; 1, p.22]. The trajectory is **down**, from 84–86% to 74.5%, because working-capital absorbed cash every single year (−₹272 Mn to −₹765 Mn) while EBITDA grew faster than cash collection in FY26 (EBITDA +20.3% vs CFO +9.5%). Management's headline "~75% EBITDA to CFO" claim [1, "Net Debt (Pre Ind-AS)" slide] is verified against Reported EBITDA (74.5%) and is, if anything, slightly conservative versus Adjusted EBITDA (73.7%). Verdict: **operating earnings are cash-backed**, but the conversion rate is eroding as the business scales receivables and inventory ahead of revenue.

## 3. Working Capital Trends

Days computed on year-end balances ÷ annual flow × 365. DSO on total revenue; DIO and DPO on COGS (purchases + change in inventories). Balances are audited (FY24/FY25) [2, p.312; 6, p.286] and from the FY26 results deck balance sheet [1, p.20]. Note: HCG's trade payables are unusually high relative to COGS because medical-consultancy charges and other operating payables sit partly in "other current/financial liabilities," so the **absolute DPO level is inflated by definition**; the *direction* is the signal.

| Metric | FY24 | FY25 | FY26 | Direction | Risk |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|---|
| Receivable days (DSO) | 56.1 | 65.8 | 60.3 | Up then partial reversal | **Flagged in FY25** (see below) |
| Inventory days (DIO) | 32.8 | 33.3 | 31.5 | Roughly flat | Low |
| Payable days (DPO) | 215.8 | 210.1 | 185.1 | Falling | Medium — cash being used to pay suppliers faster |
| Cash conversion cycle (DSO + DIO − DPO) | −126.9 | −111.0 | −93.2 | Less negative | Working capital becoming a larger cash user |

Flag tests:
- **DSO rising >10% YoY — TRIGGERED in FY25.** DSO rose from 56.1 to 65.8 days (+17.3%), and trade receivables grew **+36.3%** (₹2,940 → ₹4,009 Mn) against revenue growth of **+16.3%** [2, p.312, p.314]. Receivables grew more than 2x revenue in FY25. This is the single clearest accrual concern in the file. In FY26 it partially unwound — DSO fell to 60.3, receivables grew only +4.9% vs revenue +14.5% [1, p.20]. The most likely driver is government-scheme / insurance / corporate-billing receivables (oncology has long state-scheme collection cycles; HCG flagged Andhra Pradesh state-scheme disruption in Q3FY26) rather than channel-type revenue pull-forward — but the FY25 spike is not fully explained in the pool. *Inference, not from filings: the FY25 build looks collection-cycle driven, not fictitious revenue, because it reversed and CFO still grew; this is not proven.*
- **DIO rising >15% YoY — not triggered.** Inventory days are flat (~32–33). Inventory grew +24.3% in FY25, but off COGS that grew +22.1% — broadly matched. No channel-stuffing signal (hospital consumables, not finished-goods inventory).
- **DPO rising sharply — not triggered; opposite is true.** DPO is *falling* (216 → 185 days). HCG is paying suppliers faster, which uses cash. This is a quality positive (not stretching suppliers) but it is the main reason the cash conversion cycle is becoming less negative and CFO is lagging EBITDA.

- **Recurs every period:** ESOP add-back recurs every year (₹78.5 → ₹65.4 → ₹14.4 across FY24–FY26) — it is **not a true one-off** and should not be excluded from a "clean" earnings view. The amount is now small.

The large gap is entirely at the PAT line, from two disclosed exceptionals, not from EBITDA gaming.
- **SBC excluded from adjusted numbers:** YES — ESOP is added back to Adjusted EBITDA. Flagged. Immaterial in FY26 but a recurring-cost exclusion in principle.

## 9. Earnings Quality Score

**Score: 66 / 100 — Mostly clean but with working-capital and PAT-line adjustment noise (band 61–80).**

Single most important reason: **operating earnings are genuinely cash-backed** — CFO/EBITDA has been above 70% for four straight years (84% → 75%) and the FY25 audit opinion is unmodified [2, p.311, p.314] — but the score is held in the 60s, not higher, by three things: (1) CFO/EBITDA is **eroding** (84% to 74.5%) as working capital drains cash every year; (2) a **FY25 receivable build** (+36% vs +16% revenue, DSO +17%) with rising write-offs that is only partly explained; and (3) reported FY26 PAT is distorted by exceptionals to the point that the **adjusted-vs-reported PAT gap is +305%**, so the headline bottom line is not a usable run-rate. The distortions are transparent and disclosed (not deceptive), and the EBITDA-level adjustment is trivial (1.15%) — that is why this clears 60 rather than sitting in the 41–60 band.

## 10. The Single Biggest Quality Concern

The single biggest risk that reported earnings overstate economic reality is **at the cash-conversion and receivables level, not at the EBITDA level.** EBITDA is real and cash-backed, but the gap between EBITDA growth (+20% in FY26) and CFO growth (+9.5%) has widened for two years, and the FY25 receivable surge (receivables +36% on revenue +16%, DSO from 56 to 66 days) with rising trade-receivable write-offs (₹97 Mn) shows that a chunk of HCG's revenue — likely government-scheme, insurance and corporate billing in oncology — converts to cash slowly and with real credit-loss leakage. If that receivable build had continued instead of partly reversing in FY26, reported EBITDA growth would have looked increasingly like an accounting figure unsupported by collections. As it stands, the operating earnings are clean enough; the more practical trap for an analyst is the **reported PAT line**, which the FY26 goodwill-impairment and labour-code exceptionals have rendered almost meaningless (₹138 Mn reported vs ₹557 Mn adjusted) — so any model that anchors on reported FY26 EPS of ₹1.0 will badly misjudge the underlying earnings power, while the heavy growth capex (₹2,885 Mn) means headline FCF (₹586 Mn) understates the ~₹2.3 Bn of cash the business throws off before discretionary expansion.

**Flag for master synthesizer:** (1) Reported FY26 PAT/EPS is not a usable run-rate — use adjusted PAT (₹557 Mn) and disclose the two exceptionals. (2) FY25 receivable build + recurring trade-receivable write-offs are a recurring earnings-quality drag tied to government-scheme/insurance collection cycles — monitor DSO each quarter. (3) Depreciation useful-life change (effective FY22) modestly flatters near-term EBIT and reverses from FY28. (4) Headline FCF is depressed by disclosed growth capex, not weak cash generation — maintenance-capex FCF is ~₹2.3 Bn.
