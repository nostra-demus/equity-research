# Coverage & Covenants — HCG

HealthCare Global Enterprises Limited (NSE: HCG / BSE: 539787). Report date: 2026-06-01. Cancer-care / multispecialty hospital operator (India + one centre in Kenya). **Reporting currency: INR million (₹ Mn), consolidated, Ind AS.** Fiscal year ends 31 March (FY26 = year ended 31 Mar 2026). Operating company with corporate term-loan debt plus Ind AS 116 lease liabilities — the coverage/covenant framework applies [01_capital-structure-and-leverage; FY24-25 AR, Note 18].

**Inputs used.** Debt and the EBITDA base are taken verbatim from `01_capital-structure-and-leverage.md`. EBIT, EBITDA, finance cost and capex are from the **board-approved audited FY26 results presentation dated 19 May 2026** [FY26 deck, P&L p.19; Capex/Net Debt p.14]. The finance-cost split (lease vs non-lease interest) is from the **FY25 statutory borrowings/finance-cost note** [FY24-25 AR, Note 28], because the FY26 deck reports only the single finance-cost line and does not break it out — the FY26 split is therefore proxied and flagged. Cash-conversion quality is from `earnings/06_earnings-quality.md`.

**EBITDA basis and interest convention (stated up front).** EBITDA throughout is **Reported EBITDA ₹4,657.9 Mn** (audited statutory operating result, pre other-income); **Adjusted EBITDA ₹4,711.3 Mn** (company-defined: Reported + ESOP ₹14.4 Mn + one-time ₹39.0 Mn) is shown alongside but differs by only +₹53 Mn (+1.15%), so Reported is the conservative default [FY26 deck, p.19; earnings/06 §7]. EBITDA is **post-Ind AS 116** (leases capitalised). Interest is **gross finance cost**; the company does not net interest income against finance cost in the P&L. Because Ind AS 116 routes a large lease-interest charge through finance cost, coverage is shown on **two interest bases**: (i) total finance cost incl. lease interest (the like-for-like, post-Ind AS view), and (ii) cash borrowing interest only, ex-lease (proxied) — the two diverge materially and both are reported to avoid a misleading single number.

**Interest-basis note (critical to reading these ratios).** The FY26 single finance-cost line of ₹1,765.7 Mn includes a large Ind AS 116 lease-interest component. The FY25 statutory note splits finance cost into **non-lease (cash borrowing) interest ₹848.75 Mn** and **lease interest (net, in finance cost) ₹696.86 Mn** — i.e. **~45% of FY25 finance cost was lease interest, not cash interest on borrowings** [FY24-25 AR, Note 28]. The FY26 deck does not publish this split. *Proxy, not from filings: carrying the FY25 45.1% lease share forward implies FY26 cash borrowing interest of ~₹970 Mn and lease interest ~₹796 Mn; scaling FY25 lease interest by the broadly flat lease balance gives a similar ~₹760 Mn. The ex-lease ("cash interest") coverage figures above use ~₹970 Mn and are therefore approximate.* The practical read: on a **cash-debt-service** basis (interest actually paid on borrowings), coverage is roughly **4.8x EBITDA / cash interest** — materially above the 2.64x lease-inclusive figure — because nearly half the "interest" is the unwinding of capitalised rent, not bank interest. This is corroborated by management: quarterly interest cost "~INR40 crore" (₹400 Mn × 4 ≈ ₹1,600 Mn) is the *total* finance-cost run-rate, and the average interest **cost on borrowings is ~8%** on ₹9,104 Mn ≈ ₹730 Mn of pure bank interest [Q4/FY26 transcript, Q&A, 20 May 2026]. Both bases are legitimate; the lease-inclusive 2.64x is the conservative anchor and the cash-interest ~4.8x is the practical debt-service reality.

**EBITDA cash-backing caveat (from earnings/06).** Coverage above uses Reported EBITDA. Per `earnings/06_earnings-quality.md`, EBITDA is **cash-backed but the conversion rate is eroding**: CFO/Reported EBITDA was 74.5% in FY26 (down from 84–86% in FY23–FY24), as working capital drained cash every year and a FY25 receivable build (DSO 56→66 days, receivables +36% vs revenue +16%) only partly reversed [earnings/06 §1–2, §9]. EBITDA is therefore not materially above cash-backed EBITDA (the adjusted-vs-reported EBITDA gap is only +1.15%), but the EBITDA-to-cash slippage means the **CFO that actually services debt is ~25% below EBITDA** — so the (EBITDA−capex)/interest of 1.00x is a fair, not a flattering, gauge of cash debt-service capacity, and the heavy FY26 growth capex (₹1,696 Mn of the ₹2,885 Mn) is the swing factor (maintenance-capex coverage is 1.96x) [earnings/06 §1].

## 2. Covenant Inventory

**No financial maintenance covenant is disclosed in the data pool.** The FY25 Annual Report — which contains the full statutory borrowings note (Note 18, with security and repayment terms) and the auditor's report — discloses **no maximum-leverage, minimum-interest-coverage, minimum-net-worth or minimum-liquidity maintenance covenant** for any facility [FY24-25 AR, Note 18; searched in full]. The only "covenant" wording in the document is boilerplate in the Stakeholders' Relationship Committee charter referring to debenture-holder security cover [FY24-25 AR, Corporate Governance Report, p.~134] — and **HCG has no debentures/listed debt outstanding** ("no External Commercial Borrowing by the Company"; no bonds/notes) [FY24-25 AR, p.~93; 01_capital-structure §1]. Term loans are bilateral bank facilities; their specific financial covenants (if any) are not reproduced in the filing.

**The only quantified anchors are self-imposed or disclosure-only, not lender tests.** Management states an **internal** net-debt/EBITDA ceiling of **2.5x** (ex-lease basis) and that leverage came down "from 2.2x, 2.3x to 1.4x" after the ₹4,250 Mn rights issue [Q4/FY26 transcript, Q&A]. This is a board-level discipline, not a covenant a lender can act on. The Schedule III DSCR (FY25 1.66x) is a regulatory ratio disclosure under the Companies Act, again not a maintenance test. There is therefore **no external trigger that a lender can pull on a leverage or coverage deterioration** that is visible in the pool — which cuts both ways: there is no near-term breach risk from a disclosed covenant, but there is also no disclosed covenant protection or early-warning mechanism for creditors, and the bilateral bank loans almost certainly carry undisclosed covenants that this analysis cannot see.

## 3. Headroom & Breach Proximity

Because no lender covenant is disclosed, "tightest covenant" below is assessed against (a) the **only quantified internal constraint** management states (2.5x ex-lease net leverage), and (b) **labeled-assumption** market covenants, shown separately and not used for scoring.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---:|
| Tightest *disclosed/quantified* constraint | **Management's internal 2.5x ex-lease net-leverage ceiling** (self-imposed; not a lender covenant) |
| Headroom on that constraint | **+68%** (actual 0.80x ex-lease net vs 2.5x); +71% on the company-headline 0.73x basis |
| EBITDA decline that would breach the 2.5x ex-lease ceiling (net debt held constant) | ~−68% (EBITDA would have to fall from ₹4,658 Mn to ~₹1,497 Mn) — but ex-lease net debt is only ₹3,744 Mn, so in practice cash burn would move net debt too |
| Debt increase that would breach the 2.5x ex-lease ceiling (EBITDA held constant) | +₹7,901 Mn of net debt (net debt would have to roughly triple, from ₹3,744 Mn to ₹11,645 Mn) |
| **Tightest *assumed* lender covenant** (labeled assumption — not assessable for scoring) | **Assumed min interest coverage 2.0x** (lease-inclusive interest) |
| Indicative headroom vs assumed min coverage 2.0x | +32% (actual EBITDA/interest 2.64x) |
| EBITDA decline that would breach an assumed 2.0x coverage (interest held constant) | ~−24% (EBITDA to ~₹3,531 Mn) |
| Indicative headroom vs assumed max net leverage 3.5x (incl. lease) | +26% (actual 2.57x) |
| EBITDA decline that would breach an assumed 3.5x net leverage (net debt held constant) | ~−26% (EBITDA to ~₹3,427 Mn) |

**Read of breach proximity.** Against the internal 2.5x ceiling, headroom is wide (+68%) and only a near-tripling of net debt or a ~two-thirds EBITDA collapse would breach it — i.e. it is not a near-term constraint. **If** the bilateral loans carry typical covenants (assumed min-coverage 2.0x / max-net-leverage 3.5x incl. lease), the indicative tightest test is interest coverage, which would trip on roughly a **−24% EBITDA decline** holding interest constant — a plausible downside given oncology-volume sensitivity and state-scheme collection risk [business-model/10; earnings/06 §3]. The honest conclusion: the company looks far from any *disclosed* constraint, but the absence of covenant disclosure means a real lender test could sit closer than the internal ceiling implies, and the stress agent (06) should run breach checks against both the assumed 2.0x coverage and 3.5x leverage lines.

## 4. Coverage / Covenant Read

Earnings carry interest with a moderate, not wide, cushion on the conservative (lease-inclusive) basis: **EBITDA/interest is 2.64x and EBIT/interest is 1.40x** [FY26 deck, p.19] — but ~45% of the ₹1,765.7 Mn finance cost is Ind AS 116 lease interest, so on **cash bank-interest only the coverage is roughly 4.8x**, and after total growth-and-maintenance capex it drops to **1.00x** (1.96x on maintenance capex alone) [FY24-25 AR, Note 28; FY26 deck, p.14]. **No lender financial covenant is disclosed in the pool**, so true covenant headroom is **Not assessable** for scoring (partial-data cap applied); the only quantified anchors are management's self-imposed 2.5x ex-lease net-leverage ceiling — against which leverage of 0.80x sits ~68% below — and a Schedule III DSCR of 1.66x (FY25), neither of which a lender can act on. The practical risk is not a visible covenant breach but the combination of an **eroding cash conversion (CFO/EBITDA 74.5%, down from 86%)**, heavy growth capex that has pushed (EBITDA−capex)/interest to break-even, and the fact that an undisclosed bilateral-loan coverage covenant (if it exists at a typical ~2.0x) would trip on a ~−24% EBITDA decline — so the survival floor depends on a covenant the pool cannot show, which agent 06 must stress against labeled assumptions.
