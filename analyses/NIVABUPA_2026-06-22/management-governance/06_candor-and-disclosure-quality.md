No formal non-GAAP earnings reconciliation exists in the standard sense, but two full sets of accounts are published.

## 3. Disclosure Transparency

Management provides consistent, specific KPI disclosure across all six calls. Each quarter, the CEO presents: GWP growth on a like-for-like (ex-1/N) basis, IFRS PAT, combined ratio (IFRS), loss ratio and expense ratio decomposed, EOM ratio, retail market share, solvency ratio, investment AUM and yield, NPS across 25 touch points, auto-adjudication rate, and channel mix. These are the KPIs that actually drive value for a health insurer scaling toward underwriting profitability. The disclosure set has been consistent across all six post-IPO quarters, and in Q4 FY26 the CFO also quantified segment loss ratios (group health 60.5%, retail health improving each quarter from 68.1% H1 to 66.8% FY26) when asked directly. Loss ratio by segment is disclosed, though the group vs retail segment P&L is not separately published as a standalone segment report.

The one area that has become less transparent over time is the claims auto-adjudication rate, which was a feature KPI in Q2 FY25 (cited as 28%) and Q3 FY25 (28.6%), disappeared from the Q1 FY26 call during the suspension (management disclosed the suspension but did not restate the metric), and was not highlighted as prominently in subsequent calls. This is not a material concealment — the suspension was disclosed — but the cadence of reporting on a previously prominent KPI changed at the moment the metric declined. The FY26 intangible capex jump (₹430 mn, +343%) is mentioned in the context of named IT systems (Oracle Health, Sprinklr, Convin AI) but capitalisation methodology is not described — verifiable only from the primary annual report that is absent from the data pool.

**The most significant transparency gap is the absence of RPT disclosure.** Bupa Singapore, a promoter, plausibly receives brand royalties, reinsurance payments, and technology licence fees from Niva Bupa. None of these flows have been quantified or confirmed arm's-length on any transcript, and the primary annual report with the Ind AS 24 related-party note is not available. This is a data sufficiency gap rather than a disclosed concealment, but it is the largest missing piece in the overall transparency picture.

## 4. Tone in Bad Times

The worst recent period in terms of reported numbers is Q3 FY25 (Feb 4, 2025): the company was freshly listed (November 2024), facing its first post-IPO earnings call, with a reported EPS of ₹0.07 against a ₹0.35 consensus — an 80% miss under the headline normalized metric. CEO Ramachandran's tone on that call was direct: he opened by naming the accounting change explicitly, framed it as an industry-wide event, stated what it meant and what it did not mean for economic value, and confirmed the underlying IFRS PAT had tripled quarter-on-quarter. He did not avoid the question. The CFO provided both accounting bases side by side with enough precision that analysts could reconstruct the figures. The Q&A section shows analysts drilling into loss ratio decomposition, commission accounting mechanics, and regulatory glide path — and management answered all of these directly, including providing numerical breakdowns (9M loss ratio at 64.2% on IFRS, retail 67%, overall 64.2%).

Q1 FY26 (Jul 31, 2025) is the second worst period — the claims SLA miss was disclosed proactively in the CEO's prepared remarks, not in response to an analyst question. Critically, management separately flagged that Q1 FY26 had experienced a reserve build-up (loss ratio rise) because of the auto-adjudication suspension, quantified the SLA decline (90% to 76%), and confirmed the resolution timeline. The Q&A on that call shows analysts pressing on the loss ratio drivers, reserve adequacy, and whether the trend signals broader incidence-rate shifts. Management answered each question with numbers (retail loss ratio 68%, group 61%, prior year retail 66%, group 58%). The CFO did not deflect questions about the reserve strengthening — he confirmed it was a prudent response to the suspension and forecast stabilisation by year-end. This is straightforward Q&A, not evasion.

The consistent pattern across bad periods: management leads with IFRS to contextualise IGAAP distortions, discloses operational problems in prepared remarks rather than waiting for analyst questions, and answers Q&A with specific numbers. There is no pattern of deflecting, going to "offline" for substantive questions (one analyst question on IFRS reconciliation was briefly deferred to offline, which is acceptable and standard), or answering qualitatively when quantitative data was available.

## 5. Candor Read

Niva Bupa's management, led by CEO Ramachandran and CFO Mahendra, has a consistent record of communicating bad news directly: the FQ3 FY25 −80% EPS miss was framed in its structural accounting context before any analyst asked, the Q1 FY26 claims disruption was disclosed in prepared remarks with specific SLA numbers, and Q&A across all six calls shows management answering pointed questions with figures rather than narratives. Non-GAAP aggressiveness is low — the dual IGAAP / IFRS reporting reflects regulatory mandate, not earnings management, and the full-year GAAP-to-adjusted gap is near zero. The biggest candor limitation is structural: the audited Annual Report is absent from the data pool, so RPT flows with Bupa Singapore (brand, reinsurance, technology) are unverified, and management has not voluntarily quantified these on any call. The combined ratio comparison base chosen in Q4 FY26 (versus FY24 rather than FY25) mildly flatters the FY26 progress narrative, and the failure to proactively correct the consistently high street GWP estimates is a passive transparency shortfall.

**Verdict: candid / high-trust** — with one important caveat. On every miss that has occurred in the observable window, management disclosed it proactively, explained it with numbers, and took Q&A directly. The non-GAAP picture is not aggressive. The trust limitation is the unverified RPT exposure with Bupa Singapore, which is a data-sufficiency gap rather than an active concealment but which the audited Annual Report alone can resolve. Until that filing is reviewed, a small residual confidence reduction is appropriate. The single biggest candor signal is the Q1 FY26 claims auto-adjudication suspension disclosure: management named the failure, quantified the SLA decline, and explained the reserve impact in the same prepared-remarks paragraph that described every other positive metric — this is what candid communication looks like.
