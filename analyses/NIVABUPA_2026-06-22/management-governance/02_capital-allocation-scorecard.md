Currency: INR Millions (₹ mn). 1 crore = 10 mn. 5-year cumulative covers FY22–FY26. CFO reference for % calculation uses 3-year normalised CFO (FY24–FY26 only; FY25 CFO of ₹16,753 mn is inflated by the one-time 1/N accounting build-up effect — see Note 1). Using FY25 as a run-rate for capital-allocation math would overstate the cash base.

**Note 2 — Insurer capital model:** For a health insurer, the primary use of capital is not capex or acquisitions but building the **premium float** (the pool of policyholder premiums held as reserves until claims are paid). This float — represented by the AUM/investment book of ₹9,670 crores (FY26) — is the main "asset" being built. The insurer also must maintain a statutory **solvency ratio** above 1.50x. Capital is therefore consumed by business growth (float growth requires proportionate solvency capital) rather than deployed into acquisitions, buybacks, or physical assets. This is the correct lens for reading uses of capital here. [Q4 FY26 transcript, May 8, 2026, p.5; Q4 FY25 transcript, May 7, 2025, p.9]

**Capex composition (FY24–FY26, the directly comparable window):** Total capex over 3 years = ₹1,225 mn (FY24: ₹330 mn, FY25: ₹409 mn, FY26: ₹486 mn). The FY26 capex spike is driven by capitalised intangibles (₹430 mn vs ₹57 mn PP&E), reflecting technology investment — the Oracle Health core-system upgrade, AI/GenAI tooling, and Sprinklr CRM. This is growth capex consistent with the digital insurer model. Capex intensity is very low: ₹486 mn on ₹84,435 mn revenue = 0.6% revenue intensity. **Incremental ROIC note:** For a health insurer the standard ROIC formula (Δ NOPAT / Δ invested capital) does not map cleanly because "invested capital" includes both equity capital and policyholder reserves (which are not capital in the industrial sense). The most meaningful available proxy is:
1. **ROE trajectory:** 5.3% (FY24) → 10.7% (FY26), improving but still below a likely 13–15% cost of equity for an Indian insurer of this risk profile.
2. **Float yield:** Investment yield on the policyholder float is 7.2% (FY26) — a stable, bond-like return on the primary internal "capital deployment."
3. **Operating leverage:** EOM ratio declining 550 bps in one year indicates returns are improving on every new rupee of premium written. This is the equivalent of rising marginal ROIC in an insurer.

The evidence shows organic reinvestment is working — the franchise is becoming more efficient as it scales. However, the company is still below its own stated profitability target (99% combined ratio by FY29, currently 101.4%), meaning capital deployed in new business is marginally loss-generating on a combined-ratio basis, subsidised by investment income.

## 5. Capital-Allocation Read

Over the five years FY22–FY26, Niva Bupa has deployed capital almost entirely into organic franchise growth — building distribution (agents, banks, brokers), upgrading technology (Oracle Health core system, AI/GenAI), and absorbing the policyholder float needed to support a 27–35% annual GWP growth rate. There have been zero acquisitions, zero buybacks, and zero dividends. The IPO in November 2024 was a capital-raising event (dilutive to existing shareholders) whose proceeds correctly flowed into regulatory solvency buffers rather than management's discretionary pockets. Capital deployment has been appropriate to the business's stage: a growth-phase pure-play insurer that needs to build scale before it can cross a 99% combined ratio and generate sustainable free cash.

The best use of capital has been **organic distribution and technology investment**, which has produced measurable output: retail market share up from 9.1% (FY24) to 10.1% (FY26), EOM ratio down 550 bps in one year, and IFRS PAT roughly doubling each year for three years. The worst outcome — though not a misallocation — is that the company has diluted shareholders via IPO primary issuance and likely via ESOP grants, with no offsetting buyback. The per-share outcome is still positive: diluted EPS rose from ₹0.65 (FY24) to ₹1.98 (FY26) despite the increased share count, meaning intrinsic per-share value appears to be growing faster than dilution.

**The single biggest capital-allocation signal** is the absence of M&A or diversification moves in a sector where promoters (Bupa Singapore, Fairfax-linked FIH) are sophisticated acquirers with the means to push acquisitions. The company has resisted that pressure and stayed pure-play. That is the most material positive capital-allocation signal.

**Verdict: mixed.** The organic deployment is value-creative in direction; the combined ratio is still above 100%, so new business written is not yet self-funding on an underwriting basis; the IPO dilution was necessary but the ESOP trajectory is unverified; and the largest unknown (related-party flows with Bupa Singapore on brand/technology/reinsurance) could quietly extract value that does not appear in any transcript metric.
