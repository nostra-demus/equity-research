**Reporting standard:** India GAAP (IRDAI insurance template — primary statutory accounts). ## Critical framing note: Insurance accounting is structurally different from industrial-company accounting

EBITDA (earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation — the profit generated before these costs) is not a standard metric for health insurers. Cash from operations (CFO) for a health insurer includes premium collections and claims payments — it is not the same as cash generated by operating a factory or a software business. The key operating metrics management and analysts use are the **combined ratio** (claims paid plus operating costs as a percentage of premium earned — below 100% means the underwriting is profitable) and the **IFRS profit after tax**. This note applies throughout every section below.

## 2. Cash Conversion Assessment

CFO has consistently exceeded EBITDA every year from FY22 to FY26. The CFO/EBITDA ratios range from 180% (FY26) to 1,219% (FY23). **For a health insurer, this is structurally expected, not a quality red flag.** Health insurers collect premiums upfront and pay claims with a lag; the operating cash inflow naturally exceeds EBIT-based profits. The industrial-company benchmark of "CFO/EBITDA above 70% = healthy" does not apply here.

The more meaningful question is whether CFO is stable relative to gross written premium (GWP). GWP grew from ₹21.7 bn (FY22) to ₹94.3 bn (FY26, i.e. ₹9,433 crores per Q4 FY26 call), implying CFO as a percentage of GWP is approximately 10% in FY26 (₹9,287 mn / ₹94,330 mn). FY25 was anomalously high (approximately 22% of GWP) due to the 1/N transition inflow. FY26 normalises back toward FY24 levels. This is consistent with a growing insurer where a portion of collected premium must be held as reserve, and the reserve build grows with the book. No deterioration in the underlying cash generation is evident once the 1/N one-off is stripped out.

**No cash flow data is missing for the purpose of this bridge — the bridge is populated from vendor-tier data. However, the full IRDAI statutory annual report (primary filing) is not in the data pool, so the individual working capital line items (change in receivables, change in payables, reserve movements) cannot be verified against the primary source. This caps the working capital section below.**

## 3. Working Capital Trends

**Material limitation:** DSO, DIO, and DPO as conventionally defined are not applicable to a health insurer. A health insurer does not have trade receivables in the normal sense (premiums are collected before cover is granted), does not carry inventory, and has payables that consist of outstanding claims reserves and amounts due to reinsurers rather than supplier payables. Computing standard working capital days would produce misleading ratios.

The relevant insurance-specific working capital metrics are:
- Outstanding claims reserve as a % of earned premium (IBNR adequacy)
- Unearned premium reserve (UPR) trend
- Reinsurance recoverable trend

These are not separately available in the Capital IQ export. The primary annual report notes would be required. Not proven from available data.

## 9. Earnings Quality Score

**Score: 62 / 100**

Band: Mostly clean but some working capital or adjustment noise (61–80).

**Reason for score:** The company's earnings are genuinely backed by cash — it collects premiums before paying claims, the solvency ratio is strong (2.49x vs 1.5x regulatory minimum), and investment assets are low-risk (predominantly debt). The IFRS PAT of ₹3,660 mn in FY26 broadly reconciles to the IGAAP PAT of ₹3,661 mn at the full-year level, confirming that the 1/N accounting creates timing noise but not a full-year gap. Management is transparent about the dual-basis situation and consistently provides IFRS comparatives on calls.

The score is held to 62 rather than higher for three reasons:
1. The FY25 CFO spike (₹16,753 mn) is a genuine accounting inflow that does not repeat — anyone relying on FY25 as a run-rate would dramatically overstate the company's recurring cash generation.
2. The FY26 intangible capex jump (₹430 mn, +343% YoY) is not yet verifiable against primary filings and needs audited annual report confirmation.
3. The primary annual report (IRDAI statutory filing) is not in the data pool — working capital components, individual cash flow line items, revenue recognition policy details, and tax disclosures cannot be verified at source. This is a data limitation that caps confidence.

## 10. The Single Biggest Quality Concern

The single biggest risk that reported earnings overstate economic reality is the **FY25 CFO/FCF spike from the 1/N accounting transition.** Reported CFO in FY25 was ₹16,753 mn and FCF was ₹16,343 mn — roughly double the FY24 and FY26 levels. This surge was driven by the IRDAI-mandated change in premium recognition (the "1/N basis"), which required first-year premiums to be spread over the policy term rather than recognised upfront. The transition created a one-time cash inflow as the associated reserve built up, pulling forward cash that would otherwise arrive over multiple policy years. Anyone anchoring to FY25 as the normal free cash flow run-rate — for example, in a discounted cash flow or for dividend capacity — would overstate recurring cash generation by approximately ₹7,500–8,000 mn (roughly 90% above FY26's normalised FCF of ₹8,801 mn). FY26 FCF of ₹8,801 mn is the correct anchor for assessing recurring cash generation. Management explicitly confirmed this normalisation: the CFO decline in FY26 is entirely attributable to the one-time build-up not repeating, not to any deterioration in operating performance.

A secondary but forward-looking concern is the IFRS 17 transition effective April 2026 (FY27). This will be the third material accounting standard change in three years for Niva Bupa. While management says IFRS 17 is directionally positive (the company has voluntarily used IFRS for three years), it may create another period of comparability difficulty. The synthesis layer should flag this when assessing FY27 reported numbers.
