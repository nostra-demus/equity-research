# Business Identity — NIVABUPA

## 1. What The Company Actually Does

Niva Bupa Health Insurance Company Limited is an Indian standalone health insurer (SAHI) listed on the NSE and BSE, regulated by IRDAI. It sells health insurance policies — indemnity and benefit products — to individuals, families, and employer groups across India. Retail customers (individuals and families) buy directly or through agents, bank branches, and digital platforms; corporate customers buy group health policies to cover their employees. Customers pay an annual or multi-year premium so that, when they are hospitalised or face a covered medical event, the insurer pays the hospital bill directly (cashless) or reimburses them. The problem customers pay to solve is the unpredictable, and rapidly rising, cost of hospitalisation in India — a single serious illness or surgery can run into several lakhs or crores of rupees, which most households cannot absorb without insurance. As of FY2026, the company had a retail health market share of 10.1% and a gross written premium (GWP) of INR 9,433 crores, growing 27.4% year-on-year.

## 2. How The Company Makes Money

**Primary revenue line — health insurance premiums:**

`Revenue = policies in force × average ticket size (premium per policy)`

Breaking this down:

- **Retail health (approximately 67% of GWP):** Individual and family floater plans sold via agents, bancassurance, brokers, and direct/digital channels.
  - `Retail GWP = lives covered (retail) × average premium per life`
  - The company reported retail GWP of INR 6,582 crores in FY2026 (implied from total GWP of INR 9,433 crores and the 35% retail growth figure cited by management).
  - Average ticket size grew approximately 14–14.5% in the second half of FY2026 versus the first half, driven partly by GST reform making products cheaper and thereby raising affordability and volume, and partly by rider attachment.

- **Group health (approximately 31% of GWP):** Corporate/employer group policies. These renew annually and are more price-sensitive. Group loss ratio runs around 60.5% for FY2026, tighter than retail (~66.8%) because group contracts are more homogeneous. Group business is roughly split one-third corporate indemnity and two-thirds bancassurance-based group health.

- **Travel and personal accident (approximately 2% of GWP):** Small, not the core.

**Investment income — the float:**

`Investment income = AUM × annualised yield`

Premiums are collected upfront and claims are paid out over the policy year. The float — money held between premium receipt and claim payment — is invested in fixed-income and other regulated instruments. AUM was INR 9,670 crores at March 2026, earning an annualised yield of 7.2%. Investment income is an important contributor to overall profitability and is why the combined ratio (claims + expenses as a fraction of premiums) can exceed 100% without the business losing money overall.

**What drives volume, price, and margin:**

Volume is driven by lives added through the distribution network — more agents, more bank branches, and more digital acquisition. The company added a net 58,000 agents in FY2026, now reaching every pin code via a virtual agency for Tier 3 cities and below. Price per policy is driven by actuarial repricing at renewal (the company targets high-single-digit price revisions on mature products), product mix shift (higher-value plans carry larger premiums), and GST pass-through on coverage amounts. Margin — measured by the expense of management (EOM) ratio and the combined ratio — is primarily driven by operating leverage (GWP growing at 27% while fixed overhead grows at 6–7% inflation) and mix (renewal business carries much lower acquisition cost than new business). The combined ratio improved 160 basis points to 101.4% under IFRS in FY2026; management targets approximately 99% by FY2029 at a mid-to-high-teens ROE.

## 3. Business Type Classification

Growth-stage Indian standalone health insurer scaling volume on a retail-dominated book, with operating leverage driving the path from combined-ratio losses to profitability, in a low-penetration market with policy-driven tailwinds.

## 4. What Drives Variance

When Niva Bupa's revenue or margins move, the most likely cause is: (1) **volume** — how many new lives are added through the distribution network each quarter, which is sensitive to channel expansion (agents, bancassurance), GST reform pass-through effects, and industry awareness campaigns; (2) **loss ratio** — driven by claims frequency and severity, particularly post-monsoon infection spikes (a seasonal pattern explicitly discussed by management), by the mix between new business (higher claims early on) and renewal business (lower claims), and by any shift in group health mix toward or away from corporate indemnity; (3) **EOM / expense ratio** — operating leverage means the ratio improves as GWP grows faster than overhead, but slows if new headcount or distribution investment accelerates; and (4) **investment income** — a secondary but real driver, sensitive to AUM scale (which grows with the premium base) and to fixed-income yields in India.
