# Unit Economics — NIVABUPA

## 1. Natural Unit

The natural economic unit for Retail Health Insurance (the dominant segment at ~70% of GWP) is the retail health insurance policy — meaning one policy covering one individual or family, typically renewed annually.

Secondary unit types exist: the group health policy (employer-employee and bancassurance) accounts for the remaining ~30% of GWP. Group policies are written per account rather than per individual life, so they have a different unit structure and are addressed briefly where data allows.

## 3. Value Creation Read

**Verdict: Creates value, with a key caveat that current returns are below the cost of capital on an IFRS basis, and the thesis is path-dependent on continued operating leverage.**

The retail health policy creates value through the renewal flywheel: a new policy is acquired at a combined ratio materially above 100% (fresh business commission ~20–23% of premium, plus claims at ~66–68%, plus overheads, implies combined ratio well above 100% at inception). Once the policy renews, acquisition cost drops sharply — management says renewal has "much lower cost, both cost of acquisition and cost of administering policies" — and the renewal book runs at ~97–98% combined ratio. With ~60% of retail GWP from renewals and a claimed incidence rate below the 7–7.5% steady state (the book is still young), the maturing cohort structure creates a structural tailwind to margins over the medium term.

Investment income closes the gap. The underwriting combined ratio for the whole company is 101.4% IFRS in FY26, meaning underwriting alone loses ~1.4 cents per rupee of earned premium. But the float (₹9,670 crores in debt instruments at 7.2% yield) generates ~₹696 crores of investment income annually, turning an underwriting loss into a company-level PAT of ₹366 crores (Ind AS) for FY26. This is the standard health insurance model: the unit economics only fully close when investment income is included.

The combined-ratio trajectory — from 103.0% in FY25 to 101.4% in FY26, and management guidance for ~99% by FY29 — plus the ROE expansion from 10.7% to a mid-to-high teens target by FY29 is the quantified path to value creation at the unit level. At 10.7% FY26 ROE, the current return likely sits around the company's cost of equity (inference, not from filings). The unit economics are therefore value-creating directionally but not yet decisively above the cost of capital, and this read should not be treated as a stable-state result.

The single most valuable disclosure missing: a per-cohort (vintage-year) combined ratio or retention rate. Without it, the renewal-flywheel thesis rests on management commentary rather than demonstrated cohort data.

## 4. Sensitivity

The input that would most change the value-creation read, if it moved 20%, is the **retail loss ratio** (claims as a share of earned premium). At the FY26 level of 66.8%, every 100-basis-point move in the retail loss ratio changes the combined ratio by roughly 100 bps and — given retail GWP of ₹6,582 crores — shifts underwriting profit or loss by approximately ₹66 crores, a material fraction of total PAT (₹366 crores Ind AS FY26). A 20% relative increase in the retail loss ratio (from 66.8% to ~80%) would by itself eliminate the entire FY26 profit.

Historically, the retail loss ratio has been range-bound at 65–68% on IFRS over the three years of available transcript data, with the primary driver of movement being mix (fresh vs. renewal) and seasonal claims patterns (post-monsoon infection spikes). Medical/claims inflation at 5% per year has been stable and well below the ~10% price increases the company has been able to take annually, providing a positive pricing buffer. However, if medical inflation reverts to peer-level double digits (some competitors report 10%+ claims inflation against Niva Bupa's 5%), the claims ratio would deteriorate and the path to a 99% combined ratio by FY29 would be at risk. Commission regulation (the IRDAI's evolving EOM framework) is the second most historically volatile input: the senior-trail commission cut from 15–20% to 10% in FY26 was an exogenous regulatory event that moved the commission line materially in a single quarter [Q3 FY26 call, p.8–9]. A further regulatory tightening or a reversal of the GST-pass-through arrangement with distributors could re-inflate acquisition cost unpredictably.
