## 2. Dominant Segment

Retail Health Insurance is the dominant segment. It accounts for approximately 70% of gross written premium and is the growth engine management tracks most closely — retail market share rose from 9.1% in FY24 to 10.1% in FY26 on a full-year basis. [Q4 FY26 call, May 8, 2026, p.5 — CEO: "retail market share... closed at 10.1% on a full year basis, up from 9.4%."]

Because segment-level profit is not disclosed, the dominance call is based on revenue. However, there is a directional profit argument too: retail carries structurally lower acquisition cost per renewal, benefits from operating leverage as the renewal book grows (the CFO explicitly cited renewal cost as a key lever for expense ratio improvement [Q4 FY26 call, p.11]), and management has said it deliberately slows group business when pricing does not support economics [Q3 FY26 call, Jan 29, 2026, p.10 — CEO: "group business is only growing at 12%... materially slower than the company's overall growth rate"]. The investment float is large (₹9,670 crores AUM) but it is a by-product of the underwriting business, not a standalone segment; the CFO treats it as a supporting income stream [Q4 FY26 call, p.5].

The company is effectively a two-product insurer: retail health drives scale and margin improvement, group health provides volume and institutional relationships but is managed selectively.

## 3. Segment Disclosure Quality

**Consistency year-over-year:** The retail / group distinction has been used consistently across all six quarters of transcripts reviewed (FY25 Q4 through FY26 Q4). The labelling is stable. No segment was added or removed.

**"Other" / residual bucket:** The company also writes personal accident and travel insurance. These are small and are grouped with group or mentioned as a residual "balance" in management commentary; no size is disclosed in the available data. [Q4 FY25 call, May 7, 2025, p.4 — CEO: "65.5% retail and the balance split between group and PA and travel."] This residual is almost certainly below 5% of GWP and does not distort the retail/group read materially, but an exact figure is not available.

**Profit metrics at segment level:** Not disclosed anywhere in the available data pool. Management shares segment-level GWP, loss ratios (retail and group separately), and occasionally commission rates, but there is no segment EBIT, segment PAT, or segment contribution margin published in any filing or transcript reviewed. This is a material gap for unit-economics and competitive-map agents. The combined ratio is reported at the company level (101.4% IFRS FY26 [Q4 FY26 call, p.5]) and retail loss ratios are shared verbally (66.8% for FY26 [Q4 FY26 call, p.9]), but expense allocation between retail and group is not broken out. The CFO noted that claim management expenses are allocated "in the proportion of claims" between retail and group [Q4 FY26 call, p.13], which confirms there is an internal allocation, but it is not published.

**Segment definition changes in the last three years:** Under IRDAI regulations, health insurers operate under a 1/N (1-day/365-day) accounting change that took effect October 1, 2024, which significantly altered how premium is earned and reported. The company simultaneously migrated its primary external reporting to IFRS 17 (effective April 2026). These are accounting standard changes, not segment definition changes. The retail / group split itself has not changed. However, comparisons of absolute GWP or loss ratios across FY24, FY25, and FY26 require care because the 1/N change shifts when premium is earned; management consistently uses "like-to-like" (excluding 1/N impact) for growth rate disclosures. [Q4 FY25 call, May 7, 2025, p.4 — CEO: "I will refer to numbers on a like-to-like basis."] Downstream agents (unit-economics, competitive-map) should note this accounting complexity and use IFRS figures where possible for consistency.
