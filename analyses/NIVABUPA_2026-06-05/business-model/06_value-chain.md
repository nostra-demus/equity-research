## 2. Input Cost Pass-Through

Niva Bupa's primary "input cost" is claims incurred — the reimbursement paid to hospitals for treatment of policyholders. There are no raw material inputs or physical supply chains. Hospital billing constitutes the core cost driver. The company does not have contractual indexed pass-throughs of medical inflation to policyholders in the standard sense; instead, it passes rising claim costs through annual actuarial premium revisions at renewal. Management disclosed that the company executed high-single-digit price revisions on mature products — a 7% increase on ReAssure 2.0 in Q1 FY26 was explicitly cited, and the portfolio-wide approach is to "negate overall medical trends" through annual premium revisions [Q1 FY26 transcript, prepared remarks, Jul 31, 2025; Q4 FY25 transcript, Q&A, May 7, 2025]. Medical inflation as measured by average claim size has been running at 5–6.5% per year CAGR on the portfolio [Q4 FY25 transcript, prepared remarks, May 7, 2025]. The lag between inflation incurrence and premium revision is one renewal cycle (annual), meaning the company absorbs medical inflation for up to twelve months before recovering it through price. For the distribution cost input, GST savings from the government's 0% GST policy on health insurance were passed entirely to distributors in Q2 FY26, meaning the company did not retain those savings to offset its own distribution costs [Q2 FY26 transcript, Q&A, Nov 3, 2025]. There are no disclosed long-term fixed-price hospital contracts; tariff negotiations with hospitals are ongoing and subject to the industry common empanelment initiative, which as of Q4 FY25 was still meeting pushback from hospital networks [Q4 FY25 transcript, Q&A, May 7, 2025].

## 3. Customer Pricing Power

Niva Bupa has demonstrated moderate pricing power in its retail segment. The company executed a 7% premium increase on ReAssure 2.0 in Q1 FY26 with no disclosed material volume impact [Q1 FY26 transcript, prepared remarks, Jul 31, 2025]. Management consistently describes the approach as annual actuarial repricing on mature products to cover medical trends, with historical portfolio-level medical inflation of 5–7% per annum used as the floor for pricing actions [Q4 FY25 transcript, Q&A, May 7, 2025]. However, pricing power is constrained on two sides. First, the GST reduction in H2 FY26 effectively lowered end-prices by 18%, and management explicitly chose not to take a price hike in Q2 FY26, stating the volume uplift was sufficient and monitoring would occur before any revision [Q2 FY26 transcript, Q&A, Nov 3, 2025]. Second, the corporate/group segment is explicitly described as a market where "pricing levels fluctuate" and where the company writes business only "where pricing makes economic sense," signalling it is a price-follower rather than a price-setter in group [Q1 FY26 transcript, Q&A, Jul 31, 2025]. The retail book renewal mix is approximately 60% of retail GWP, providing a recurring pricing opportunity each year. Volume growth has been positive following price actions — Q3 FY26 volume growth was 29% and ticket size (sum insured) rose 15% following the GST change [Q3 FY26 transcript, prepared remarks, Jan 29, 2026]. There is no evidence that price increases have triggered measurable lapse-driven volume losses in the retail book; retention appears stable.

---

## 4. Economic Control Verdict

**Mixed**

Niva Bupa controls its retail pricing cycle: it sets annual renewal premiums using actuarial models and has executed documented price increases without volume loss. On the downstream side this gives it moderate economic control over retail customers. However, on the upstream side it is a price-taker at the hospital level — hospitals remain the single largest cost input, the industry common empanelment initiative to set common rates is still meeting pushback, and the company's PPN network covers only 20% of claims as of FY26 Q4, meaning the majority of claim costs are settled at non-negotiated rates. The IRDAI EOM cap is a hard regulatory ceiling that limits how much the company can spend on distribution or operations regardless of competitive dynamics, further constraining economic degrees of freedom. The company is therefore strong on one side (retail premium setting) and materially weaker on the other (hospital claim cost).

---

## 5. The Single Biggest Bargaining Risk

**Hospital tariff — if major hospital networks successfully resist the industry common empanelment initiative and continue to push through medical inflation above the company's actuarial assumptions, the loss ratio will deteriorate faster than annual premium revisions can offset, directly compressing underwriting margins and pushing the combined ratio above the 99% FY29 target.**
