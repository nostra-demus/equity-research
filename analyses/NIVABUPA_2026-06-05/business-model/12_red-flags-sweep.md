## 3. Most Severe New Flag

The IT system suspension and claims SLA failure (Q1 FY26) is the most material new flag. It is the only flag in this sweep that has already materialised as an operational event — not a forward risk or a structural constraint. For a health insurer, the claims auto-adjudication engine is the production-critical process: it handles the "moment of truth" for every cashless policyholder in a hospital. Suspending it for an entire quarter — due to an information security review of third-party vendors — caused measurable SLA deterioration (cashless approval rate: 90% → 76%), forced prudent reserve strengthening, and compressed reported Q1 profitability. What is not disclosed is which vendor was implicated, whether IRDAI was notified, what remediation controls were put in place, or whether the same third party continues to support the platform. Management confirmed the system restarted in Q2 (July 2025) and framed it as resolved, but no audit trail, vendor disclosure, or root-cause explanation is available in the data pool. The synthesizer should weight this flag because it reveals an unquantified cyber/operational dependency on undisclosed third parties, and the episode took place within the IPO window — a period when public scrutiny and investor reliance on operational continuity metrics is highest.

---

## 4. Cross-Cutting Patterns

Two patterns, taken together, are more concerning than either individually. First, the combination of an IT system failure (Q1 FY26) and the reinsurance recoverable spike (2.6x, counterparty unidentified) both represent opaque third-party dependencies where the company's disclosed risk management appears to lag the scale of exposure. Second, the persistent consensus EPS misses in the first three post-IPO quarters, alongside a management guidance style that consistently emphasises positive developments (NPS, market share, EOM glide path) while leaving quantified downside scenarios to analyst inference, suggests a pattern where disclosed numbers routinely fall short of the market model. Neither of these items is a standalone disqualifier, but the combination of IT concentration risk, counterparty credit opacity, and systematic earnings underdelivery relative to buy-side forecasts is a pattern the synthesizer should not average away.
