# Unit Economics — NIVABUPA

## 1. Natural Unit

The natural economic unit for Retail Health Insurance (the dominant segment, ~70% of FY26 GWP) is the retail health insurance policy (one policy = one unit of analysis).

Secondary unit types present in the business: group health policy (covering employer-employee and SME cohorts) and the float dollar (investment income on the INR 9,670 crores AUM). The table below is built for the retail policy unit. Key differences for the group unit are noted inline where material.

## 3. Value Creation Read

**Verdict: Creates value — but with a structural qualification on the float dependency.**

At the renewal unit level, the math is clearly positive: a renewal policy runs at a combined ratio of ~97–98% (management-disclosed), meaning claims plus all costs are covered by the premium alone, with the float yield adding on top. This is the unit the business is scaling into: 60% of FY26 GWP is renewals, and the mix has been stable or improving.

At the fresh-policy level, the unit is loss-making on an underwriting basis before investment income, consistent with Indian health insurance industry norms. The combined ratio at entity level (101.4% FY26) is above 100%, and the business converts to PAT-positive only when investment income (~INR 697 crores gross on a INR 9,670 crore float) is included. This structure is not a red flag by itself — it mirrors the industry and is the structural model for Indian health insurers — but it means value creation depends on (a) maintaining the float yield through a rate cycle and (b) enough renewal volume to offset fresh-policy acquisition losses.

The single most valuable disclosure missing is a per-policy INR acquisition cost figure (CAC) for new retail business vs renewal, which would allow a precise payback period calculation. Management confirms a lifetime-value framework is used internally and that renewal cost is "much lower" than fresh, but the quantification is not in the public record.

## 4. Sensitivity

The input that would most change the value-creation read if it moved 20% is the **expense of management (EOM) ratio**. A 20% deterioration from 33.7% would push EOM to ~40.4%, which would exceed the IRDAI-prescribed allowable limit (~36%) and would structurally impair the economics of fresh-business acquisition — effectively capping growth. Conversely, a 20% improvement (EOM to ~27%) would substantially increase the contribution margin per unit and could compress payback from the current estimated 2–3 years on fresh business. EOM has historically been the most volatile input in Niva Bupa's unit economics, declining ~750 bps over three years (FY23: 41.2% to FY26: 33.7%), and management's stated steady-state target is ~99% combined ratio by FY29 — which implies further EOM improvement is both planned and load-bearing. The second most sensitive input is the **medical claims inflation rate**: management reports average claim size (ACS) growing at ~5–6.5% CAGR; a step-up to 10%+ (consistent with competitor disclosures) would push the retail loss ratio from ~66.8% toward 70–71%, compressing underwriting margin and requiring premium price revisions that may not fully offset in a regulated pricing environment. Of the two, EOM has been the more volatile historically (750 bps swing over 3 years); medical inflation has been relatively contained in the Niva Bupa book vs peers, though the sustainability of that outperformance is not fully explained from available data.
