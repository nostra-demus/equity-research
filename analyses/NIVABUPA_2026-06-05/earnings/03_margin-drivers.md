This is effectively a **retail-dominant** business: retail at ~70% of GWP is the primary economic engine. The company is not single-segment by the strict >85% threshold, so both retail and group driver tables are presented.

## 3. Gross Margin → EBITDA Margin → EBIT Margin Walk

**Important accounting note:** Standard CIQ "gross margin" is not analytically meaningful for an insurer due to template reclassifications (see historical-financials note [13]). The relevant margin hierarchy for Niva Bupa is: **Loss Ratio → Combined Ratio → EBITDA Margin → PAT Margin**.

|---|---:|---:|---:|---|---|
| IFRS Loss Ratio (blended) | ~65% (est.) | ~63.8% | +~120 bps adverse | Group segment mix shift toward higher-loss-ratio corporate accounts; partially offset by Q3-Q4 retail improvement | Q4 FY25 transcript (CFO: FY25 IFRS overall loss ratio 63.8%); Q4 FY26 transcript, Q&A (analyst cites net claims ratio 59.4% → 61.3%, p.13); retail loss ratio 66.8% vs 66% prior year |
| IFRS Combined Ratio | 101.4% | ~103.0% | −160 bps favourable | EOM improvement (−270 bps) more than offset loss ratio increase (+110 bps); management states: "increase in overall loss ratio by 1.1%, primarily due to mix change, offset by reduction in expense ratio by 2.7%" | Q4 FY26 transcript, prepared remarks (CFO, p.5) |
| EBITDA Margin (CIQ/IFRS) | 6.1% | 5.1% | +100 bps | EOM leverage + investment income growth | Capital IQ Financials, FY26 EBITDA 5,148 / Total Revenue 84,435 = 6.1%; FY25 3,239 / 63,303 = 5.1% |
| EBIT Margin (CIQ/IFRS) | 5.9% | 4.9% | +100 bps | Same as EBITDA; D&A (133 mn) is immaterial | Capital IQ Financials, FY26 EBIT 5,015 / 84,435 = 5.9% |
| PAT Margin (CIQ/IFRS) | 4.3% | 3.2% | +110 bps | Operating leverage on EOM; investment income growth; PAT up 80% | Capital IQ Financials, FY26 PAT 3,661 / 84,435 = 4.3%; Q4 FY26 transcript (CEO: "profit grew 80%") |

**Pass-through lag:** Medical inflation (claims cost inflation) is the primary input cost. The company passes medical inflation through annual premium revisions at each policy renewal. The lag is one renewal cycle — up to 12 months. Management executed a 7% price increase on ReAssure 2.0 in Q1 FY26 [Q1 FY26 transcript, prepared remarks, CEO, Jul 31, 2025]. If medical inflation spikes, the company absorbs it for up to one full year before recovery at the next renewal. Historical medical inflation: 5–6.5% CAGR on average claim size (ACS) [Q4 FY25 transcript, prepared remarks, CEO, May 7, 2025].

## 4. Margin Walk — Which Margin Level Matters Most?

For Niva Bupa, **the combined ratio (IFRS) is the primary margin metric**. This is because the business model is a pure-play health insurer: the "income statement" is dominated by claims paid vs premiums earned, and the economic question is whether premiums (plus investment income) cover claims and expenses. EBITDA and EBIT margins as computed by CIQ are secondary and derivative — they capture operating leverage but the underlying economics are better read through the combined ratio and its two components (loss ratio + expense ratio).

Investment income is a critical complement: the company currently operates with a combined ratio above 100% (101.4% in FY26), meaning it runs a technical underwriting loss at entity level. The INR 6,190 mn of investment income (FY26) is what converts the underwriting loss into a PAT of INR 3,661 mn. This is structurally common across Indian health insurers and is not a distress signal, but it means PAT margin is doubly sensitive — to combined ratio direction AND to investment yield. An investor tracking Niva Bupa should monitor (a) the combined ratio trajectory toward management's 99% FY29 target, and (b) the AUM yield.

SG&A leverage is embedded in the EOM ratio for this business (insurers do not have a separate "SG&A" line that is separable from commissions + operating expenses within EOM). R&D in the conventional sense does not exist; technology investment is captured within operating expenses inside EOM.

## 8. The Single Biggest Margin Driver

**The Expense of Management (EOM) ratio improvement is the single most powerful current margin driver — and its deceleration is the most important risk to the margin story.**

Over three years, EOM fell from 41.2% (FY23) to 33.7% (FY26), a 750 basis point improvement. Each 100 bps fall in EOM translates directly into 100 bps of combined ratio improvement (and, with investment income cover, into proportionally higher PAT growth). This improvement has been driven by three levers that management has explicitly described: (a) new-vs-renewal mix improving as the renewal book scales (renewal policies are much cheaper to administer and acquire), (b) sheer economies of scale where GWP grows 22–27% while fixed overheads grow at 6–7% inflation, and (c) technology investment in automation and AI reducing unit processing costs [Q4 FY26, CFO, Q&A, p.11].

The risk is that this improvement is approaching a natural floor. The allowable EOM regulatory ceiling including additional allowances is ~36%; Niva Bupa is already at 33.7% — 230 bps below the ceiling. Management's FY29 target is a combined ratio of ~99%, which implies a loss ratio that "may inch up by 100–150 bps" from current levels and the remaining improvement coming entirely from further expense ratio reductions [Q4 FY26, CFO Q&A, p.7]. If GWP growth decelerates to the 17–19% industry CAGR that management projects for the long run [Q4 FY26, CEO, Q&A, p.8], the operating leverage from scale shrinks, the pace of EOM improvement slows materially, and the combined ratio glide path toward 99% relies more heavily on the loss ratio being contained — which, as this analysis documents, faces the independent structural headwind of medical inflation and group mix change.

In short: EOM leverage is the tailwind that has powered the margin expansion of the last three years. The direction is still favourable, but the magnitude of further improvement is decelerating. Whether margins continue to expand in FY27 will depend primarily on whether management can hold EOM improvement even as GWP growth slows.
