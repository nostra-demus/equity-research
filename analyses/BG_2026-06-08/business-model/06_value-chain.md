## 2. Input Cost Pass-Through

Bunge does not hold unhedged commodity price exposure — it hedges its raw material inventory using exchange-traded futures and options, so it is the spread (crush spread for oilseeds; merchandising margin for grains) rather than the outright commodity price that flows through to profit. This means rising soybean prices do not automatically hurt Bunge's earnings, but they do increase working capital requirements, as the 10-K notes: "increases in agricultural commodity prices will also generally cause our cash flow requirements to increase as our operations require increased use of cash and associated borrowings to acquire inventories" [FY2025 10-K, MD&A Overview, p.34]. For energy and freight inputs — the two non-commodity variable costs — the 10-K states directly that freight supply agreement costs are "typically passed through to our customers as a component of the prices we charge for our products," though "changes in the market value of such freight services compared to the rates at which we have contracted them may affect margins" [FY2025 10-K, contractual obligations note, p.56]. The Q1 2026 transcript illustrates the lag risk: a spike in bunker fuel costs in Q1 2026 squeezed grain merchandising results before the pass-through could fully offset it [Q1 2026 transcript, April 29, 2026, prepared remarks]. For broader inflation (labor, overhead), Bunge acknowledges uncertainty explicitly: "we have historically been able to recover the impacts of inflation through sales price increases, however we cannot reasonably estimate our ability to successfully recover any impact of inflation through price increases in the future" [FY2025 10-K, Inflation Risk, p.55]. In summary: commodity exposure is hedged rather than passed through; freight costs have contractual pass-through with a short-term lag risk; general inflation recovery is uncertain and not contractually guaranteed.

## 3. Customer Pricing Power

Bunge does not exercise pricing power over its customers. Its core businesses — oilseed processing and grain merchandising — sell into global commodity markets where price is set by exchange-traded reference points and where multiple large competitors (ADM, Cargill, Louis Dreyfus, Wilmar, COFCO) sell equivalent products. The 10-K states repeatedly that competition in each segment "is principally based on price" [FY2025 10-K, Item 1, pp.3–6]. There is no disclosure of any price increase imposed on customers in the last 24 months; the disclosed earnings improvement in FY2025 and Q1 2026 was driven by higher crush spread margins (market-set, not company-initiated) and by volume growth from the Viterra acquisition, not by Bunge raising prices above market. The company's own risk disclosure confirms that competitive pressure "could cause us to... reduce pricing" [FY2025 10-K, Risk Factors, p.15]. The specialty and value-added oil business (Loders) offers modestly more stability — the CFO described refining premiums as "pretty resilient" in Q1 2026 [Q1 2026 transcript] — but even here, customers can switch suppliers, and Bunge does not claim brand-driven pricing authority. Bunge's revenue model is volume-times-market-price, not volume-times-Bunge-set-price.

## 4. Economic Control Verdict

**Squeezed**

Bunge is a price-taker on both sides of its core operations. On inputs, raw oilseed and grain prices are set by global markets and farmer/seller decisions; Bunge partially offsets this via farmer financing in Brazil, but that mechanism creates credit risk, not pricing power. On outputs, Bunge sells into globally competitive commodity markets where it is one of four or five large interchangeable processors and merchants; there is no evidence of sustained pricing authority above market. The crush spread — the core earnings driver — is entirely market-determined. The 10-K's own language describes "highly competitive" markets where "competition is principally based on price" for every segment. Inflation pass-through is explicitly disclosed as uncertain. The company earns a volume-scale logistics and processing margin, not a margin it can expand by raising prices.

---

## 5. The Single Biggest Bargaining Risk

The crush spread compressing — because Bunge cannot raise prices on meal or oil to offset rising oilseed input costs, any period where global processing capacity exceeds demand forces EBIT per ton sharply lower with no remedy on either side of the transaction.
