# Unit Economics — BG

## 1. Natural Unit

The natural economic unit for Soybean Processing and Refining (the dominant segment, 51.6% of FY2025 revenue and 52.6% of segment EBIT) is one metric ton of soybeans processed (crushed).

Secondary unit types present in the business: metric ton of softseeds processed (Softseed segment), metric ton sold for grain merchandising (Grain Merchandising and Milling), and metric ton of specialty oilseed products sold (Tropical Oils and Specialty Ingredients). The table below covers the dominant soybean processing unit exclusively.

## 3. Value Creation Read

**Unclear — marginally positive on adjusted metrics, marginally negative on GAAP metrics, with high cycle-driven volatility that makes a clean answer impossible.**

The contribution margin per ton of soybeans processed is positive and improving: $72/t in FY2025 versus $66/t in FY2024, meaning each incremental ton crushed covers its variable costs and makes a real contribution to covering fixed plant costs and generating profit. That is the basic condition for a unit to not destroy value in the near term. But the margin on the existing capital base is thin and unstable. EBIT per ton swung from $62/t in FY2023 to $24/t in FY2024 — a 61% decline in one year — and has only partially recovered to $30/t in FY2025. Q1 2026 data shows renewed pressure at $19/t. At the company level, GAAP ROIC (6.7%) sits just below the company's own cost of equity (7.2%), implying no economic value is being created on reported numbers. Adjusted ROIC (8.0%) is slightly above that threshold, but the adjustments are real costs — $223M of Viterra integration expenses in FY2025 alone — not accounting artefacts.

The single most valuable missing disclosure is the segment-level weighted-average cost of capital (WACC) applied to the soybean processing segment specifically. Without that number, it is impossible to confirm whether the 7.5% return on segment assets in FY2025 clears the hurdle. At the consolidated level, the company says ROIC exceeds cost of equity only on an adjusted basis. Until Viterra integration costs stop, that adjusted-versus-GAAP gap will persist.

## 4. Sensitivity

The input that would most change the value-creation read is the crush spread — the difference between what Bunge receives for soybean meal and oil versus what it pays for raw soybeans. This is the dominant driver of revenue per ton and of contribution margin per ton, since raw materials represent $801/t of the $885/t revenue figure in FY2025, leaving only $84/t of gross margin before fixed costs. A 20% improvement in the crush spread (holding raw material costs constant) would lift contribution margin from $72/t to roughly $249/t — a more than threefold increase — and would swing annual soybean segment EBIT from roughly $1.2B toward $3.0B. Conversely, a 20% compression in net price realisation (if raw material costs rise 20% while revenue stays flat) would make contribution margin deeply negative (-$88/t), destroying value at every ton processed.

Historically, crush margins have been the most volatile input: EBIT per ton moved by 61% in a single year (FY2023 to FY2024). Argentine weather and government policy (affecting farmer selling behaviour, export taxes, and FX access), global soybean supply and demand balances, and biofuel policy in the US, EU, Brazil, and Indonesia are the underlying drivers of that spread. Volume retention (capacity utilisation) is secondary in size of impact — a 20% increase in volumes processed would lift annual soybean EBIT by roughly 49% (holding contribution per ton constant) because fixed plant costs are absorbed over more units — but volume changes are constrained by rated capacity (58% utilised in FY2025), meaning there is room to grow throughput without new capital if crush margins recover.
