# Business Model Reality Check — BG (Synthesis)

## Abstract

Bunge Global SA is a global grain and oilseed merchant and processor, buying raw soybeans, canola, sunflower seed, and grains from farmers, then crushing or merchandising those commodities to food manufacturers, animal feed producers, and biofuel companies in more than 50 countries. Its soybean processing segment dominates, contributing 51.6% of FY2025 revenue ($36.3B) and 52.6% of segment EBIT ($1.2B). The strongest business-model positive is Bunge's position within a stable oligopoly — the same four ABCD players have controlled global grain and oilseed flows for decades, and the entry barriers (port terminals, crush plants, origination networks) are genuinely difficult to replicate. The single most damaging negative is that Bunge is a pure price-taker on both sides: raw material cost consumed 95.2% of FY2025 revenue, soybean segment EBIT collapsed 61% in one year (FY2023 to FY2024), and the bet-the-company Viterra acquisition left net leverage at roughly 5.8x EBITDA with negative free cash flow (cash from operations minus capex = -$879M). No disqualifier triggered, but the business is a commodity-cycle and biofuel-policy bet, not a durable compounder, and earns a verdict of Average business — worth deeper work only if valuation is cheap.

## 4. Note To The Final Synthesizer

**Strongest business-model positive:** Bunge participates in a genuine oligopoly with very high physical infrastructure barriers. Its post-Viterra global network — 35 soybean crush plants, 29 softseed plants, 46 port terminals, 298 grain storage facilities — cannot be replicated by a new entrant without a decade of capital investment and regulatory approvals. This protects Bunge from disruption in a way that an asset-light business model cannot claim. The stable competitive set means Bunge is still playing the same game a decade from now against the same three opponents — no technology threat, no new-entrant risk at scale. But this positive is shared by all ABCD incumbents; it does not produce differential returns above peers.

**Strongest business-model negative:** The business has no control over the single variable that matters most — the crush spread between what soybean oil and meal sell for versus what raw soybeans cost. In FY2024, that spread collapsed and took soybean segment EBIT down 61% in one year. At the same time, Bunge committed to a $10.6B acquisition that doubled its debt load, diluted shareholders 44%, and produced negative free cash flow (-$879M in FY2025). The company now services $14.1B in debt (~5.8x EBITDA) using operating cash flow that fell from $3.3B to $0.8B in two years — a 74% decline. If crush margins stay at FY2025 levels or compress further while the company absorbs Viterra integration costs and services elevated debt, the financial profile deteriorates materially.

**Most important segment:** Soybean Processing and Refining — 51.6% of revenue, 52.6% of segment EBIT. Every downstream analysis of Bunge should start and finish here. The soybean crush spread is the single most important external variable; everything else is secondary.

**Cleanest unit-economics read:** The EBIT-per-ton derivation for soybean processing is the cleanest available metric ($30/t in FY2025, down from $62/t at the FY2023 peak, $24/t in FY2024, $19/t in Q1 2026). It is derived from disclosed segment financials and clearly shows how much earnings power exists per unit of throughput across the cycle. The unresolved question is whether this margin level clears a cost of capital hurdle — GAAP ROIC of 6.7% sits just below the company's stated cost of equity (7.2%), meaning the business as constituted creates no economic value on a reported basis.

**Where Bunge sits vs named peers on margin / ROIC:** Bunge's FY2025 gross margin of 4.85% is below ADM's estimated 6.3% (unverified web source) — the lowest available in the named-peer comparison. Both companies suffered the same industrywide crush-margin compression. Cargill and LDC do not disclose audited margins. ROIC for Bunge (~3.6% on an independently derived basis) compares unfavorably to its own FY2023 peak (~12%), illustrating the cycle's severity. In an oligopoly where all players face identical commodity-market conditions, Bunge's lower margin versus ADM in FY2025 may reflect the absorption of Viterra integration costs rather than a permanent structural disadvantage — but this cannot be confirmed without a post-integration clean year.

**Main external dependency:** Agricultural commodity crush margins — set by global supply and demand for soybean meal and vegetable oil — are the primary earnings driver. Government biofuel mandates (US RVO, EU RED, Indonesia, Brazil) constitute the second most important external variable because they set the demand side of the vegetable oil market. Together, these two externally determined factors account for the great majority of Bunge's earnings variance. Weather in South America and trade policy (US-China tariffs, Argentine export taxes) are the third and fourth most material external forces. No combination of management actions can reliably offset a simultaneous compression in crush margins plus a biofuel policy reversal.

**Most important capital allocation or governance signal:** The Viterra acquisition is the dominant signal — a $10.6B deal that exceeded Bunge's pre-deal equity market cap, doubled total debt, diluted shares 44%, and has produced thin goodwill impairment headroom (9% cushion on $593M of Grain Merchandising goodwill) just four months after close. This is the serial-acquirer pattern that CLAUDE.md §24 Filter 4 targets. The acquisition itself is not necessarily value-destructive — Viterra's origination assets genuinely extend Bunge's network — but the price paid and the leverage taken on leave almost no margin for error in the next two years. Separately, the Viterra ICFR exclusion from the FY2025 assessment means 33% of Bunge's consolidated assets have not yet been subject to a complete internal controls review. Clean governance hygiene (stable auditors, no restatements, experienced management team) is genuine, but it is offset by the capital-structure risk concentration.

**Whether any automatic disqualifier triggered:** None of the eight hard disqualifiers triggered. The verdict is not locked.

**Which rejector filters (§24) tripped and the cap each applied:**
- Filter 1 (crooks/integrity): Not tripped. No fraud evidence, no integrity adverse "buzz."
- Filter 4 (serial acquirers): **Tripped.** Acquisition-pattern severity scored 72 (above 70 threshold). Capital allocation & governance score capped at 50/100 (reported 42/100, below the cap). Overall usefulness capped at 70/100 (reported 58/100, below the cap due to additional business-quality and external-dependency weakness).
- Filter 5 (fast-changing industry): Not tripped. Industry rate-of-change score of 60 is above the ≤40 trigger level.

**Biggest missing data point:** Per-ton soybean crush economics broken down between processing margin (farm-to-crush output) and refining/hedging margin — disclosed only in aggregate in Note 26, not separately. Without this breakdown, it is impossible to confirm whether Bunge's origination infrastructure advantage (South American origination density, Brazilian farmer financing) produces a measurable per-ton cost advantage versus ADM or Cargill. This one disclosure would either confirm or refute the infrastructure-moat argument.

**Whether the business deserves deeper work, and what would change the answer:**

The business deserves deeper work only if the valuation is meaningfully below what the commodity cycle currently implies. The business is not a durable compounder — it does not have pricing power, a moat against named peers, or controllable earnings. Deeper work should focus on cycle timing: where are soybean and softseed crush margins relative to mid-cycle norms, and what does the current price imply about normalized earnings? What would change the answer toward high-quality: five years of demonstrated post-integration crush margins that sustainably exceed ADM on a per-ton basis, a material paydown of the ~$14B debt load, and a return of adjusted ROIC consistently above cost of capital. What would change the answer toward low-quality: a goodwill impairment charge on the Grain Merchandising unit (already at 9% headroom), a further commodities downcycle that brings CFO below $500M, or a biofuel mandate reversal that permanently shrinks vegetable oil demand — any of which would expose the leverage as unsustainable.

## 5. Simple Summary

- **What it does:** Bunge buys soybeans, canola, sunflower seed, and grains from farmers, then either crushes them into meal and vegetable oil or ships them to end-markets — connecting South American and North American farm supply to industrial buyers across Asia, Europe, and the Americas.

- **How it makes money:** By capturing the spread between what raw oilseeds cost and what the crushed outputs (meal and oil) sell for, plus a logistics and merchandising margin on grain flows. Both spreads are set by global commodity markets, not by Bunge. Raw material cost is 95.2% of revenue — the residual is the entire value-add.

- **Whether each new unit creates value:** Marginally uncertain on current economics. The contribution margin per ton of soybeans processed is positive ($72/t in FY2025), so each incremental ton crushed covers its variable costs. But GAAP ROIC (6.7%) sits just below cost of equity (7.2%), meaning the existing capital base is not clearly earning above the hurdle — especially with $223M of Viterra integration costs still running through the P&L. Post-Viterra, each new unit adds volume to an asset base that has not yet demonstrated it earns above its cost of capital.

- **Which segment matters most:** Soybean Processing and Refining — 51.6% of revenue, 52.6% of segment EBIT. The soybean crush spread is the single variable that makes or breaks annual results. EBIT from this segment swung from $2.2B to $0.9B to $1.2B in three years.

- **Whether it has a moat, and against whom:** No moat proven against ADM, Cargill, or Louis Dreyfus, who operate equivalent physical networks. The infrastructure (plants, port terminals, origination) functions as a barrier against new entrants — meaningful, but not the same as a moat. Bunge earns oligopoly economics alongside peers, not above them.

- **What external variables it depends on:** In order of magnitude — (1) soybean and softseed crush margins (market-set, primary driver of earnings); (2) government biofuel mandates in the US, EU, Brazil, and Indonesia (set vegetable oil demand, not controllable); (3) weather in South America and Europe (determines crop availability and farmer selling); (4) Argentine export policy and FX controls (affects South American origination economics and ~$835M Brazilian farmer advance book). These four external factors together explain most of the earnings variance across any given year.

- **Whether capital is allocated well:** No. The Viterra acquisition — a bet-the-company deal exceeding pre-deal equity market cap, financed largely with debt — doubled total debt to $14.1B, produced negative FCF (-$879M in FY2025), diluted shares 44%, and left net leverage at ~5.8x EBITDA. Two further acquisitions within 12 months confirm a serial-acquirer pattern. Clean governance hygiene (stable auditors, experienced management, no restatements) is genuine but does not offset the capital-structure risk. Capital allocation score is capped at 50/100 per the serial-acquirer and capital-structure transaction rules.

- **Whether it deserves deeper work:** Yes, but only at a price that adequately reflects a commodity-cycle and biofuel-policy business with ~5.8x net leverage and thin goodwill cushion on the biggest deal in company history. The business itself — structurally — is a commodity-throughput machine with earnings that depend almost entirely on external forces. Deeper work should focus on: (a) where crush margins are in the cycle and what normalized earnings look like post-Viterra integration; (b) whether the December 2026 trade-receivable securitization renewal and $1.28B senior note maturities create a liquidity event; and (c) whether the Grain Merchandising goodwill headroom (9% buffer on $593M) survives the next annual impairment test.
