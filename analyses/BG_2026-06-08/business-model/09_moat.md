# Moat — BG

## 1. Named Competitors

Inherited from `08_competitive-map.md` (all named in BG FY2025 10-K, Item 1, pp. 3–6):

- **ADM — Archer-Daniels-Midland Co. (NYSE: ADM):** Global oilseed crushing, grain origination, and value-added ingredients. Competes with Bunge in all four reportable segments across North America, South America, and Europe.
- **Cargill Incorporated (private):** Global grain origination and merchandising, oilseed crushing, food ingredients, animal protein, and ocean freight. Competes with Bunge across all segments and geographies.
- **Louis Dreyfus Company B.V. (private):** Global grain origination and merchandising, oilseed processing, and ocean freight. Competes with Bunge primarily in South American origination and soybean and softseed flows.

*No single moat provides a clearly evidenced, durable profit shield against the named competitors (ADM, Cargill, LDC). The nearest real advantages — physical infrastructure network (distribution/location) and soybean crush volume leadership vs. ADM — are matched or exceeded by Cargill and replicated by LDC. They protect Bunge from new entrants, not from the ABCD peers who already sit inside the same oligopoly.*

## 4. Where The Company Sits

**Bunge sits at the bottom of the available named-peer comparison on gross margin in FY2025 (4.85% vs. ADM's 6.3%), but the two companies are directionally aligned — both experienced severe margin compression in FY2024–2025 vs. the FY2023 commodity-cycle peak. Cargill and LDC are not comparable on margin due to non-disclosure.**

## 5. Moat Verdict

**No moat proven**

Bunge's competitive position rests on physical infrastructure scale — a global network of crushing plants, grain elevators, and port terminals built over decades — that protects the business from new entrants but provides no measurable economic advantage over the three named competitors who operate equivalent networks. The closest claim to a moat is soybean crush volume leadership over ADM (41.0M mt vs. 36.3M mt in FY2025), but this volume edge has not produced superior margins: Bunge's FY2025 gross margin of 4.85% trails ADM's 6.3% (web source, unverified), and both companies earned sub-4% EBIT margins in FY2025 consistent with the same industrywide crush-spread compression cycle. There is no pricing power, no technology IP, no switching cost, and no network effect. The Viterra synergy capture of $70M on a $70B revenue base is operationally useful but not a structural moat.

The durability test the infrastructure-scale argument would need to pass over the next five years: Bunge would need to demonstrate that its post-Viterra grain origination density and South American port footprint translate into consistently higher crush-spread capture or lower cost-per-ton than ADM across a full commodity cycle — and it cannot make that case from available data. The business-quality module (upstream output, industry rate-of-change score: 60) confirms the industry is structurally stable, which prevents moat decay from technology disruption; but a stable industry where all incumbents operate equivalent infrastructure is not the same as a moated one. Bunge earns oligopoly economics — participation in a concentrated industry with high entry barriers — but earns those alongside, not above, the named peers.
