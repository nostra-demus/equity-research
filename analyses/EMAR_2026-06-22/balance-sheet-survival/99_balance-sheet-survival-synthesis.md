# Balance-Sheet-Survival Module — EMAR (Synthesis)

## Abstract

Emaar Properties PJSC carries gross debt of AED 10,615 mn against an EBITDA of AED 24,132 mn (FY2025), producing gross leverage of 0.44x and strict-basis net leverage of just 0.04x — effectively zero — with the company in outright net cash (AED −2,115 mn strict basis) as of Q1-2026. The single largest near-term maturity, the Sukuk Series 3 (AED 2,753 mn, September 2026), is already covered 4.4x by unrestricted cash alone, with no reliance on capital markets or the revolver. The liquidity runway exceeds normalised FCF (AED 24,295 mn annually), which itself covers all 12-month obligations by a surplus of AED 8,203 mn; the tightest assumed covenant (min EBITDA/interest ≥ 3.0x, an assumption — no actual covenants are disclosed) is only breached if EBITDA falls by 94% from today's level. The structure does not break at −30%, −40%, −60%, or even the FY2020 historical trough of −77%. The verdict is Fortress balance sheet.

## 1A. Module Disconfirmation

**Strongest bear point:** 59.8% of financial debt (AED 5,866 mn) matures within 24 months, and covenants are entirely undisclosed — if actual covenant thresholds are tighter than the market-typical assumptions used here, headroom could be narrower than the 94%/99% indicative figures suggest. Subsidiary-level debt (INR, EGP, PKR facilities) may carry dividend blockers not visible in consolidated data, which in a stress could trap cash at the OpCo level and impair the parent's ability to service its own sukuk from subsidiary earnings. [04_coverage-and-covenants.md; 01_capital-structure-and-leverage.md §6A]

**Strongest bull point:** Even at the FY2020 COVID trough EBITDA of AED 5,500 mn — the deepest actual decline in Emaar's recorded history, a 77% fall from today's peak — coverage remains 11.2x against the assumed 3.0x floor, and the company carries net cash, not net debt, on the strict basis at Q1-2026. A fortress verdict requires no optimistic assumptions.

**Single killer risk:** Reclassification of restricted project-escrow cash (AED 43.3 Bn at Q1-2026) as unavailable in a DLD-regulation dispute or project-delivery failure wave, combined with a Dubai property-market seizure. This would not break the debt stack — AED 12.2 Bn of unrestricted cash already covers all near-term debt — but it would change the liquidity perception for capital markets and revolver lenders dramatically. [01_capital-structure-and-leverage.md §3; 05_off-balance-sheet-and-contingencies.md §1]

**Disconfirming evidence already visible:** None that overturns the fortress verdict at current numbers. The one visible tension is the 52.8% of financial debt due within 12 months — a concentration that looks alarming on the surface but is entirely self-funded by existing unrestricted cash without market access. The lack of covenant disclosure is a data gap, not evidence of a breach risk.

## 3. Reconciliation

No material disagreements between specialists. All agents used the same canonical net-debt basis (strict: gross debt minus unrestricted cash only), the same gross debt figure (AED 10,615 mn at FY2025 / AED 10,064 mn at Q1-2026), and the same EBITDA figure (AED 24,132 mn reported, with mid-cycle AED 14,000 mn alongside). The liquidity-runway agent (03) applies a conservative treatment to the EGP and PKR revolving facilities in the 2026 maturity bucket (full repayment assumed rather than expected rollover), producing an overstated 12-month use figure of AED 16,092 mn — this is deliberate conservatism and does not create a disagreement with the maturity-wall agent (02), which flags those lines as "rolling" in practice.

The only internal tension is the static-runway figure: because the MODULE_RULES cap limits liquidity-runway score to max 60 (revolver availability not certified), the score understates the underlying position materially. This is a compliance-with-rules outcome, not a disagreement about the facts. The conservative (capped) reading is applied throughout.

## 5. Survival Summary

Emaar's gross leverage of 0.44x (FY2025 reported EBITDA) and strict-basis net leverage of 0.04x — effectively zero — are among the lowest for a company of its scale in any sector. The leverage trend is unambiguously improving: the company moved from 2.11x net debt / EBITDA in FY2021 to net cash at Q1-2026, driven simultaneously by debt paydown (from AED 19 Bn to AED 10 Bn) and EBITDA tripling. The near-term maturity wall — 52.8% of financial debt due within 12 months — is entirely self-funded: unrestricted cash of AED 12.2 Bn at Q1-2026 covers the AED 5,182 mn 2026 bucket 2.4 times over without drawing on the AED 7,343 mn committed undrawn revolving credit. No market access is required and no asset sale is needed.

The liquidity runway in static terms is 14.6 months (committed liquidity of AED 19,522.5 mn vs AED 16,092 mn of 12-month obligations, zero FCF assumed); on a going-concern basis normalised FCF of AED 24,295 mn exceeds total annual obligations by AED 8,203 mn and the runway has no finite end. Covenant headroom cannot be assessed — no covenant documents exist in the pool — but applying market-typical thresholds for an investment-grade UAE revolving credit facility, the indicative headroom is 93.9% on coverage and 99.0% on leverage.

The stress test does not break the structure at −30%, −40%, or −60% EBITDA decline, nor at the FY2020 actual trough of −77%. The break point for the assumed minimum coverage covenant (EBITDA/interest ≥ 3.0x) is a 94% EBITDA decline from peak — a level that has no plausible single-year mechanism for a company with AED 154.8 Bn of locked-in revenue backlog. A normal recession of −30% to −40% is survived without an equity raise, asset sale, waiver, or revolver draw.

## 6A. Survival Playbook (non-speculative levers)

- **Refi actions:** The three sukuk series (Series 3: Sep-2026; Series 4: 2029; Series 5: 2031) are the natural refinancing sequence. Emaar has issued all three prior series successfully in the GCC Islamic finance market. Management commentary references "comfortable" liquidity, and the FY2025 investor presentation highlights the undrawn AED 7,343 mn revolving facility as a backstop. No pre-announced tender or extension is in the data pool, but the capital-market track record is established.
- **Asset-sale programs:** No asset sale has been announced or committed in the available data. The segment map (development, malls, hospitality) and the AED 60 Bn+ JV pipeline suggest latent disposal capacity (individual towers, mall stakes, hospitality assets) if needed, but no programme is underway. This lever is available but unconfirmed.
- **Capex flexibility:** Total capex is AED 934 mn (FY2025) — only 2.8% of CFO (AED 33.5 Bn). The development model is asset-light at the maintenance level; capex can be cut meaningfully without impairing near-term operations. Inference, not from filings.
- **Dividend flexibility:** The FY2024 dividend of AED 1.00 per share (~AED 8.8 Bn) is large relative to gross debt. No contractual floor on the dividend is disclosed. Suspending or halving the dividend would free approximately AED 4–9 Bn per year of FCF — a material lever in a stress scenario where EBITDA had fallen significantly. No constraint is disclosed.
- **Covenant-amendment likelihood:** Not assessable — covenants are undisclosed. No prior amendment is disclosed in the pool.

## 7. Note To The Final Synthesizer

- **Leverage:** Gross debt / EBITDA is 0.44x (FY2025 reported) and 0.76x on mid-cycle EBITDA of AED 14,000 mn. Strict-basis net leverage is 0.04x (FY2025) and net cash (−0.08x) at Q1-2026. This is the definition of a Fortress balance sheet, not a stretched or adequate one.

- **Maturity wall and refinancing:** 52.8% of financial debt (AED 5,182 mn) matures within 12 months, anchored by Sukuk Series 3 (AED 2,753 mn, September 2026). This is self-funded: unrestricted cash at Q1-2026 covers the entire 2026 bucket 2.4x without revolver draw, asset sale, or market access. The refinancing risk for this module is a cost risk — the ~3.73% sukuk coupon will reset closer to ~5.3% (indicative market rate, web-sourced June 2026, unverified) on rollover, adding roughly AED 101 mn to annual interest on the three series. At AED 24,132 mn EBITDA and AED 492 mn interest expense, this is financially immaterial.

- **Liquidity runway:** Normalised annual FCF of AED 24,295 mn covers all 12-month obligations of AED 16,092 mn by AED 8,203 mn surplus; no finite runway applies. Static committed liquidity (AED 19,522.5 mn) covers 14.6 months of obligations on zero FCF. The runway depends on two things: (a) the AED 12.2 Bn unrestricted cash figure being accurately classified (not restricted escrow); and (b) the AED 7,343 mn revolving credit remaining available — terms not disclosed.

- **Tightest covenant and headroom:** Covenants are entirely undisclosed in the data pool — no credit agreement, sukuk trust deed, or facility letter is available. The headroom calculation in this module is computed against assumed market-typical thresholds and is marked "Not assessable" for scoring. The master synthesizer should not treat the indicative 94% / 99% headroom figures as confirmed — they are directional only. At 0.04x net leverage and 49x coverage, no plausible covenant design triggers a breach under any scenario tested.

- **Largest off-balance-sheet / contingent exposure:** The AED 5,487 mn Dubai Municipality performance guarantee (5.1% of equity) and the AED 2,675 mn JV-specific contingent obligations (Note 16) are the sized contingent items. Litigation claims from two India cases are disclosed but not quantified by management.

- **Stress break point:** No tested scenario — including −77% EBITDA (the FY2020 COVID trough) — breaks any coverage, leverage, or liquidity constraint. The first assumed covenant breach (min EBITDA/interest ≥ 3.0x) requires EBITDA to fall by 94% from today's level. This module's downside scenarios are not the inputs for a base-case downside — they are the outer limits of survival. The master synthesizer should treat the survival floor as robust even in a severe Dubai property-market downturn.

- **Strategic flexibility of net cash (CLAUDE.md §24 Filter 3):** The net-cash position (strict basis, Q1-2026) and the AED 154.8 Bn revenue backlog together give Emaar counter-cyclical optionality that most real estate developers lack. If the Dubai market corrects, Emaar can acquire land at distressed prices, support contractors and JV partners, and hold staff — without accessing the capital market. This is not a lazy balance sheet; it is a strategic asset appropriate to a long-cycle, high-volatility business.

- **Partial-data caps applied:** (1) Covenant headroom: "Not assessable" — no covenant disclosure; overall usefulness capped at 75. (2) Liquidity runway score: capped at 60 — revolver availability not certified by borrowing-base certificate or availability notice.

- **Biggest missing data point (single highest-value request):** The sukuk trust deeds and the AED revolving credit facility agreement — together they would provide covenant definitions, maintenance-covenant thresholds, change-of-control put mechanics, cross-default provisions, and dividend-blocker language for the AED 6,424 mn sukuk stack and the AED 3,673 mn revolving credit.
