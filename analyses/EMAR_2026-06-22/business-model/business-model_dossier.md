# Business Model Reality Check — EMAR (Synthesis)

## Abstract

Emaar Properties PJSC (DFM: EMAAR) is a Dubai-based real estate developer and property-asset operator that sells residential units in master-planned communities, owns and leases retail malls, and runs hotels under its own brands. Development sales drive 73% of revenue and approximately 65% of EBITDA, with recurring mall and hospitality income providing a 21%/32% revenue/EBITDA buffer. The single clearest positive is the AED 134.3 billion UAE revenue backlog — roughly 3.7 times FY2025 UAE development revenue — locked in at contracted prices at a 50% gross margin, giving three to five years of near-certain revenue visibility. The single clearest negative is structural cyclicality: EBITDA fell 41% in FY2020 from its FY2019 level, the current FY2025 margins are at a cyclical peak, and contingent liabilities jumped 45% year-on-year to AED 25 billion in FY2024, growing faster than earnings in the same year that margins peaked. No disqualifier triggered; this is an average-to-cyclical business that deserves deeper work only with a clear view on the Dubai property cycle.

## 5. Simple Summary

- **What it does:** Emaar builds and sells master-planned residential communities in Dubai, owns and operates The Dubai Mall and 74 other retail malls, and runs 41 hotels. Burj Khalifa and Downtown Dubai anchor its brand.

- **How it makes money:** Primarily by selling off-plan residential units (73% of revenue), recognising revenue as construction progresses under IFRS 15. Secondarily by leasing mall space (12% of revenue, 86% EBITDA margin) and running hotels (5%). Buyers pay in instalments that fund construction, meaning Emaar largely builds with customers' cash.

- **Whether each new unit creates value:** At current cycle conditions, yes — a ~47–50% gross margin on UAE development, 0.5% customer default rate, and advance-funded construction all support value creation. Through the cycle, the picture is less certain: FY2020 showed EBITDA falling 41%, and undisclosed per-unit construction costs prevent independent confirmation of trough-cycle unit profitability.

- **Which segment matters most:** UAE Development — it is 73% of revenue and ~65% of EBITDA and controls the thesis. The malls business (86% EBITDA margin, 19% of EBITDA) is the quality anchor but is too small to override the development cycle.

- **Whether it has a moat, and against whom:** Narrow moat. Irreplaceable location (Downtown Dubai / Dubai Mall), brand (Burj Khalifa), and land-bank scale give Emaar a through-cycle ROIC of ~28–35% against a ~12% cost of capital. No competitor can acquire the plot next to the Burj Khalifa. But Aldar, DAMAC, and Nakheel replicate the master-plan model in other districts, and new boutique developers are taking market share in high-end niches.

- **What external variables it depends on:** Dubai residential property demand (HIGH — controls ~73% of EBITDA), UAE government land supply and tax policy (HIGH — two tax introductions in two years already clipped net margin ~400 bps), EGP/AED FX (moderate, affects ~5% of group revenue), and construction cost inflation (moderate, partially contracted away). Interest rate risk is very low.

- **Whether capital is allocated well:** Largely yes — net cash AED 61.7 Bn, no serial M&A, no equity dilution, strong dividend coverage. The structural GRE discount (Dubai government controls the boardroom and has broader goals than minority shareholders) and AED 60+ Bn of off-balance-sheet JV commitments are the main governance concerns, not acute misallocation.

- **Whether it deserves deeper work:** Yes, but only with a clear view on the Dubai property cycle and where it stands relative to 2020-equivalent demand risks. This is a timing-conditional thesis, not a durable-compounder buy-and-hold. The contingent liabilities spike (+45% YoY in FY2024) and IFRS 15 POC revenue recognition are the two earnings-quality flags the next module must investigate.

# Business Identity — EMAR

## 1. What The Company Actually Does

Emaar Properties PJSC (DFM: EMAAR) is a Dubai-based real estate developer and asset operator incorporated in the UAE and reporting under IFRS in AED. It does three things: it sells residential and commercial units in large master-planned communities (apartments, villas, townhouses, and plots); it owns and operates retail malls and leases space to tenants; and it owns and manages hotels. The residential development business is the dominant revenue driver — selling homes to buyers who pay in instalments over construction, with revenue recognised on a percentage-of-completion basis under IFRS 15. The malls business earns base rent and turnover-linked rent from retail tenants on three-to-five year lease terms. The hospitality business earns room revenue, food and beverage, and related fees from owned and managed hotels. Geographically, the UAE — principally Dubai — generates the large majority of revenue; international operations (primarily Emaar Misr in Egypt) are much smaller. The company's best-known assets are Burj Khalifa, The Dubai Mall, and the Downtown Dubai master community, which anchor its brand credibility for every new project launch. [Investor Presentation FY 2025, Feb-12-2026, pp. 5-7]

## 2. How The Company Makes Money

**Development (UAE and International)**
```
Revenue = units under construction × % completion × contracted sales price
```
Under IFRS 15, Emaar recognises residential revenue incrementally as construction progresses, not on handover. It first sells units at launch (booking revenue), accumulates a revenue backlog (AED 154.8 bn globally as of Dec 31 2025, of which AED 134.3 bn in UAE), and then converts that backlog to reported revenue as build-out advances. [Investor Presentation FY 2025, Feb-12-2026, p. 16] Volume is driven by new launches and by the speed of construction delivery; price per unit reflects location, product type, and Dubai market conditions. FY2025 UAE development revenue was AED 36.4 bn (up 55% vs FY2024) on property sales of AED 71.1 bn. [Investor Presentation FY 2025, Feb-12-2026, p. 15]

**Malls (Emaar Malls)**
```
Revenue = total GLA (sq ft) × occupancy rate × rent per sq ft
    where rent = higher of (fixed base rent) or (% of tenant turnover)
```
Emaar owned or consolidated approximately 10.6 mn sq ft of GLA in UAE as of Dec 2025, with occupancy above 98% at flagship assets. Rent per sq ft was AED 657 at Dubai Mall in FY2025. Total malls revenue was AED 5.75 bn in FY2025 at an EBITDA margin of 86%. [Investor Presentation FY 2025, Feb-12-2026, pp. 22-27; Capital IQ Financials Annual – Segments, FY2025]

**Hospitality**
```
Revenue = available rooms × occupancy rate × average daily rate (ADR)
```
Emaar operated 41 hotels with over 10,000 keys globally as of FY2025. Hospitality revenue was AED 2.33 bn in FY2025. [Investor Presentation FY 2025, Feb-12-2026, p. 5; Capital IQ Financials Annual – Segments, FY2025]

**What drives each line:**
Development margins move with product mix (apartments vs villas vs plots), cost of construction, and the time lag between launching projects and recognising revenue — the backlog acts as a buffer and forward-visibility signal. Mall revenue is relatively stable because occupancy is near-full and rent resets occur on a three-to-five year cycle; volume risk is low, rate risk is modest. Hospitality tracks tourism and Dubai's air-traffic volumes. Consolidated EBITDA margin was 52% in FY2025 on AED 49.6 bn revenue. [Investor Presentation FY 2025, Feb-12-2026, pp. 11-12; Capital IQ Financials Annual – Income Statement, FY2025]

## 3. Business Type Classification

Dubai-domiciled large-scale master-plan developer with attached recurring-income portfolio (malls and hotels), whose earnings are primarily driven by property-cycle volume and IFRS backlog conversion pace.

**Sector-specific red flags to watch (malls):** occupancy dropping below 95%; WALT shortening to under 2 years (increases rollover risk); tenant sales turning negative (which would trigger turnover-rent shortfall below base rent floors).

## 4. What Drives Variance

The biggest single driver of revenue and profit in any year is the pace at which the existing revenue backlog converts to recognised revenue — which is a function of construction completion speed and how many new projects were launched one to two years earlier. When launches are strong (AED 80.4 bn of gross property sales in FY2025) and construction runs on schedule, reported revenue jumps by 40% as in FY2025; a slowdown in new launches or a delivery delay would compress the next one to three years of reported revenue even if sales activity stays flat. Price is set at the time of launch and is largely locked in for existing backlog, so short-term market price movements affect future launches, not current-period revenue. Malls and hospitality add stability — together they contributed 21% of FY2025 revenue and 32% of EBITDA — and move with Dubai consumer-spending and tourism volumes rather than the residential property cycle. [Investor Presentation FY2025, Feb-12-2026, pp. 7, 13; Capital IQ Financials Annual – Segments, FY2025]

**Peak-cycle flag — required disclosure.** The UAE property market has been in a pronounced upcycle since 2021. Sales volumes, average selling prices, and margins are all at or near historical highs in FY2025. The data above reflects peak-cycle returns. For context: in FY2020 (the Covid-year trough), Emaar group EBITDA fell to AED 5.2Bn on AED 17.7Bn revenue (~29% group EBITDA margin), compared to AED 25.6Bn on AED 49.6Bn in FY2025 (~52%). Emaar Development PJSC (the listed UAE subsidiary) reported revenue of AED 9.8Bn in FY2020 vs AED 27.5Bn in FY2025. The 46% EBITDA margin and ~AED 5.1M average unit sales price are not structural constants — they are peak-cycle outcomes.

---

## 3. Value Creation Read

**Each new UAE residential unit creates value at current cycle conditions, but the margin of comfort is heavily cycle-dependent and cannot be stated as a durable constant.**

The arithmetic as disclosed is favourable: a typical unit sold at ~AED 5.1M average sales value (FY2025) against a portfolio-weighted gross margin of ~47–50% implies ~AED 2.4–2.6M of gross profit per unit before overhead and financing costs. Customer advances fund a substantial share of construction (advances from customers represent ~22% of total group assets, or roughly AED 41Bn as at December 2025), which means Emaar is largely building with buyers' cash rather than its own debt — a structural feature that reduces capital at risk per unit and accelerates cash payback. The near-zero default rate (0.5% of sales value) confirms buyers are sticky and collections are reliable.

The critical gap is the absence of disclosed per-unit construction cost or project-level return on invested capital (ROIC — the profit earned on each dirham of capital deployed in a project). Without land-cost allocation and build-cost per unit from the audited IFRS notes, the full unit economics cannot be confirmed from filings alone. What is disclosable supports the "creates value" read; what is missing means the exact margin of safety above the cost of capital cannot be independently verified. The single most valuable missing disclosure is segment-level capital employed and capex (absent for FY2022–FY2025 in the statutory segment note), which would allow a project-level ROIC calculation.

At peak-cycle conditions: creates value, with EBITDA margins of 46% and a fully-funded backlog of AED 134.3Bn providing multi-year visibility. Through the cycle (using the FY2020 trough as a reference), margin compression is material — group EBITDA margins halved and unit volume fell sharply. Value creation at trough-cycle conditions depends on whether average selling prices hold above build cost plus overhead, which cannot be confirmed without disclosed per-unit cost data.

---

## 4. Sensitivity

The input that would most change the value-creation read if it moved 20% is **average selling price per unit**. A 20% fall in selling price — consistent with the kind of correction seen in Dubai property between 2014 and 2020, when prices fell 30–40% from peak — would compress EBITDA margins from ~46% toward 20–25% and could eliminate profit on lower-margin projects (several active projects show achieved gross margins in the 27–43% range, per slide 19). Historically, the most volatile input has been price: unit volumes in UAE Development swung from 2,305 (FY2020) to 19,058 (FY2024), while selling prices per unit have roughly doubled since 2020. Retention (pre-sold backlog coverage) is partly structural protection — with AED 134.3Bn of committed backlog, a significant portion of the next 5–6 years of revenue is already locked in at today's prices, reducing near-term price sensitivity. However, new launches from 2026 onward will be priced at whatever the market supports at that time, making future unit economics fully exposed to the price cycle. Acquisition cost (land) is an embedded historical cost for most current projects and is therefore the least volatile input in the near term, though its opacity in disclosures means this assessment rests on inference rather than filed data.

## 4. Read

This is a geographically concentrated business, not a customer-concentrated one. On the customer side, Emaar sells to many thousands of individual residential buyers and hundreds of retail tenants — no single customer is material, and the customer default rate of ~0.5% of sales value confirms the diffuse buyer base. On the geography side, the UAE (primarily Dubai) accounts for approximately 90–95% of group revenue, a share that has increased as international development shrank to just ~5% of group revenue in FY2025. The concentration is contractually naked at the country level: while individual sales contracts and 3–5 year mall leases exist, there is no agreement that locks the UAE market to Emaar for any extended period.

The single biggest dependency the synthesizer should know about is UAE / Dubai property market demand. A sustained correction in Dubai residential prices or a reversal of the immigration and wealth-inflow trends that have driven the cycle since 2021 would hit roughly 90–95% of Emaar's revenue base simultaneously, with limited international diversification to absorb the shock.

## 2. Input Cost Pass-Through

Emaar does not disclose contractual cost-escalation clauses or indexed pricing in its development contracts with buyers — the FY2024 Annual Report (notes to financial statements, pp.149-153) confirms that development revenue is recognised under IFRS 15 on a percentage-of-completion basis at the price set at launch. This means the sales price is locked in at the moment of off-plan booking, and any rise in construction costs after launch is absorbed by Emaar, not passed through to the buyer. There is no disclosed mechanism to transfer post-launch cost overruns. The gross margin buffer is substantial — UAE development gross margin was approximately 50% in FY2025 and the blended group gross margin was approximately 57% in FY2024 (revenue AED 35,505 mn less cost of revenues AED 15,124 mn; FY2024 Annual Report, income statement, p.142) — which means Emaar can absorb meaningful cost creep before a project turns unprofitable, but the risk sits entirely on Emaar's balance sheet, not the buyer's.

**Supplier and input concentration.** Emaar draws on 5,006 active suppliers in the UAE, and 98.68% of FY2024 spending was directed to local (UAE-based) suppliers and contractors. [FY2024 Annual Report, p.82] The supplier base is therefore geographically concentrated in the UAE but numerically broad. No single-supplier concentration percentage is disclosed anywhere in the available pool — neither the annual report nor the investor presentation quantifies the share of construction spend attributable to the largest contractor or the top three. No sole-source or single-source input or component is identified in the risk disclosures. The primary raw material risk cited by management is construction-cost inflation, which the company addresses through digital-transformation and AI-driven procurement efficiencies. [FY2024 Annual Report, p.81] Because the disclosure does not quantify single-supplier dependency, a material single-source risk cannot be confirmed or ruled out from available data — this is a disclosure gap, not a zero-risk statement.

---

## 3. Customer Pricing Power

In the development segment, Emaar is effectively the price-setter at the point of launch, not a price-taker. Dubai's residential property market experienced price appreciation of roughly 20% in FY2024, and Emaar's gross property sales rose 72% in FY2024 to AED 65.4 bn before growing a further 9% to AED 71.1 bn in UAE in FY2025. [Investor Presentation FY2025, Feb-12-2026, p.15] The 0.5% buyer default rate on contracted sales value confirms that buyers are not walking away from locked-in prices — the opposite of a price-taker dynamic. Emaar launches new projects at market-reflective prices (not discounted) and sustains near-full subscription, which is consistent with a business that sets rather than accepts prices.

In the malls segment, occupancy above 98% at flagship assets and growing tenant sales (up 10.1% in FY2025 vs FY2024) indicate Emaar can hold or raise rents at lease renewal with little vacancy risk. [Investor Presentation FY2025, Feb-12-2026, p.22] Rent per sqft at Dubai Mall rose to AED 657/sqft in FY2025. The turnover-linked rent structure (higher of base or % of tenant sales) means Emaar participates in tenant upside automatically. In hospitality, pricing power is weaker — Dubai's hotel market is competitive and RevPAR data is not disclosed, so the degree of rate-setting power cannot be independently confirmed.

---

## 4. Economic Control Verdict

**Mixed** — Emaar controls the downstream economics of its two largest businesses (development and malls) but does not control its upstream construction input costs, and has no mechanism to pass post-launch cost increases to buyers. On the downstream side, Emaar sets off-plan prices for a product that buyers accept with a 0.5% default rate, and it leases mall space to tenants at above-98% occupancy with no pricing concessions visible. On the upstream side, construction is outsourced to a broad but undisclosed contractor base, prices are locked in at launch, and any cost overrun between launch and delivery sits on Emaar's gross margin with no contractual relief. The government land relationship is a structural advantage that effectively gives Emaar control over its primary input (land in prime Dubai locations), which limits the pure squeeze dynamic — but it is a privileged relationship rather than market-derived bargaining power.

---

## 5. The Single Biggest Bargaining Risk

Construction cost escalation after project launch: prices are locked in at booking, the buyer has no obligation to absorb overruns, and Emaar must deliver or forfeit; a sustained rise in UAE labour and materials costs would compress development gross margins project by project with no pass-through route to the AED 154.8 bn of already-contracted backlog.

## 4. Read

Emaar is a classic cyclical property developer with an embedded recurring-income portfolio. It is not a durable compounder — it is a property-cycle and Dubai-demand-cycle business dressed in a strong brand and a high-quality land bank. The revenue backlog of AED 134.3 Bn (UAE) provides 3–4 years of near-term visibility, but does not break the cyclical character of the business: when the property cycle turns (as in FY2020, when EBITDA fell 41%), a new-launch slowdown eventually drains the backlog, and reported revenue and margins follow down with a lag. The FY2025 EBITDA margin of 52% and the 85% two-year revenue growth rate are cyclical peak numbers; the through-cycle EBITDA margin is closer to 40–45%, and any investment thesis must be anchored to those levels rather than to the current peak. The single factor a buyer should watch most closely over the next 24 months is the pace of new property sales launches and bookings in Dubai — specifically whether AED 70–80 Bn in annual gross property sales can be sustained as global wealth flows, visa-driven migration, and dollar-strength continue, or whether a reversal in any of those demand drivers causes bookings to slow and the backlog to stop refilling. The industry rate-of-change row at 62 (strong) reflects that property development is not a technology-disruption story; the risk here is cyclical and policy-driven, not structural. The regulatory dependence row at 35 (weak) is a permanent ceiling on the quality score: the Dubai government is simultaneously Emaar's partner, its land supplier, its planning authority, and its tax setter, and three of those four roles shifted against minority shareholders in 2024–2025 (new corporate tax, DMTT). The thesis is company-specific and Dubai-cycle-conditional — it is not a pure technology bet, but it is not a durable compounder either.

**The economic moat test (required).** A moat must produce a return on capital **above the cost of capital**. Peer-superiority alone is not a moat: an entire industry can earn below its cost of capital, leaving a "best of a bad lot" leader with no economic moat.

> Return on capital **above** cost of capital: **~28–35% through-cycle ROIC** (gross-capital basis) vs **~12% WACC** (~1,600–2,300 bps spread) — through-cycle ROIC computed from FY2019–FY2025 EBITDA history [FY2025 Investor Presentation, slide 15] and normalized at a 13% structural tax rate; WACC is a CAPM inference, *not from filings*.

1. The FY2025 peak-year ROIC of ~45% is materially above the through-cycle range. The moat test is run on the through-cycle ~28–35% figure.
2. AED ~43 Bn of the cash balance sits in project escrow (not lien, but purpose-restricted). Gross capital computed before this cash is treated as freely deployable, which is correct — the escrow cash is a liability offset, not deployed invested capital.
3. Management does not disclose a ROIC figure. There is no management-headline ROIC to cross-check against.

## 4. Where The Company Sits

**Relative to peers:** Company sits at the **top** of named peers on margin and capital efficiency. Emaar's FY2025 gross margin of 55% and EBIT margin of ~50% compare to Aldar's ~28% EBITDA margin; DAMAC and Nakheel have no disclosed comparable data.

**Absolute (the economic moat test):** The company earns a return on capital **above** its cost of capital — through-cycle ROIC of ~28–35% (gross-capital basis, normalized tax rate 13%) vs WACC of ~12% (CAPM inference). The spread is substantial and survives even the more conservative end of the range. This qualifies as an economic moat by the absolute test, not merely a structural one.

## 5. Moat Verdict

**Narrow moat**

Emaar's moat is real but more limited in scope than the peak-cycle economics suggest. The genuinely durable sources are location (irreplaceable Downtown Dubai, Dubai Marina addresses), brand (Burj Khalifa / Dubai Mall; the most-visited mall on Earth), and scale (land bank, backlog, delivery track record that competitors cannot quickly assemble). These three together produce a through-cycle ROIC of ~28–35% — materially above the ~12% cost of capital and well above the Aldar peer (~14.8% ROIC). The spread is earned, but it rests on land acquired at historical cost (a depleting asset), a cyclically elevated demand environment (Dubai's 2022–2025 boom is exceptional, not the norm), and a government-granted oligopoly that is structurally supportive but not legally exclusive. No competitor can build on the plot next to the Burj Khalifa, but Aldar, DAMAC, Nakheel, and others can replicate the master-plan community model with different land in different districts. The moat does not prevent competitors from taking meaningful share — it prevents them from taking Emaar's specific prime addresses.

The 5-year durability test: Emaar's moat holds if (a) Dubai's master-planned community model remains premium over competing formats (no evidence yet that it is eroding), (b) the malls business defends its 86% EBITDA margin and 98%+ occupancy against e-commerce substitution (footfall at 204 Mn in FY2025 shows resilience), and (c) the new land bank (Dubai Creek Harbour, Emaar Estate / "Emaar Hills") replicates the historical margin profile. The verdict cannot be "Strong moat" because the through-cycle ROIC range (28–35%) rests partly on a cyclical peak environment — a sustained demand reversal similar to 2019–2020 (EBITDA fell 45% from FY2019 to FY2020) would compress the through-cycle ROIC and the verdict would need to be revisited.

**Moat trajectory: stable, with widening risk on the downside.** Over the last 3–5 years (FY2020–FY2025), Emaar's ROIC has risen sharply — from the FY2020 trough to a FY2025 peak — driven by the Dubai demand cycle, not by a widening structural moat. Market share has been roughly stable (~15–16% of Dubai primary sales since at least 2022). Pricing power exists but is cyclical (UAE Development gross margin moved from 52% FY2024 to 50% FY2025 — slight compression even in a peak year). Entry pressure from new boutique developers and DAMAC's expanded luxury launches is real (competitive intensity score 42 from `07_business-quality`). The trajectory is not widening in a structural sense — the moat level is approximately where it was pre-cycle. The risk is that the moat trajectory tips to **eroding** if: (i) the Dubai property cycle reverses and new-launch volumes fall, (ii) the government introduces further tax measures (UAE corporate tax was raised from 0% to 9% in 2024 and DMTT to 15% in 2025, compressing margins in two successive years), or (iii) new Dubai supply — which is at record levels — leads to price competition on comparable master-plan communities. None of these has materially eroded the moat yet, so stable is the correct label as of June 2026.

## 2. Classification

**Standard professional management** — no major capital allocation concerns and clean governance mechanics (net cash balance sheet, stable external auditor, no dilution, no serial M&A, transparent dividend policy), but the company does not qualify as owner-operator because effective control rests with the Dubai government via six government-linked board members rather than with private entrepreneurs with meaningful personal equity at risk. The GRE (government-related entity) character and the scale of off-balance-sheet JV commitments prevent a full owner-operator classification, even though day-to-day management execution has been consistent and aligned with minority shareholders.

---

## 3. Most Material Signal

The off-balance-sheet JV structure is the single signal most capable of deteriorating the classification if it worsens. Emaar runs a sizable portion of its development pipeline through equity-accounted joint ventures — Dubai Hills Estate, Emaar South, Rashid Yachts & Marina, and others — with a combined JV project pipeline of AED 60+ Bn. Under IFRS this is correctly reported and not hidden, but the consolidated balance sheet and cash flow statement do not capture funding calls, cost overruns, or completion obligations at the JV level unless they crystallise onto Emaar's books. If any major JV required an unplanned equity injection, hit a major cost overrun, or saw its partner (Meraas, DWTC, Dubai Holding — all government-linked entities) face liquidity pressure and push obligations back to Emaar, the clean balance sheet would absorb the shock from outside the consolidated perimeter. Given the JV partners are also government-linked, the risk is politically managed but not commercially eliminated. Should regional real estate markets turn sharply or any JV partner face stress, this signal would move from a 32-severity footnote to a classification-changing red flag.

**72 / 100**

This is not a current failure but a structural ceiling on alignment, and it warrants a meaningful discount.

2. **JV complexity and off-balance-sheet scale:** A AED 60+ Bn JV pipeline is large relative to the consolidated balance sheet and introduces material contingent commitments that are opaque from the consolidated statements alone.

These two factors together justify pulling the score from the mechanical ~86/100 down to 72/100.

## 4. Cross-Cutting Patterns

Two patterns emerge from the sweep that are individually manageable but collectively signal a business running at capacity with compressed buffers. First, the customer advance refund liability growing 42% YoY, the contingent liabilities jumping 45% YoY, and the off-balance-sheet JV pipeline at AED 60+ Bn all represent commitments or exposures growing faster than the earnings base in the same year that margins touched a cyclical peak. This is the profile of a business that has presold enormous future delivery obligations in a bull cycle — not unusual for a developer — but the simultaneous acceleration in all three liability categories in FY2024 means a demand reversal or execution stumble would hit the consolidated balance sheet from multiple directions at once, rather than in sequence. Second, the IFRS 15 POC revenue recognition KAM and the 45% contingent liability spike share a root cause: the scale of commitments made in the FY2022–FY2024 bull-market launch wave is now materialising as both a recognised-revenue driver and a growing set of unrecognised obligations, and the two are moving in opposite directions — which is exactly the earnings-quality signal the synthesizer should flag as a potential mean-reversion setup.
