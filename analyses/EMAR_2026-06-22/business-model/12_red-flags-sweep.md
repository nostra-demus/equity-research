## 3. Most Severe New Flag

The contingent liabilities spike — AED 25,008,580k at FY2024 year-end, up 45% from AED 17,228,974k in FY2023 — is the single most material new finding for the synthesizer. No upstream specialist read Note 30 of the FY2024 Annual Report; the figure did not appear in any of the six upstream outputs. The 45% YoY growth substantially outpaces Emaar's 33% revenue growth over the same period, meaning the company's off-balance-sheet liability exposure is widening in relative terms at the same moment its revenues and margins are peaking. The composition matters as much as the total: the AED 5,487,093k performance guarantee to Dubai Municipality ties Emaar to completing committed urban infrastructure, and any shortfall could expose the guarantee. The India litigation through Andhra Pradesh courts (Note 30) represents an active legal proceeding in a jurisdiction where Emaar has a meaningful backlog (30,597 units released, 89% sold per FY2025 slide 20). For the synthesizer, this flag sits at the intersection of execution risk, legal risk, and off-balance-sheet exposure — three dimensions that individually appeared in upstream reports but whose joint materialisation through Note 30's 45% YoY jump was not surfaced by any of them.

## 4. Cross-Cutting Patterns

Two patterns emerge from the sweep that are individually manageable but collectively signal a business running at capacity with compressed buffers. First, the customer advance refund liability growing 42% YoY, the contingent liabilities jumping 45% YoY, and the off-balance-sheet JV pipeline at AED 60+ Bn all represent commitments or exposures growing faster than the earnings base in the same year that margins touched a cyclical peak. This is the profile of a business that has presold enormous future delivery obligations in a bull cycle — not unusual for a developer — but the simultaneous acceleration in all three liability categories in FY2024 means a demand reversal or execution stumble would hit the consolidated balance sheet from multiple directions at once, rather than in sequence. Second, the IFRS 15 POC revenue recognition KAM and the 45% contingent liability spike share a root cause: the scale of commitments made in the FY2022–FY2024 bull-market launch wave is now materialising as both a recognised-revenue driver and a growing set of unrecognised obligations, and the two are moving in opposite directions — which is exactly the earnings-quality signal the synthesizer should flag as a potential mean-reversion setup.
