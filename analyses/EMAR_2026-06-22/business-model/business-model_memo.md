## 2. What This Module Found

Emaar makes ~73% of its revenue and ~65% of EBITDA (earnings before interest, tax, depreciation and amortisation — a rough cash-profit proxy) from selling off-plan residential units in Dubai. Mall leasing (86% EBITDA margin) and hotels add a 21% / 32% revenue / EBITDA recurring-income buffer. The single biggest positive is an AED 134.3 Bn UAE revenue backlog — roughly 3.7× FY2025 UAE development revenue — already contracted at 47–50% gross margins and largely funded by ~AED 43 Bn of buyer cash sitting in project escrow; the customer default rate is 0.5% of sales value. That locks in three to five years of near-certain IFRS revenue.

The single biggest negative is structural cyclicality at peak. FY2025's 52% EBITDA margin and 55% revenue growth are peak-cycle outcomes; through-cycle margins look closer to 40–45%, and in FY2020 EBITDA fell 41% in one year. At the same peak, three liability categories grew faster than earnings: contingent liabilities up 45% year-on-year to AED 25 Bn in FY2024; customer advance refund liabilities up 42% YoY to AED 32.5 Bn; and an off-balance-sheet joint-venture pipeline of AED 60+ Bn. That is the profile of a developer running at maximum commitment at the top of the cycle.

The disconfirming evidence already on the page: UAE Development gross margin slipped from 52% (FY2024) to 50% (FY2025) at peak demand, and government taxes cut net margin from 49% to 45% without any downturn — both small, both moving the wrong way at the top.

## 3. The Specialists, Briefly

- **Data triage:** Sufficient — FY2024 audited report + Q1 2026 Capital IQ; FY2025 full audited report not yet published; primary annual report in Arabic.
- **Disqualifier scan:** All 8 cleared — KPMG unqualified for 6 consecutive years; no restatement above 5%; related-party deals under 2% of expenses; operating cash flow positive in all confirmed years.
- **Business identity:** Dubai-domiciled master-plan developer with embedded recurring-income portfolio (developer + partial REIT). AED 154.8 Bn global revenue backlog.
- **Segment map:** UAE Development dominant (73% revenue, ~65% EBITDA); Emaar Malls highest-margin sub-business (86% EBITDA margin); segment capex missing FY2022–FY2025.
- **Unit economics:** Each new UAE unit creates value at current conditions (avg AED 5.12 Mn selling price, ~47–50% gross margin); through-cycle margin of safety (the cushion between price and value) not confirmable without per-unit cost data.
- **Customer / geography:** No customer concentration; UAE concentration ~90–95% of revenue is the single biggest dependency.
- **Value chain:** Strong pricing power at launch but no post-launch cost pass-through to buyers; construction cost escalation is the biggest bargaining risk.
- **Business quality:** 47/100 — anchors (low disruption, commodity-buffered) undercut by cyclicality (28/100) and regulatory dependence (35/100).
- **Competitive map:** Oligopoly; ~15–16% Dubai primary-market share; Emaar's ~52% EBITDA margin is roughly 2× Aldar's ~28%.
- **Moat:** Narrow — through-cycle ROIC (return on invested capital — profit per dirham of capital tied up) ~28–35% vs ~12% WACC (cost of capital — what that money costs to raise); stable, not widening.
- **External dependency:** 42/100 (higher is worse); AED-USD peg removes main FX risk; interest rate exposure negligible (49× coverage).
- **Capital allocation & governance:** 72/100 — clean mechanics; AED 60+ Bn off-balance-sheet JV pipeline (severity 32); 6 of 9 board members government-linked; no CEO succession plan after 27-year tenure.
- **Red-flags sweep:** Two flags above severity 40 — contingent liabilities spike (62) and IFRS 15 percentage-of-completion (POC) revenue recognition Key Audit Matter (42).

**Tensions reconciled:** business-quality scored pricing power 52/100 (mixed); value-chain called Emaar a price-setter (strong). The synthesis carries the conservative 52/100 — pricing power is real at launch but point-in-time, not structural, because launch prices lock in for the life of the contract. On moat math, through-cycle ROIC of 28–35% vs ~12% WACC uses inferred invested capital, so the moat is carried as narrow (not wide) to reflect the uncertainty.

## 4. What Would Change This Read

**Would shift toward "high-quality business":**
- Recurring revenues (malls + hotels) grow to 35%+ of group EBITDA, cutting development-cycle dependence
- Through-cycle EBITDA margins demonstrably hold above 45% through a demand correction (not yet proven — FY2020 showed a 41% EBITDA decline)
- Contingent liabilities stabilise or fall as a share of revenue, reversing the FY2024 trend

**Would shift toward "low-quality business":**
- New Dubai property sales bookings fall more than 20% for two consecutive quarters without a matching cut in launches
- Construction-cost inflation pushes UAE development gross margin below 40% on existing backlog
- Any JV partner (Meraas, Dubai Holding, DWTC) hits liquidity stress and pushes equity calls back to Emaar
- The AED 25 Bn contingent liability pool partially crystallises (e.g., the AED 5.5 Bn Dubai Municipality performance guarantee, or India litigation)

---

## 5. Bottom Line

- **Verdict:** Cyclical business — deeper work only with a strong timing view on the Dubai property cycle.
- **Best reason it could be better than it looks:** The AED 134.3 Bn backlog is contractually committed at 47–50% gross margins and largely pre-funded by AED 43 Bn of buyer cash in escrow; even in a downturn, three to five years of revenue are substantially locked in.
- **Best reason it could be worse than it looks:** FY2025 is a cyclical peak (52% EBITDA margin, 55% revenue growth); FY2020 showed EBITDA can drop 41% in a single year, and three liability categories are growing faster than earnings at that peak.
- **Most important missing data:** Segment-level capex for FY2022–FY2025 — without it, per-project ROIC cannot be derived from filings and through-cycle unit economics cannot be independently confirmed. FY2025 full audited annual report (not yet published) is a close second.
- **One thing to watch next:** New Dubai sales bookings vs new launch volume — the earliest signal of whether the backlog can refill at current prices, or whether the cycle is rolling over.

## 6. Plain-English Glossary

- **EBITDA** — earnings before interest, tax, depreciation and amortisation; a rough cash-profit proxy.
- **Backlog** — revenue already contracted with buyers but not yet recognised in the accounts (recognised over time as construction progresses).
- **IFRS 15 percentage-of-completion (POC)** — the accounting rule that lets a developer record revenue gradually as a project gets built, based on management's estimate of how much of the work is done; if remaining costs are under-estimated, revenue gets pulled forward.
- **ROIC** — return on invested capital, the profit earned per dirham of capital tied up in the business.
- **WACC / cost of capital** — what the money tied up in the business costs to raise from debt and equity holders combined.
- **Net cash** — cash minus total debt; a positive figure means cash exceeds debt.
- **Margin of safety** — the cushion between what something is worth and what is paid for it.
- **GRE (government-related entity)** — a listed company controlled by a government, which can prioritise national goals alongside per-share value for minority shareholders.
- **Key Audit Matter (KAM)** — an item the auditor singles out as needing extra attention because it relies heavily on management judgement.
- **Sukuk** — an Islamic-finance bond; pays a fixed return without conventional interest.
