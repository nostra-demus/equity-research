# Earnings Quality — EMAR (Emaar Properties PJSC, DFM:EMAAR)

**Reporting standard:** IFRS  
**Reporting currency:** AED (UAE Dirham), in millions unless noted  
**Fiscal year end:** 31 December  
**Sources:** Capital IQ & Proprietary Data export (FY2021–FY2025 + LTM Mar-31-2026); FY2025 Investor Presentation (Feb 12, 2026 — preliminary, unaudited); FY2024 Annual Report (IFRS, audited, Mar 14, 2025 — primary Arabic-language filing)  
**Upstream input:** `01_historical-financials.md` — present and used.

**Important structure note for this business:** Emaar is an off-plan property developer operating under IFRS 15 percentage-of-completion (POC) revenue recognition. Customers pay upfront or in instalments over the construction period; the cash enters the company as "unearned revenue" (contract liabilities) years before it converts to EBITDA and revenue. This makes EMAR's cash conversion metrics look different from most businesses — CFO/EBITDA consistently exceeds 100% not because of aggressive collection but because cash is collected before the income is recognised. The key quality question is not "does cash exceed profit?" (it does, structurally) but "what does the normalised operating FCF look like once advance customer payments are separated from recurring cash generation?"

## 2. Cash Conversion Assessment

CFO/EBITDA has been 135–203% across FY2021–FY2025, which superficially looks exceptional. It is, but for a structural reason: off-plan property developers collect cash before they recognise revenue. The more informative ratio is **normalised operating FCF as a percentage of reported EBITDA**: FY2021 = 92%, FY2022 = 148%, FY2023 = 111%, FY2024 = 88%, FY2025 = 101%. This too is above 70% in every year and above 100% in three of five years — unambiguously healthy. The trajectory shows FY2024 as a soft year (88%) because the advance-payment surge outpaced earnings delivery, followed by a strong FY2025 recovery. Cash conversion is not a concern for this company; the more important question is whether the advance receipts (AED 40.7Bn of non-current unearned revenue at Dec 2025) convert into deliveries on schedule. [Capital IQ Annual Cash Flow; FY2025 Investor Presentation, slide 12]

## 3. Working Capital Trends

Working capital analysis for a UAE property developer under IFRS 15 requires care. "Accounts receivable" of AED 58Bn is not trade receivables in the conventional sense — it is the long-dated installment receivable from off-plan buyers who have contracted but not yet paid their remaining instalments (typically 5–10% due on handover). DSO calculated against annual revenue is therefore extremely high by construction (400–770 days) and is not a sign of collection risk per se, but it must be monitored for any widening that exceeds the payment schedule. DIO is near-zero (EMAR carries almost no finished inventory — units pass directly from construction to the buyer's name upon handover). DPO is modest and declining.

|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|---|
| Receivable days (DSO) (b) | 583 | 766 | 675 | 577 | 427 | Improving | Low (structural, not collection) |
| Inventory days (DIO) | 6.8 | 1.9 | 2.3 | 1.2 | 0.7 | Improving | None |
| Payable days (DPO) | 35 | 60 | 71 | 35 | 29 | Declining | Low |
| Cash conversion cycle (DSO + DIO − DPO) | 554 | 708 | 607 | 542 | 399 | Improving | Low |

(b) DSO = Accounts receivable (current, CIQ) / (Revenue / 365). For EMAR, receivables are primarily installment-plan balances from contracted buyers, not overdue invoices. [Capital IQ Annual Balance Sheet; Income Statement]

**Flags checked:**

- **DSO rising >10% YoY:** Flagged only in FY2022 (+31.4%) — explained by the surge in new off-plan property sales creating new long-dated receivable balances. FY2023–FY2025 showed declining DSO (−11.9%, −14.6%, −25.9%) as revenue recognition caught up. No ongoing concern.
- **DIO rising >15% YoY:** Not triggered. Inventory is negligible and declining (AED 45M in FY2025 on AED 49.6Bn revenue = effectively zero).
- **DPO rising sharply:** Reversed. DPO rose FY2021–FY2023 (35→71 days) then dropped to 29 days by FY2025 — no stretching-supplier signal.

## 4. Non-GAAP Adjustments

Emaar discloses one systematic non-GAAP EBITDA adjustment. There is no stock-based compensation (options outstanding = 0 per CIQ Supplemental data). The single adjustment is legitimate under the company's IFRS reporting model.

| Adjustment | Amount (FY2025) | Amount (FY2024) | Recurring? | Concern Level | Evidence |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|---|
| Finance cost from discounting/unwinding of long-term receivables under IFRS 15/IFRS 9 backed out of company-adjusted EBITDA | +AED 1,429M | +AED 1,714M | Yes, structural | Low | FY2025 Investor Presentation, slide 8 ("*Excluding finance cost related to discounting/unwinding of long-term payments & leases*"); §4 hierarchy: Investor Presentation |
| Company EBITDA vs CIQ EBITDA reconciliation (cumulative) | Company: 25,561 / CIQ: 24,132 = +5.9% gap | Company: 19,277 / CIQ: 17,563 = +9.8% gap | Structural | Low | [Capital IQ Income Statement; FY2025 Investor Presentation, slide 12] |

**Assessment:** Under IFRS 9, long-term receivables from off-plan buyers are discounted to present value; the unwinding of this discount is a non-cash finance item (income) that inflates CIQ's EBITDA derivation. Emaar's company-adjusted EBITDA removes this, which is the more informative operational figure. The adjustment is not obscuring a poor result — it is genuinely cleaning a financing mechanic. The gap (5.9–9.8% of CIQ EBITDA) is material enough to track but not a red flag. Downstream agents should use CIQ EBITDA for peer comparison and company-adjusted EBITDA for internal credit/covenant analysis.

## 9. Earnings Quality Score

**Score: 68 / 100**

Band: **61–80 — Mostly clean but some working capital or adjustment noise.**

**Reason for this score:**

The underlying EBITDA-to-cash conversion is genuine and exceptionally healthy — normalised operating FCF was positive in every year, covering dividends with room to spare, and CFO consistently exceeds EBITDA. There is no stock-based compensation, no aggressive earnings management, and no revenue recognition that pulls future income forward (if anything, IFRS 15 POC causes income to lag cash). The single biggest structural adjustment (IFRS 9 unwinding) is legitimate and well-disclosed.

The score is not in the 81–100 band because of three real concerns: (1) reported FCF of AED 32.5Bn meaningfully overstates normalised operating FCF of AED 24.3Bn, and a reader who takes the reported figure as recurring cash generation is misinformed; (2) deferred charges (long-term capitalised costs) have grown faster than revenue for three consecutive years, reaching 9.2% of revenue, and cannot be fully evaluated without the FY2025 audited annual notes (not yet in the data pool); and (3) the three-year run of asset writedowns (FY2021–FY2023 totalling AED 2Bn) while now stopped is a pattern that deserves scrutiny in any future year that brings hospitality or international stress.

## 10. The Single Biggest Quality Concern

**The single biggest quality concern is the gap between reported FCF (AED 32.5Bn in FY2025) and normalised operating FCF (AED 24.3Bn), driven by AED 8.2Bn of advance customer payments that inflate reported cash generation.** This is a structural feature of the off-plan property model, not a fraud risk, but it has two real implications. First, analysts who take reported FCF at face value will overstate recurring cash generation by 34% and assign an artificially low apparent FCF yield. Second, and more importantly: the AED 40.7Bn of non-current deferred revenue (and additional current portion) is a delivery obligation, not a profit guarantee. If construction costs rise above budgeted levels, if Dubai's property market softens and buyers seek to cancel or defer handover, or if Emaar's delivery schedule slips, the advance payments it has already collected will have to convert to real project completions at lower margins than originally priced in. The advance-payment model front-loads cash and back-loads the margin realisation risk. As of FY2025, that risk is well-managed — the revenue backlog of AED 154.8Bn covers ~3 years of current revenue at high margins — but it is the single mechanism through which reported quality could erode quickly if the Dubai property cycle turns.

If earnings quality is judged on a purely mechanical basis (cash backing, repeatable margins, low adjustments), this is a high-quality business. The score of 68 reflects the specific structural optic risk in the FCF reporting and the pending verification of the deferred-charges classification.
