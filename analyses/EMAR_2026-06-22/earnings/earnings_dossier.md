## 5. Earnings Setup Summary

### Revenue Setup

The reported revenue trajectory — +7.3% FY2023, +32.7% FY2024, +39.6% FY2025, TTM +33.4% — is not driven by current business momentum in the conventional sense. It is the mechanical output of an IFRS 15 percentage-of-completion backlog conversion process: Emaar sold aggressively in 2021–2024, booked AED 154.8 Bn of contracts, and is now delivering those units and recognising the associated revenue at a rate that will sustain elevated reported growth through at least FY2028–2029 regardless of what happens to new sales today. The revenue trajectory is therefore sustainable at the current pace for another 2–3 years at minimum — backlog coverage is 3.7x FY2025 UAE Development revenue. The single factor that would flip the revenue direction is not a demand shock in 2026 (which would not be visible in reported revenue until FY2029) but a sustained multi-year new sales collapse (30–40% AED value decline for 2+ consecutive years) that stops refilling the backlog and eventually creates a cliff — not a gradual decline — in recognition. The reported revenue and underlying organic demand tell materially different stories right now: reported revenue is accelerating while the forward-demand engine (new unit sales volumes) fell 27% YoY in FY2025. The gap in magnitude is potentially 3–5 years' worth of lagged revenue recognition.

### Margin Setup

The EBITDA margin settled at 47–51% across the six quarters from Q2 2024 through Q1 2026 after the anomalous 53.8% peak in FY2023 (driven by a single Q3 2023 POC project mix event). The current 48.7% (CIQ basis) or 52% (company-adjusted) is at or near sustainable, but with a structural headwind building: UAE Development gross margin fell from 52% (FY2024) to 50% (FY2025), and will likely continue declining as newer, lower-cost-basis projects (booked at 2022–2024 prices against higher 2025–2026 construction costs) replace legacy high-margin projects (Downtown Dubai, Emirates Living) in the recognition queue over the next two to three years. The segment that would take the largest bite out of consolidated margins in an adverse scenario is UAE Development — at 73% of revenue, a 200 basis point gross margin compression on UAE Development reduces group EBITDA margin by roughly 130–150 basis points with no offset mechanism. Emaar has no contractual margin protection on the locked-in backlog (prices are set at off-plan booking date, 3–6 years before revenue is recognised), making the company a price-taker on construction costs for the existing AED 134.3 Bn UAE pipeline. New project launches can reprice higher, but those revenues appear in FY2028–2030, not FY2026.

### Quality Check

The single largest gap between reported and economic earnings is the AED 8.2 Bn difference between reported FCF (AED 32.5 Bn in FY2025) and normalised operating FCF (AED 24.3 Bn), driven by customer advance payments collected under the off-plan model that inflate reported CFO. This gap is widening in directional importance: the TTM CFO growth of only +3.8% against TTM revenue growth of +33.4% suggests the advance-payment inflow is already decelerating, meaning reported FCF will slow faster than revenue in FY2026 if new bookings moderate further. A second gap — deferred charges growing from 5.9% to 9.2% of revenue over three years, unverifiable without the FY2025 audited annual report — represents a potential AED 900–1,400M earnings overstatement if costs are being capitalised rather than expensed. The non-GAAP EBITDA adjustment (IFRS 9 finance-cost unwinding) is genuine and well-disclosed. For modelling normalised FY2026 earnings, the correct starting point is GAAP EBITDA (Capital IQ basis, AED 24,132M FY2025) as the base, adjusted for the declining gross margin trajectory on UAE Development rather than assuming margin stability; the company-adjusted EBITDA of AED 25,561M adds back a legitimate but non-operational IFRS 9 item and should be used only for internal credit analysis.

### Consensus Bar

For the consensus bar (FY2026 EPS mean AED 1.922) to be beaten by a material margin (approximately +10–15%, in line with the three-year average), the UAE Development delivery pace in H2 2026 — specifically Q4 2026 — must meet or exceed the Q4 2025 pace (AED 16,450M revenue; 33% of full-year total), and UAE Development gross margin must hold at approximately 50% or above rather than compressing further. The bar is most likely set incorrectly in two directions simultaneously: the revenue bar is too low (AED 52.7 Bn consensus vs a backlog that mechanically supports higher recognition at current delivery pace), but the gross margin bar (consensus at 51.2%, down 460 bps from 6 months ago) may still be too high if construction costs continue rising. This split — revenue beat with margin miss — is a specific and plausible outcome that does not cleanly resolve into a headline beat or miss on EPS. How much of the current consensus is anchored to a recent tailwind? A significant portion of the prior beat history relied on the Dubai property boom years of 2021–2023, which are now baked into the backlog and no longer represent a repeating tailwind — the current recognition rate is the macro tailwind realised, and the tailwind will not repeat unless a new sales cycle of comparable magnitude begins.

## 6. Key Trend Summary

**Revenue direction:** Accelerating. After a −10.6% dip in FY2022 (pandemic-era deliveries running off), revenue inflected sharply: +7.3% in FY2023, +32.7% in FY2024, +39.6% in FY2025. The LTM Mar-2026 figure of AED 51,858M implies the absolute growth pace is continuing at scale, with Q1-2026 up 22.8% year-on-year. This acceleration is driven by an unprecedented off-plan property sales wave that began in 2021–2022 and is now converting to IFRS 15 POC revenue as projects complete. The AED 154.8Bn revenue backlog (mostly UAE) means this tailwind is locked in over the next 5–6 years.

**Margin direction:** Inflecting (peak behind, stabilising). EBITDA margins peaked at 53.8% in FY2023 (inflated by Q3-2023 POC mix). They have since compressed to 49.5% (FY2024) and 48.7% (FY2025) and appear to be stabilising in the 47%–51% range — a structurally higher level than FY2021's 28.0%, reflecting the shift to higher-margin UAE development projects in Downtown Dubai, Dubai Hills, and similar masterplans. Capital allocation discipline is evident: capex remains low (AED 534M–934M annually vs AED 33Bn+ CFO), preserving the asset-light development model.

**Seasonality:** Pronounced. Q4 is consistently the largest quarter (averaging 32.5% of annual revenue). The Q1 dip of 20%–24% sequential decline from Q4 is structural and repeatable. Any earnings estimate for the full year must anchor to Q4, which is the single most important quarter for revenue recognition.

**Inflection points:**
- **FY2021→FY2022:** Revenue fell 10.6% as COVID-era project delivery cycles normalised; EBITDA grew +19.6% because project mix shifted to higher-margin completions. This masked the near-term revenue weakness.
- **FY2023 Q3 spike:** COGS collapsed in Q3-2023 pushing quarterly gross margin to 75.8% and FY2023 EBITDA margin to 53.8%. This is a one-time POC milestone effect, not a structural improvement. Analysts modelling off FY2023 margins will overestimate future profitability.
- **FY2024–FY2025 re-acceleration:** Revenue reaccelerated as the record 2022–2024 property sales volumes began converting to revenue. With UAE Development revenues up 55% in FY2025 to AED 36,443M, this segment is the dominant driver.
- **Tax regime change FY2024–FY2025:** UAE Corporate Tax (9%) was introduced in FY2024 and a Domestic Top-Up Tax (DMTT, 15%) was introduced in FY2025, both visible in the rising effective tax rate: from near-zero in FY2023 to 7.7% in FY2024 and 13.0% in FY2025. This is an inflection in the tax line that reduces net income growth relative to EBIT growth and is a permanent structural change, not a one-time item. [1][2]

## 2. Revenue Driver Tree

**Business type:** Multi-segment real estate developer + recurring-asset operator.

**Company-specific revenue formula:**

> **Revenue = UAE Development (POC backlog burn × completion pace × project mix) + Recurring Revenues (GLA × rent/sqft + hotel keys × RevPAR × occupancy + entertainment visits × yield)**

The dominant engine is a **backlog-burn model**: Emaar sells off-plan residential units (collecting AED at point of sale), then recognises revenue over a 3–6 year construction period using the percentage-of-completion (POC) method under IFRS 15. Revenue in any period is therefore a function of:

1. **What was sold 3–6 years ago** (the backlog stock), and
2. **How fast construction milestones are completed** in the current period (the burn pace, controlled by delivery schedules).

New property sales today set the revenue floor 3–6 years out, not today. This is the critical distinction between Emaar's revenue drivers and those of a typical volume × price manufacturer.

The recurring businesses — malls, hospitality, entertainment — are operated on conventional contractual models: GLA × rent for malls; keys × occupancy × RevPAR for hotels.

## 7. The Single Biggest Revenue Driver

**The UAE Development revenue backlog and construction delivery pace are the single biggest determinant of Emaar's revenue.**

The AED 134.3 Bn UAE backlog at December 31 2025 is mechanically locked revenue: 93% of units under construction are already sold, contracts are signed, and Emaar is legally obligated to deliver those units. The only question is timing — the pace at which construction milestones are reached determines when that backlog converts to reported revenue under IFRS 15.

If this backlog burned 10–20% faster (through accelerated delivery schedules, additional project staffing, or a front-loading of POC milestones), UAE Development revenue could run AED 3.6–7.3 Bn above the current-year run-rate in a single year, lifting group revenue by 7–15%. If delivery is delayed (labour shortages, regulatory slow-downs, adverse weather, material supply disruptions), the same revenue shifts forward — it does not disappear, it defers.

The current direction is accelerating delivery: 6,129 UAE units delivered in FY2025 vs 4,242 in FY2024, and 50,800+ units are actively under construction with stated delivery timelines of 5–6 years. The most visible near-term catalyst is whether Q4 2026 delivery volumes maintain the Q4 2025 run-rate (Q4 is consistently ~33% of annual revenue).

However, the second-order risk is worth stating plainly: the backlog is refilled by new property sales. FY2025 saw unit sales fall 27% in volume terms (though rise 9% in value) versus FY2024. If the Dubai property market softens and new sales drop 30–40% in AED value — a scenario not yet visible in the data but present as a base-rate risk given the 4-year up-cycle — the backlog would stop growing after 2–3 years, and revenue would eventually plateau or fall with a 3–5 year lag. This does not threaten FY2026 or FY2027 revenue (which are funded by the existing AED 134.3 Bn backlog), but it would cap the long-term revenue trajectory. The next 1–2 years' new property sales data are therefore the leading indicator to watch, not the current backlog level.

## 4. Margin Walk — Which Margin Level Matters Most?

**EBITDA margin is the most useful primary metric for Emaar.** Here is why:

Emaar is a mixed real estate developer (UAE Development) and recurring asset operator (Malls, Hospitality, Entertainment). For the development segment, the relevant metric is gross margin — it directly measures the spread between locked-in contract prices and construction costs. For the recurring segments, EBITDA margin (which strips out D&A from the investment properties and hotel assets) better captures the cash generation of the operating portfolio. At the consolidated level, EBITDA bridges both: it captures project delivery margins and recurring cash yields without being distorted by the large D&A on investment properties (mall buildings, hotel assets) or the IFRS 9 finance income unwinding on long-term receivables (which inflates both company-adjusted EBITDA and net income). EBIT is less useful because D&A on the recurring asset base is a real economic cost of the business. Net margin has become significantly harder to read since the introduction of UAE Corporate Tax (FY2024) and DMTT (FY2025), which add ~600–700 bps of new structural drag; comparing FY2023 net margins to FY2025 net margins requires adjusting out the tax step-change.

The company's own preferred metric is its adjusted EBITDA (AED 25,561M in FY2025, 52% margin), which removes the finance-cost-from-discounting item. For cross-segment consistency, downstream agents should use Capital IQ EBITDA (AED 24,132M, 48.7% margin) for peer comparisons, and note the company-adjusted figure for absolute level analysis.

## 8. The Single Biggest Margin Driver

**UAE Development gross margin — driven by construction cost inflation vs locked-in off-plan selling prices — is the single driver most capable of compressing Emaar's margins materially.**

At its current direction, this driver is a slow but persistent **headwind**. UAE Development gross margin has already declined from 52% (FY2024) to 50% (FY2025) — a 200 basis point compression over one year. The mechanism is structural: Emaar sets selling prices at launch (3–6 years before revenue recognition), construction costs rise over the delivery period, and there is no contractual mechanism to pass those increases to the buyer. With AED 134.3 billion of UAE revenue backlog locked in at historical contract prices, a sustained 5–10% rise in UAE construction costs (labour, cement, steel, concrete) would compress development gross margins by 100–300 basis points across the next two to four years of deliveries, with no offset route. Because UAE Development represents ~73% of group revenue and ~66% of EBITDA, a 200 bps compression at the development gross margin level translates into roughly 130–150 bps compression at the group EBITDA margin level. The only partial protection is the gross margin buffer (currently ~50%), which means projects would need to see a roughly 50% cost overrun to become unprofitable — a remote but not zero risk in a sustained construction-cost surge.

The secondary and fully irreversible driver is the UAE Corporate Tax + DMTT regime change, which has permanently reduced net margins by roughly 400–700 basis points vs the pre-tax era and is not recoverable. However, this does not affect EBITDA margins and is already fully priced into FY2025 reported numbers.

## 7. Bar Assessment

**Bar is low.**

This has compressed the mean estimate, not because existing analysts bearishly revised down, but partly because new entrants with lower starting points joined the pool. The bar is now set below last year's achieved result.

Second, Emaar has beaten EPS consensus by an average of approximately +17% in each of the three prior fiscal years (FY2023, FY2024, FY2025) and beat in all 5 of the last 5 quarters. The company carries a revenue backlog of AED 134.3 Bn (UAE alone), which provides substantial visibility into near-term recognition; the current FY2026 revenue consensus of AED 52.7 Bn is comfortably covered by the backlog at the current recognition pace of roughly AED 43–50 Bn per year in UAE development revenue. This means the Q2 print is never going to be a headline shocker on revenue in absolute terms, and the market knows it — consensus for any individual Q2 is necessarily anchored to a minority-quarter share of the annual total. What this means for the beat-miss setup is that **the bar for the Q2 quarter is lower in absolute terms**, reducing the hurdle rate for a beat. However, it also means the year-on-year comparison is the more important lens: Q2 2025 delivered AED 9,736M in revenue, so even a subdued Q2 2026 of AED 11,500M would represent +18% YoY growth. The seasonality pattern is not a headwind for Q2 — it simply means Q2 is not the quarter that defines the year. Investors will look through the Q2 print and focus on the annualised run-rate, the backlog update, and the new property sales disclosure, all of which carry more information about the full-year trajectory. [01_historical-financials.md, Section 5]

## 7. Historical Pattern

Emaar has beaten EPS consensus in every one of the last five reported quarters (FQ1 2025 through FQ1 2026), with beat magnitudes of +9.6% to +45.8%. On an annual basis, the company beat EPS consensus in FY2023 by +23.4%, FY2024 by +15.9%, and FY2025 by +11.1% — a pattern of consistent, large outperformance that reflects analysts historically anchoring estimates too conservatively against the POC revenue mechanics. The only miss in the visible history is FY2022 (−10.8% on EPS, −3.9% on revenue), which coincided with a softer delivery cycle and revenue recognition timing. The beat pattern is strong enough that the synthesiser should weight it meaningfully — but it should not be mechanically extrapolated. The pattern has occurred during a strong Dubai property up-cycle with an accelerating backlog. If the cycle turns (falling new sales, cost overruns, delivery delays), the same structural mechanism that made analysts look conservative could make them look optimistic. The pattern is a tailwind for the setup but not a guarantee; weight it at roughly 60% confidence that the historical beat tendency continues into Q2–Q3 2026 given the specific evidence from Q1 2026 (+9.6% beat on EPS), the backlog growth to AED 163.4Bn, and the unchanged delivery pipeline. ## 3. Working Capital Trends

Working capital analysis for a UAE property developer under IFRS 15 requires care. "Accounts receivable" of AED 58Bn is not trade receivables in the conventional sense — it is the long-dated installment receivable from off-plan buyers who have contracted but not yet paid their remaining instalments (typically 5–10% due on handover). DSO calculated against annual revenue is therefore extremely high by construction (400–770 days) and is not a sign of collection risk per se, but it must be monitored for any widening that exceeds the payment schedule. DIO is near-zero (EMAR carries almost no finished inventory — units pass directly from construction to the buyer's name upon handover). DPO is modest and declining.

## 10. The Single Biggest Quality Concern

**The single biggest quality concern is the gap between reported FCF (AED 32.5Bn in FY2025) and normalised operating FCF (AED 24.3Bn), driven by AED 8.2Bn of advance customer payments that inflate reported cash generation.** This is a structural feature of the off-plan property model, not a fraud risk, but it has two real implications. First, analysts who take reported FCF at face value will overstate recurring cash generation by 34% and assign an artificially low apparent FCF yield. Second, and more importantly: the AED 40.7Bn of non-current deferred revenue (and additional current portion) is a delivery obligation, not a profit guarantee. If construction costs rise above budgeted levels, if Dubai's property market softens and buyers seek to cancel or defer handover, or if Emaar's delivery schedule slips, the advance payments it has already collected will have to convert to real project completions at lower margins than originally priced in. The advance-payment model front-loads cash and back-loads the margin realisation risk. As of FY2025, that risk is well-managed — the revenue backlog of AED 154.8Bn covers ~3 years of current revenue at high margins — but it is the single mechanism through which reported quality could erode quickly if the Dubai property cycle turns.

If earnings quality is judged on a purely mechanical basis (cash backing, repeatable margins, low adjustments), this is a high-quality business. The score of 68 reflects the specific structural optic risk in the FCF reporting and the pending verification of the deferred-charges classification.

## 4. The Single Highest-Sensitivity Variable

**UAE Development backlog burn pace — specifically, the timing and volume of construction deliveries in Q4 — is the single variable that can move earnings the most in any given year.**

Q4 consistently accounts for ~33% of annual revenue (32.5% average over FY2023–FY2025). In absolute terms, Q4-2025 alone contributed AED 16,450M of revenue. A delay across a handful of major projects that pushes 10% of annual revenue out of one fiscal year causes an estimated AED ~2,400M EBITDA reduction — roughly equal to four full quarters of hospitality and entertainment profit combined. This variable is **partly company-controlled**: Emaar manages construction schedules, contractor relationships, and can accelerate or slow project delivery timelines. However, external shocks — labour availability, material supply disruptions, regulatory permit delays, or extreme weather — can override management's delivery plan. The current trend is accelerating delivery (6,129 UAE units in FY2025 vs 4,242 in FY2024), and 93% of units under construction are already sold, providing strong incentive to complete on schedule. The adverse case would require a systemic disruption to the construction pipeline — not a remote risk given 50,800+ units active — but not a base case given current momentum.

---

## 5. Interaction Effects

Two interaction effects are worth noting. First, **delivery pace and POC project mix interact mechanically**: which projects reach milestones fastest is not independent of which projects are being pushed hardest. If Emaar accelerates delivery on newer, lower-margin projects (The Oasis, recent launches) to hit volume targets, the bull case on delivery pace can simultaneously produce the bear case on POC project mix, partially offsetting the gross EBITDA uplift from higher delivery volumes. The net effect of a delivery acceleration scenario therefore depends on the project-specific margin of the units being accelerated, and available data does not permit decomposing this.

Second, **construction cost inflation and delivery pace compound**: a supply-side construction shock (e.g. a regional steel shortage or UAE labour cost surge) tends to simultaneously slow delivery timelines and push up costs, hitting EBITDA from both the margin and the revenue-timing direction at once. In an adverse construction environment, the bear case on delivery pace (~AED 2,400M EBITDA hit) and the bear case on construction cost inflation (~AED 910M hit) could materialise concurrently, producing a combined EBITDA headwind of up to ~AED 3,300M (roughly −14% vs the FY2025 base) — materially larger than either variable in isolation. The EGP and corporate tax variables are independent of each other and of the construction variables.

---

## 6. Non-Linear Or Asymmetric Risks

Two asymmetries are present.

**Construction cost pass-through lag creates a one-way trap for the existing backlog.** For the AED 134.3 billion UAE revenue backlog (all of which is at prices locked in at off-plan booking date), there is zero ability to recover construction cost increases from buyers. Prices are contractually fixed. This means cost inflation hits margins immediately as projects complete, but a subsequent cost reduction only helps new project launches (whose revenue will not appear for 3–6 years). The mechanism is structurally asymmetric: cost inflation hurts the existing backlog faster and more certainly than cost deflation helps it. A 5% construction cost surge translates to an irreversible, multi-year margin compression on the entire in-flight backlog; a 5% cost decline benefits only projects not yet in active construction.

**New property sales have a long lag but a cliff risk if the cycle turns.** A 30–40% drop in new UAE property sales in FY2026 has no immediate EBITDA impact because the AED 134.3 billion backlog funds revenue through FY2028–2029. But if new sales drop and stay low for 2–3 consecutive years, revenue would eventually cliff off — not gradually decline — because the backlog stop-filling and delivery pipeline runs dry at similar times. The asymmetry is that the revenue protection from the existing backlog creates false comfort: management has 2–3 years of early warning time, but if that window is not used to restock the backlog, the eventual revenue correction can be sharp rather than gradual. The Q4 2025 unit-volume decline (first meaningful YoY decline in the current cycle) is the earliest visible signal that this variable may be approaching a turn.

## 7. Earnings Volatility Score

**48 / 100** — higher = WORSE (inverted: a higher score means more volatile / sensitive earnings)

Emaar's earnings sit in the **material sensitivity** band (41–60): EBITDA is highly stable in the near term due to the backlog buffer (3.7x annual UAE Development revenue coverage), but is meaningfully exposed to two variables it cannot fully control — construction cost inflation and POC project mix — and one that it cannot control at all: government tax policy. The EGP sensitivity is company-disclosed and relatively small at group level. The biggest single swing (delivery pace) is partly controllable. What keeps the score below 60 is that the AED 134.3 billion UAE backlog provides multi-year revenue visibility that is genuinely unusual for a developer — the near-term earnings range is narrower than the driver table might suggest for a typical volume-price business — but the existence of two high-magnitude, poorly-forecastable variables (POC mix and construction cost trajectory) prevents a lower score.

**One-line reason:** The backlog mechanic provides strong near-term revenue floor, but EBITDA can still swing ±10% from delivery timing and project mix alone, and the tax regime is government-controlled and permanently less favourable since FY2025.
