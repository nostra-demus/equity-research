## 1. Next Quarter Context

The next print is **Q2 2026** (April–June 2026), scheduled for release on **2026-08-10**. Historically Q2 is Emaar's second-weakest quarter by revenue recognition, averaging approximately 21% of annual revenue over FY2023–FY2025 [01_historical-financials.md, Section 5]. The FY2026 consensus bar sits at AED 52,657M revenue, AED 26,154M EBITDA, and AED 1.922 EPS normalized — figures that imply a year-on-year EPS *decline* of ~3.5% versus FY2025's AED 1.99 actual, even though the underlying backlog has grown to AED 163.4Bn globally as of March 31, 2026 [Capital IQ Estimates export, data as of 2026-06-22; Web: Kanebridge News, 2026-05-11, unverified]. Consensus has been cut approximately 17% over the past 90 days on EPS, partly because new initiations (AlphaMena, FAB Securities, Arqaam) entered the pool with conservatively anchored models — making the current bar arguably softer than it first appears [04_guidance-consensus.md, Section 4].

## 6. Seasonality Read

Q2 is Emaar's second-weakest quarter for revenue recognition, averaging 21.2% of annual revenue across FY2023–FY2025 (versus Q4's average 32.5%). This means the Q2 print is never going to be a headline shocker on revenue in absolute terms, and the market knows it — consensus for any individual Q2 is necessarily anchored to a minority-quarter share of the annual total. What this means for the beat-miss setup is that **the bar for the Q2 quarter is lower in absolute terms**, reducing the hurdle rate for a beat. However, it also means the year-on-year comparison is the more important lens: Q2 2025 delivered AED 9,736M in revenue, so even a subdued Q2 2026 of AED 11,500M would represent +18% YoY growth. The seasonality pattern is not a headwind for Q2 — it simply means Q2 is not the quarter that defines the year. Investors will look through the Q2 print and focus on the annualised run-rate, the backlog update, and the new property sales disclosure, all of which carry more information about the full-year trajectory. [01_historical-financials.md, Section 5]

## 7. Historical Pattern

Emaar has beaten EPS consensus in every one of the last five reported quarters (FQ1 2025 through FQ1 2026), with beat magnitudes of +9.6% to +45.8%. On an annual basis, the company beat EPS consensus in FY2023 by +23.4%, FY2024 by +15.9%, and FY2025 by +11.1% — a pattern of consistent, large outperformance that reflects analysts historically anchoring estimates too conservatively against the POC revenue mechanics. The only miss in the visible history is FY2022 (−10.8% on EPS, −3.9% on revenue), which coincided with a softer delivery cycle and revenue recognition timing. The beat pattern is strong enough that the synthesiser should weight it meaningfully — but it should not be mechanically extrapolated. The pattern has occurred during a strong Dubai property up-cycle with an accelerating backlog. If the cycle turns (falling new sales, cost overruns, delivery delays), the same structural mechanism that made analysts look conservative could make them look optimistic. The pattern is a tailwind for the setup but not a guarantee; weight it at roughly 60% confidence that the historical beat tendency continues into Q2–Q3 2026 given the specific evidence from Q1 2026 (+9.6% beat on EPS), the backlog growth to AED 163.4Bn, and the unchanged delivery pipeline. [04_guidance-consensus.md, Section 6 and 7]

## 8. Setup Verdict

**Setup favors beat.**

The weight of evidence — an AED 163.4Bn global backlog providing 3+ years of revenue visibility, a Q1 2026 actual that already beat by 9.6%, a consensus EPS bar set below last year's achieved result (AED 1.922 vs AED 1.99 actual), a 5-quarter consecutive beat streak, an easy year-on-year Q2 comparison (+18% growth implied by any decent Q2), and recent property sales acceleration of +16% YoY in Q1 2026 — all point toward another beat at Q2 2026.

The single most important factor is **the UAE Development delivery pace and POC milestone timing**: if Emaar completes its planned H1 2026 construction milestones on schedule, revenue will mechanically run above the AED 52.7Bn full-year consensus given the backlog scale, and the Q2 print will beat. The single biggest risk that could flip the verdict is a **sustained slowdown in new property sales below AED 18Bn per quarter** — not because it hurts Q2 revenue (which is already funded by the backlog), but because it triggers a narrative shift to "cycle peak passed" and forces analysts to cut FY2027–FY2029 estimates, wiping out the stock's forward earnings support regardless of the current-quarter result.

## 9. Second-Quarter Look-Ahead

Q3 2026 (July–September) is seasonally the second-strongest quarter, averaging 25.3% of annual revenue versus Q2's 21.2% — meaning the Q3 print tends to be a meaningful step-up from Q2 [01_historical-financials.md, Section 5]. The Q3 2025 print of AED 13,273M was the strongest non-Q4 quarter in Emaar's history, and with the backlog now 8–9% larger than a year earlier, a Q3 2026 above AED 14,000M is mechanically plausible. However, note that Q3 2023 saw a one-time EBITDA margin spike to 67.9% driven by POC project mix — if higher-margin Downtown Dubai or Dubai Hills legacy projects happen to reach milestones in Q3 2026, margins could print above expectations; if only lower-margin newer projects deliver in that period, margins could disappoint even with revenue inline. The POC mix effect is the main source of margin uncertainty for Q3.

---

## 10. Pre-Mortem

If this beat setup fails, the most likely cause is that consensus was actually right about construction cost inflation compressing UAE Development gross margins further — to 47–48% from the current 50% — at a pace faster than the revenue acceleration from delivery ramp-up can offset. The second most likely cause is that Q2 2026 new property sales disappoint sharply (below AED 15Bn) due to geopolitical disruption or a sudden credit-tightening event in the Gulf, triggering a sentiment reversal that makes even a clean Q2 operating print feel irrelevant to the re-rating story.
