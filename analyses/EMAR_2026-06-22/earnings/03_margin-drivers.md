## 1. Segment Decomposition Status

Business-model module is available and has been read. The five-segment investor-presentation view is used as the primary lens, cross-checked against the statutory three-segment Capital IQ data.

**Emaar is not a single-segment business.** UAE Development at 73% of revenue and ~65–66% of EBITDA is the dominant segment, but it is not above the 85% single-segment threshold; decomposition by segment is therefore required.

**Segment-level P&L is disclosed at the EBITDA and NPBT level** for four of five segments in the FY2025 Investor Presentation (Feb 12, 2026, slides 16, 21–22, 32), which is management-prepared and unaudited. The statutory audited IFRS 8 segment note in the FY2024 Annual Report discloses revenue and NPBT by three segments (Real Estate, Leasing/Retail, Hospitality). Segment-level capex is not disclosed for FY2022–FY2025 in the statutory data — this is a stated gap.

**Cycle-position statement (per MODULE_RULES Cycle-Position Rule):** The external-dependency module classifies the consumer cycle dependency as "High." UAE residential property sales are running at all-time highs — AED 71.1 billion in UAE property sales in FY2025, up from AED 65.4 billion in FY2024, and a backlog of AED 134.3 billion (UAE) as of Dec 31, 2025 — placing the current period squarely at or near a **cycle peak** by historical standards (FY2025 UAE Development revenue of AED 36.4 billion is more than 3x the FY2020 trough level of ~AED 9.9 billion). However, the IFRS 15 percentage-of-completion (POC) mechanic means reported revenue reflects deliveries from 2020–2023 sales bookings; the current revenue peak is not yet a real-time demand peak but a conversion of a historical booking peak. The pipeline through 2030 (backlog AED 134.3 billion vs FY2025 UAE Development revenue AED 36.4 billion = 3.7x coverage) provides runway even if new sales slow. Any one-time demand tailwind from the 2021–2023 Dubai property boom that has been absorbed into the backlog should be treated as **partially baked in, non-run-rate** for new-sales dynamics going forward — the current sales rate of AED 71 billion/year may moderate. The recurring businesses (Malls, Hospitality, Entertainment: 21% of revenue, 32% of EBITDA) sit at mid-cycle to post-recovery positioning — well above COVID troughs but not at peak demand; Dubai Mall footfall of 204 million in FY2025 sets a new record. This assessment agrees with the business-model external-dependency output (cycle position: consumer cycle "High" dependency, mid-to-peak positioning).

**Pass-through lag:** For UAE Development, selling prices are locked at the point of off-plan booking (the moment of contract signing, typically 3–6 years before revenue is recognised under IFRS 15). There is no mechanism to pass post-launch construction cost increases through to the buyer. This means construction cost inflation hits gross margin with zero pass-through ability for the existing backlog of AED 134.3 billion. New launches can reprice higher, but the lag between a cost increase and its effect on recognized gross margin is 3–6 years. For Malls, rent escalation follows pre-defined schedules in 3–5 year leases, meaning pass-through is partial and lagged by up to the remaining lease term (WALT of ~2.9 years). [FY2025 Investor Presentation, slides 16 and 24; FY2024 Annual Report, Note on revenue recognition, p.149]

## 4. Margin Walk — Which Margin Level Matters Most?

**EBITDA margin is the most useful primary metric for Emaar.** Here is why:

Emaar is a mixed real estate developer (UAE Development) and recurring asset operator (Malls, Hospitality, Entertainment). For the development segment, the relevant metric is gross margin — it directly measures the spread between locked-in contract prices and construction costs. For the recurring segments, EBITDA margin (which strips out D&A from the investment properties and hotel assets) better captures the cash generation of the operating portfolio. At the consolidated level, EBITDA bridges both: it captures project delivery margins and recurring cash yields without being distorted by the large D&A on investment properties (mall buildings, hotel assets) or the IFRS 9 finance income unwinding on long-term receivables (which inflates both company-adjusted EBITDA and net income). EBIT is less useful because D&A on the recurring asset base is a real economic cost of the business. Net margin has become significantly harder to read since the introduction of UAE Corporate Tax (FY2024) and DMTT (FY2025), which add ~600–700 bps of new structural drag; comparing FY2023 net margins to FY2025 net margins requires adjusting out the tax step-change.

The company's own preferred metric is its adjusted EBITDA (AED 25,561M in FY2025, 52% margin), which removes the finance-cost-from-discounting item. For cross-segment consistency, downstream agents should use Capital IQ EBITDA (AED 24,132M, 48.7% margin) for peer comparisons, and note the company-adjusted figure for absolute level analysis.

## 8. The Single Biggest Margin Driver

**UAE Development gross margin — driven by construction cost inflation vs locked-in off-plan selling prices — is the single driver most capable of compressing Emaar's margins materially.**

At its current direction, this driver is a slow but persistent **headwind**. UAE Development gross margin has already declined from 52% (FY2024) to 50% (FY2025) — a 200 basis point compression over one year. The mechanism is structural: Emaar sets selling prices at launch (3–6 years before revenue recognition), construction costs rise over the delivery period, and there is no contractual mechanism to pass those increases to the buyer. With AED 134.3 billion of UAE revenue backlog locked in at historical contract prices, a sustained 5–10% rise in UAE construction costs (labour, cement, steel, concrete) would compress development gross margins by 100–300 basis points across the next two to four years of deliveries, with no offset route. Because UAE Development represents ~73% of group revenue and ~66% of EBITDA, a 200 bps compression at the development gross margin level translates into roughly 130–150 bps compression at the group EBITDA margin level. The only partial protection is the gross margin buffer (currently ~50%), which means projects would need to see a roughly 50% cost overrun to become unprofitable — a remote but not zero risk in a sustained construction-cost surge.

The secondary and fully irreversible driver is the UAE Corporate Tax + DMTT regime change, which has permanently reduced net margins by roughly 400–700 basis points vs the pre-tax era and is not recoverable. However, this does not affect EBITDA margins and is already fully priced into FY2025 reported numbers.
