# Earnings Module — TMCV (Synthesis)

## Abstract

TMCV's dominant earnings trend is one of recent structural improvement interrupted by a genuinely uncertain transition: revenue has grown roughly 8% per year over the past three years and Underlying EBITDA margin has expanded approximately 550 basis points from FY23 to FY26, driven primarily by operating leverage as volumes scaled and operating costs fell as a proportion of revenue. The consensus bar — FY27 normalised EPS of ₹19.17, implying +20.5% growth — was set before the Q4 FY26 beat was confirmed and before the Iveco acquisition financing structure became clear, making it simultaneously stale and likely to shift sharply downward once analysts fully model the ~₹3,000–4,000 crore of additional annual interest from the EUR 3.8 billion bridge loan expected to close in Q2 FY27. The biggest near-term earnings risk is the compound of commodity cost inflation (already 100 basis points in Q4 FY26, confirmed "significantly higher" in Q1 FY27) and Iveco deal financing, which together could compress FY27 normalised EPS by 30% or more versus consensus. The verdict is a mixed earnings setup: the underlying CV business is in good health, but FY27 earnings are structurally harder to forecast than they appear.

- `03_margin-drivers` and the business-model synthesis correctly note that the Iveco bridge loan (~EUR 3.8 billion, ~₹33,250 crore) will convert TMCV from net cash to significant net leverage in a single transaction upon deal close.
- **Reconciled view:** Net cash is a real, audited balance-sheet fact at March 31, 2026. It is NOT a structural characteristic. It is a pre-deal snapshot that the Iveco acquisition will reverse sharply. The synthesis treats it as a current-period strength, not a forward-looking buffer. The "biggest risk" line in Section 1 and Section 5b reflect this.

**Disagreement 2: Is the consensus bar "low" or is near-term miss risk genuine?**

## 5. Earnings Setup Summary

### Revenue Setup

The revenue trajectory is real and supported by audited data for the single full year available. But it should not be extrapolated uncritically. The FY26 growth of ~9.8% (management-stated, against a re-presented FY25 base) was almost entirely volume-driven — wholesale units rose 14% YoY — with price and mix together contributing a modest net effect, as blended consolidated ASP actually declined due to a shift toward lower-ASP vans and the near-absence of high-ASP electric buses. This means the revenue story is more cyclically exposed than the headline margin expansion suggests: when freight cycle support fades or diesel prices suppress fleet-owner economics, there is no durable pricing cushion to soften the blow. The single factor that would flip revenue direction is a sustained diesel price increase large enough to compress transporter profit margins below the replacement-cycle threshold — management put this at 30–50% of fleet operators' total operating cost. The Indonesia 70,000-unit export order and growing non-cyclical downstream revenue (parts, Fleet Edge) provide genuine partial offsets, but neither is large enough to fully compensate for a domestic CV volume correction. On the M&A overlay: Iveco, when consolidated, would roughly double revenue scale — but this is a transformation event, not organic growth, and the integration economics are not yet disclosed.

### Margin Setup

The 550 basis-point EBITDA margin expansion from FY23 to FY26 is structurally credible in direction (confirmed by audited filing data in FY26) but rests partly on non-audited carve-out figures for the starting point. The expansion was driven by operating leverage as revenue scaled, SG&A falling as a share of revenue, and a declining finance cost burden — not by gross margin improvement, which was essentially flat (−40 basis points from 32.7% to 32.3%). As of Q1 FY27, the tailwinds that drove margin expansion are reversing: operating leverage is contracting as the volume cycle moderates into H1 FY27, finance cost improvement is fully played out, and commodity costs are moving adversely at a pace management described as "significantly higher" than Q4's 100 bps hit. Management has no contractual pass-through mechanism; the company absorbs roughly 40–60% of commodity shocks and recovers partially through pricing with a one-quarter lag. If steel, aluminium, and copper prices sustain their current trajectory through H1 FY27, a 150–200 basis-point margin compression from the FY26 12.3% baseline is a credible scenario, which would push margins to 10–11% — technically still within "teens" guidance but well below what +20.5% EPS growth requires. The single segment that could most damage consolidated margins is the HCV truck book: it carries the highest ASPs, the most operating leverage, and is the first segment to see demand deferral when diesel prices rise.

### Quality Check

The largest gap between reported and economic earnings is the FVTPL mark-to-market item on a subsidiary's equity investment portfolio — ₹2,418 crore in FY26, equivalent to 80% of reported PAT. This gap narrowed in the sense that reported PAT understated economic earnings by ~94%, but the item is recurring at varying magnitude: in years when equity markets perform well, this line swings to a gain and makes reported PAT look far larger than operating performance justifies, creating noise in the opposite direction. Management's non-GAAP adjustments — stripping the FVTPL loss and exceptional items to derive Underlying EBITDA and normalised EPS — are conceptually sound and the methodology is fully disclosed. However, the exceptional items include small recurring categories (pension provision appeared in both FY25 and FY26) and the warranty provision is a Key Audit Matter with actual spend diverging from provision, so the "genuinely one-time" characterisation is imperfect. For modelling FY27 normalised earnings, start from Underlying EBITDA (₹10,314 crore, 12.3% margin) and normalised EPS (₹15.91), not from GAAP PAT or reported EPS — the GAAP numbers are structurally too noisy to be useful anchor points.

### Consensus Bar

For the consensus bar to be beaten materially, three things would need to go right simultaneously in FY27: commodity costs would need to ease from their Q1 FY27 trajectory, volumes would need to sustain mid-single-digit domestic growth supported by a healthy H2, and the Iveco deal financing would need to either (a) not close until late FY27 or (b) be structured in a way that keeps incremental interest well below the ~₹3,000–4,000 crore implied by the bridge loan size. The bar is most likely wrong on EPS — too high for the near-term (H1 FY27 will almost certainly disappoint the +20.5% EPS growth assumption embedded in consensus) and potentially too low for the medium term if the commodity cycle turns and Iveco integration proceeds smoothly. The current consensus is anchored to: (a) the continuation of FY26's operating leverage story into FY27, and (b) the absence of significant Iveco financing drag — both assumptions are questionable as of June 2026. The stale Apr-27-2026 consensus has not absorbed the Q4 FY26 beat, the explicit commodity warning management gave on the earnings call, or any post-call analyst revisions.

## 5b. Leverage & Capital Structure

**Trigger B fires:** Net debt / Adjusted EBITDA moved from approximately 0.61x (net debt ₹4,016 crore at FY25 end, against annualised FY25 EBITDA ~₹8,793 crore) to net cash (negative ratio) at FY26 end — a dramatic improvement. However, the forward-looking leverage change is what triggers this section: the Iveco acquisition bridge loan of approximately EUR 3.875 billion (~₹33,250 crore at EUR/INR 86) will, upon deal closure in Q2 FY27, convert TMCV from its current net cash position of ₹13,713 crore (company-defined) to a significant net debt position, representing a net debt change of approximately ₹47,000 crore — a year-over-year swing of more than 2x the FY26 net cash base.

At March 31, 2026, TMCV is a net-cash company. Basic net cash (total debt ₹4,817 crore minus cash ₹6,899 crore) is ₹2,082 crore; the company-defined net cash including liquid investments is ₹13,713 crore. The current net debt / Underlying EBITDA ratio is negative (net cash). [SEBI LODR Consolidated Audited Results, May 13, 2026, Balance Sheet; FY26 Annual Report MD&A p.219]

Upon Iveco deal closure, the single driver of leverage change is the ~EUR 3.875 billion acquisition bridge loan. At a rate of Euribor + 102.5 basis points (approximately 5.5–6.5% all-in, based on current Euribor), annual interest expense on this facility would be approximately EUR 210–250 million (~₹1,800–2,200 crore) on the EUR-denominated portion, plus the impact of the deal collapsing the current net cash buffer. Pro-forma net debt / Underlying EBITDA post-close (using FY26 Underlying EBITDA of ₹10,314 crore as the denominator, and assuming TMCV raises ~EUR 1 billion of equity as separately reported in web sources — which would reduce net debt from the bridge to approximately EUR 2.875 billion, ~₹24,700 crore) would be approximately 2.4x — a leverage ratio that is material but not distress-level, provided Iveco's own EBITDA begins contributing promptly. [FY26 Annual Report Board's Report p.116; FQ4 2026 Earnings Call Transcript, May 13, 2026, G.V. Ramanan; web sources: Yahoo Finance / Automotive World, unverified; web: Autocar Professional, unverified]

The reported leverage ratio for the current period uses company-defined net cash / Underlying EBITDA (management's preferred metric); no GAAP-equivalent leverage ratio is separately disclosed. On a GAAP basis (basic net debt ₹−2,082 crore vs Underlying EBITDA ₹10,314 crore), the company is net cash on both measures at March 31, 2026 — the GAAP-adjusted ratio is also net cash.

Debt maturity profile as of March 31, 2026 on the existing pre-deal structure: total borrowings ₹4,817 crore (non-current ₹1,344 crore; current ₹3,473 crore). Current maturities represent ~72% of total debt, meaning most existing debt matures within 12 months. The weighted-average interest rate is not separately disclosed in the available filings; finance costs were ₹874 crore on an average debt base of approximately ₹7,000 crore (average of FY25 ₹9,156 crore and FY26 ₹4,817 crore), implying a rough all-in rate of approximately 12.5% — consistent with Indian rupee commercial borrowing rates. [SEBI LODR May 13, 2026, Balance Sheet and Cash Flow Statement]

The Iveco bridge loan is a EUR-denominated facility at floating Euribor rates — meaningfully lower in coupon than the company's INR borrowings, but introducing EUR/INR currency risk on a large principal. Post-deal, capital allocation will be constrained: no buybacks are probable given the leverage step-up, the recommended FY26 dividend of ₹4 per share (~₹1,500 crore outflow) may be reviewed for FY27 once deal financing is known, and the equity raise (rights issue or QIP, ~EUR 1 billion equivalent) would be dilutive to per-share EPS. No credit rating agency action on TMCV is noted in the available data pool; the entity is newly listed and may not yet carry standalone external ratings.

**Cross-reference:** Trigger B fires AND the earnings trajectory for FY27 is at risk of inflecting negatively in H1. Accordingly, the "Biggest earnings risk" in Section 1 references both the leverage change (Iveco bridge loan) and the commodity-driven margin deceleration, as required.

## 8. Note To The Final Synthesizer

- **Dominant earnings trend and driver:** TMCV's operating business is in genuine good health — three years of margin expansion (audited at least for FY26), strong cash conversion, and a growing non-cyclical revenue stream. The earnings trend for the CV business itself, viewed in isolation and in isolation from Iveco, is late-cycle expansion that may moderate but is not broken. The complication is that this business will not exist in its current form if Iveco closes in Q2 FY27; the FY27 earnings setup must be modelled as a different, much larger, more leveraged entity.

- **Whether earnings are clean and cash-backed:** Core operating earnings are genuinely cash-backed (negative 54-day cash conversion cycle, CFO/EBITDA ratio structurally above 100%). Reported GAAP earnings are NOT clean — the FVTPL mark-to-market on a subsidiary's equity portfolio produced a ₹2,418 crore non-cash, non-deductible loss in FY26 that made reported PAT (₹3,030 crore) less than half of normalised PAT (~₹5,900 crore). This item will recur at different magnitudes in every future period, making reported PAT and reported EPS unreliable comparison metrics. Use Underlying EBITDA and normalised EPS exclusively.

- **Consensus bar assessment:** The consensus (FY27 normalised EPS ₹19.17, revenue ₹88,136 crore as of Apr-27-2026) is stale, anchored to pre-Q4 actuals, has an unknown analyst count, and does not reflect the Iveco bridge loan. The +20.5% EPS growth embedded in consensus requires either margin expansion (against a commodity headwind) or significant exceptional-item normalisation — the exceptional normalisation case is real (demerger stamp duty ₹962 crore was a genuine one-off), but the Iveco financing costs partially offset it. The bar is technically beatable for the full year if commodity and MENA headwinds prove temporary and Iveco does not close until very late in FY27, but near-term Q1 and Q2 prints will almost certainly disappoint because the seasonal softness and commodity pressure are already documented and confirmed by management as more acute than Q4 FY26.

- **Next-quarter setup and second-quarter look-ahead:** Q1 FY27 (results expected late July/August 2026): balanced with downward bias. Volume indicators (April MHCV retail +20% YoY, e-way bills growing) are constructive, but commodity cost pressure is "significantly higher" than Q4's 100 bps, monsoon seasonality suppresses June freight activity, and the base comparison is the strongest quarter of FY26 (Q4). Q2 FY27: operationally better (monsoon recovery, festive-season setup) but structurally messier — Iveco deal closure expected in Q2, which will introduce bridge loan interest from close date plus deal-related exceptional charges. Per-share EPS may look worse in Q2 even if the underlying CV business recovers. The two-quarter period through Q1–Q2 FY27 is the highest-risk window for consensus disappointment.

- **Top sensitivity variable and its current direction:** India CV wholesale volumes — currently guided as "single-digit growth if not more" for Q1 FY27, moving cautiously and with multiple downside risks (diesel price, monsoon, Operation Sindoor sentiment). A ±10% volume swing produces a ±₹6.57–7.28 EPS impact, equivalent to 41–46% of the FY26 normalised base. This variable is external and uncontrollable; management can only partially offset volume deceleration through pricing and cost discipline.

- **Whether any partial-data cap applied and what it limits:** Yes — consensus setup score is capped at 30/100, reflecting the combination of: (a) no full revision series available (Capital IQ estimates workbook inaccessible, triggering the "no revision history" cap at 60), and (b) staleness of the Apr-27-2026 consensus (pre-Q4 results, pre-Iveco clarity, unknown analyst count), which effectively reduces consensus quality to near-absent. The combined cap of 30 limits the synthesis's ability to assess the beat/miss setup with confidence; all consensus-dependent conclusions should be treated as directional, not precise.

- **Biggest missing data point:** The Iveco acquisition financing structure — specifically, the final equity/debt split, the bridge loan rate, and any Iveco EBITDA contribution disclosure. This single data point, when disclosed, will force a material reset of the FY27 earnings model and may change the synthesis verdict from "Mixed" to either "Earnings decelerating" (if deal adds more interest than Iveco EBITDA offsets in year one) or "Earnings stable" (if the deal is structured with less near-term dilution than feared). The Q1 FY27 Underlying EBITDA margin print is the second most valuable data point.

- **What would change the earnings verdict:** See Section 7. In brief: a Q1 FY27 EBITDA margin above 12% (commodity headwind contained) would tilt toward "Earnings stable"; a confirmed Iveco financing with ~₹3,000+ crore annual interest would tilt toward "Earnings decelerating."

Two Critical flags: (1) commodity prices moving adversely entering FY27 with confirmed escalation in Q1; (2) Iveco EUR 3.8 billion bridge loan unmodelled in stale consensus.
