# Earnings Sensitivity — TMCV (Tata Motors Limited, formerly TML Commercial Vehicles Limited)

**Reporting regime:** India / SEBI-LODR. Reporting standard: Ind AS. Currency: INR (₹ crores). Fiscal year ends March 31. Shares: 3,682,331,373 fully diluted. FY26 baseline: Underlying EBITDA ₹10,314 cr (12.3% margin); Underlying EBIT ₹8,538 cr; Reported EPS ₹8.23; Normalised EPS ₹15.91 (S&P Capital IQ adjusted, excludes FVTPL loss and exceptional items).

*Note on EPS base used in this analysis:* All EPS sensitivity impacts are computed against the normalised EPS base of ₹15.91, which removes the non-cash FVTPL fair-value loss (₹2,418 cr) and exceptional items (₹1,428 cr). This is the metric on which analysts and management are effectively measuring operating performance. The reported EPS of ₹8.23 is lower solely because of these non-recurring adjustments. Tax rate used: 28% (effective normalised rate; the reported effective rate of ~50% is distorted by the non-deductible FVTPL loss).

---

## 1. Variable Selection

Six variables were selected from the upstream driver tables (02_revenue-drivers.md and 03_margin-drivers.md) based on their magnitude ratings and directional importance.

From the revenue driver table, the highest-magnitude drivers are: (1) India CV industry demand / wholesale volumes — rated "High" and directly controlling 70–80% of revenue; (2) fleet operator diesel price sensitivity — rated "High" as the primary demand brake on transporter purchase decisions; and (3) pricing / ASP discipline — rated "Mid-High," with management explicitly calling out the partial pass-through constraint.

From the margin driver table, the highest-magnitude cost drivers are: (4) input commodity costs (steel, aluminium, copper) — rated "High," flagged as the single biggest margin driver in Section 8 of 03_margin-drivers.md; and (5) operating leverage / volume growth — rated "High," identified as responsible for the bulk of the 550 bps EBITDA expansion from FY23 to FY26.

One additional variable is drawn from both the margin driver table and the business-model external-dependency output (10_external-dependency.md): (6) Iveco acquisition financing cost — rated "High (if deal closes)" in 03_margin-drivers.md and confirmed as a material transformation event in the external dependency report. This variable is binary and near-term, with the acquisition expected to close in Q2 FY27.

FX translation, government capex policy, VAVE savings, and D&A trends were considered but excluded because their individual first-order EPS impact is smaller than any of the six above, or because (in VAVE's case) the effect is embedded within commodity cost movement and is not independently moveable.

## 4. The Single Highest-Sensitivity Variable

India CV wholesale volumes are the single biggest mover of TMCV earnings. A ±10% swing in total volumes — consistent with what the Indian CV industry has historically delivered across cycle turns — translates to an estimated ±₹6.57 to ₹7.28 per share of EPS impact, which is 41–46% of the normalised EPS base of ₹15.91. This variable is external: TMCV holds roughly 39–40% market share in domestic CV sales, which means industry demand, not company decisions, sets the volume ceiling. Management has confirmed single-digit growth guidance for Q1 FY27, and explicitly flagged the combination of diesel price pressure and monsoon seasonality as the two factors that could push the number toward the low single digits or negative.

The direction right now is cautious. The underlying freight fundamentals (e-way bill generation, transporter fleet utilisation, infrastructure capex) remain constructive, and the Indonesia export order (70,000 units over FY27) provides a partial volume buffer. But H1 FY27 faces both seasonal softness and a diesel price headwind. For the bear case to materialise — a 10% domestic volume contraction — two things would need to happen simultaneously: diesel prices would have to rise materially enough to defer fleet buying decisions, and government infrastructure execution would have to slow in a way that reduces tipper demand. The 2% April 2026 price increase, if it stifles demand, could itself be a partial trigger for the bear case.

## 5. Interaction Effects

Two compounding interactions are material.

First, diesel prices and India CV volumes move in the same direction: when diesel rises, both transporter profitability and TMCV volumes fall simultaneously. This pair reinforces the bear case. If diesel rises 15%, the demand effect (Variable 4 bear) combines with a direct volume impact (Variable 2 bear), and the two are not independent — the diesel price IS the mechanism through which volumes deteriorate. An analyst should not add the full diesel-demand EPS impact on top of the full volume-sensitivity EPS impact; they overlap. In the bear case scenario where both are simultaneously adverse, the incremental diesel impact (once the volume decline is already captured) is primarily through further compression on the remaining fleet operator demand elasticity.

Second, commodity costs and pricing discipline interact: when input costs spike (Variable 1 bear), the company's ability to pass through costs (Variable 5) becomes the buffer. If commodity costs rise 200 bps AND the company cannot recover through pricing (net pricing = 0%), the combined EBITDA impact is the sum of both variables, totalling approximately −₹4.59 EPS in the simultaneous bear case — which compresses the normalised EPS of ₹15.91 by 29%. This compounding is real and was partially visible in Q4 FY26 when a 100 bps commodity impact was absorbed without full pricing recovery. Management has explicitly acknowledged this trade-off.

Volume growth (Variable 2) and operating leverage (embedded in the volume impact calculation above) are mechanically linked: TMCV has a significant fixed cost base (employee cost ₹5,804 cr = 6.9% of revenue, largely fixed; other expenses ₹11,689 cr partly fixed). When volumes fall, fixed costs do not shrink proportionally, so the EBITDA margin declines faster than revenue. This is why the bear EPS impact of a 10% volume decline (−₹6.57) is proportionately larger than the revenue decline alone would imply.

## 6. Non-Linear Or Asymmetric Risks

Three asymmetric or non-linear risks exist.

**Operating deleverage on volume downturns.** TMCV operates 11 manufacturing facilities with meaningful fixed costs. At the current scale, a 10% revenue decline does not produce a 10% EBITDA decline; it produces a significantly larger proportional EBITDA decline because fixed costs must still be covered. The estimated incremental EBITDA margin on new volume is 35–40% (Inference from management's commentary that "operating leverage plays out as we scale" and from the historical FY23–FY26 experience where revenue grew ~27% and EBITDA grew ~100%). On the way down, this leverage is reversed. A 10% revenue decline (~₹8,386 cr) at a 35% incremental margin erases ~₹2,935 cr of EBITDA — a 28% EBITDA decline against a 10% revenue decline. This is the most material non-linearity in the model.

**Commodity cost pass-through lag and asymmetry.** When input costs rise, the company absorbs the cost first (within the quarter) and attempts partial pass-through in the following quarter. When input costs fall, the company does not automatically return the benefit to customers — it retains the margin improvement. The result is that commodity inflation creates an immediate and certain EBITDA drag, while commodity deflation creates a delayed and potentially partial EBITDA benefit. The downside is faster than the upside. Management confirmed this directly: "we don't want to impact the demand momentum" when declining to pass on the full April 2026 commodity increase, meaning the direction of asymmetry is asymmetric against the company in the short run (absorb costs now, recover partially later).

**Iveco threshold effect.** The Iveco acquisition, if it closes, introduces approximately ₹1,286 cr of net incremental annual interest expense in year one (before any Iveco EBITDA contribution is consolidated). This is not a gradual effect — it is a step change the moment the deal closes. It would approximately reverse the entire ₹874 cr finance cost saving achieved in FY26, and then add further burden. The key risk is that the bridge financing is at floating rates, which means the actual interest cost could be higher than the ₹2,160 cr estimate if rates rise. This is a threshold-type risk: if the deal does not close, there is no impact; if it closes, the impact is immediate and large. The net earnings effect depends on Iveco's own EBITDA margin, which management has not disclosed in sufficient detail to allow a net EPS estimate with Medium confidence or better.

## 7. Earnings Volatility Score

**62 / 100 — higher = worse**

TMCV's earnings are materially sensitive to two variables that are largely outside management's control: India CV industry demand and commodity input costs. The dominant revenue driver (volumes) can swing ±10% in a single year based on the freight cycle and diesel prices, producing a ±₹6.57–7.28 EPS impact against a ₹15.91 normalised base. This means earnings can move ±40% from base from this one variable alone. Commodity costs add another ±₹2.95 bear EPS hit. Together, the two variables in their combined bear case can wipe out roughly half the normalised EPS without any company-specific failure. The score is 62 rather than higher because: TMCV has a strong net-cash balance sheet (₹13,713 cr, which means no covenant risk or liquidity threat), growing non-cyclical revenues that act as a partial earnings buffer through the cycle, and a demonstrated management capability (550 bps margin expansion over FY23–FY26) to use pricing discipline and VAVE to absorb part of external shocks. The score would be lower (more stable) if more than 30% of revenue came from non-cyclical downstream rather than the current minority share.
