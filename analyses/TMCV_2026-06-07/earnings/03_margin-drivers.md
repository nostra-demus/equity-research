# Margin Drivers — TMCV (Tata Motors Limited, formerly TML Commercial Vehicles Limited)

**Reporting regime:** India / SEBI-LODR. Reporting standard: Ind AS. Currency: INR (₹ crores). Fiscal year ends March 31. FY26 = full year ended March 31, 2026. FY25 comparative = nine months only (June 23, 2024 to March 31, 2025) — not directly comparable. Transcript available: FQ4 2026 earnings call, May 13, 2026 (S&P Capital IQ).

---

## 1. Segment Decomposition Status

TMCV is a single-segment business for practical purposes. The Commercial Vehicle segment contributes 98.5% of revenue (₹82,611 cr of ₹83,855 cr total) and over 100% of operating segment profit (₹8,727 cr of ₹8,414 cr total net segment results, with the Corporate/Unallocable drag of −₹448 cr pulling the total below the CV segment alone). The Others segment (insurance broking) contributes 1.2% of revenue and 1.6% of profit. The CODM reviews the automotive operations as one segment under Ind AS 108.

Segment-level EBITDA and capex are not disclosed — only segment results at the EBIT-equivalent level. Underlying EBITDA (₹10,314 cr, 12.3% margin) and Underlying EBIT (₹8,538 cr, 10.2% margin) are disclosed at consolidated level only.

**Conclusion:** Segment-level margin decomposition beyond the consolidated CV segment is not possible from disclosed data. All analysis proceeds at the consolidated CV-group level. The business-model segment-map (analyses/TMCV_2026-06-07/business-model/03_segment-map.md) confirms this assessment.

Within the CV segment, management discloses product-line revenue (HCV ₹37,199 cr, ILMCV ₹11,212 cr, CV Passenger ₹8,807 cr, SCV/Pickup ₹7,558 cr) but not product-line profitability. Product-mix within the CV segment is therefore treated as a driver but its margin impact per product line cannot be precisely quantified.

**Pass-through lag:** Price increases lag input cost increases by approximately one quarter, and the pass-through is partial. When Q4 FY26 saw a 100 basis point variable cost impact from elevated steel, aluminium, and copper prices, the company took a 2% price increase in April 2026 (for Q1 FY27) but explicitly chose not to pass on the full amount. Management stated: "we have decided to not pass on the entire commodity increases... we don't want to impact the demand momentum." [FQ4 2026 Earnings Call, May 13, 2026, Girish Wagh, Q&A] This implies the pass-through covers roughly half the commodity shock in any given quarter, with the balance absorbed through Value Analysis and Value Engineering (VAVE). The business-model value-chain output (analyses/TMCV_2026-06-07/business-model/06_value-chain.md) characterises this as 40–60% pass-through, ~1 quarter lag.

## 4. Margin Walk — Which Margin Level Matters Most?

For TMCV, **Underlying EBITDA margin** is the primary metric to track.

The reason: TMCV is a capital-intensive manufacturer with significant depreciation from 11 manufacturing facilities and ongoing product development capitalisation. D&A is real and recurring but can vary quarter to quarter based on asset completion timing (D&A fell in FY26 partly due to amortisation completion for some assets — a non-recurring tailwind). Finance costs have collapsed with the debt paydown (from 1.9% of revenue in the nine-month FY25 to 1.0% in FY26) and are now approaching zero as the company moves to net cash; this makes reported EBIT and PBT less useful as comparators going forward since the interest benefit is fading. Reported EBITDA is distorted by the large non-cash FVTPL fair-value loss on a subsidiary's equity investment portfolio (₹2,418 cr in FY26, nil in FY25 nine-month). Underlying EBITDA, which strips these non-operating items, is the metric management guides to, analysts ask about, and which best reflects operating performance.

EBIT margin is also important but Underlying EBIT is a better version of it than reported EBIT, for the same reasons. Gross margin is a useful secondary check on commodity pass-through dynamics but the company's operating leverage story plays out below the gross line.

## 8. The Single Biggest Margin Driver

**Commodity input costs — specifically steel, aluminium, and copper.**

At 67.7% of revenue, material costs are by far the largest cost line in TMCV's P&L. The company has no contractual pass-through mechanism with customers. When input costs rise, the company must choose between absorbing the cost (compressing gross margin) or taking price increases that risk damaging demand. In Q4 FY26 alone, elevated commodity prices caused a 100 basis point variable cost impact, and management explicitly declined to pass on the full amount: "we have decided to not pass on the entire commodity increases... we don't want to impact the demand momentum." [FQ4 2026 Earnings Call, May 13, 2026, Girish Wagh] By the time of the same call, management confirmed commodity pressures in Q1 FY27 were "significantly higher" than Q4 FY26. Ashok Leyland (the direct competitor) confirmed the same dynamic on its FQ4 2026 call (May 28, 2026), with steel specifically called out as the predominant source of cost pressure entering Q1 FY27.

The current direction of this driver is a **headwind**. If commodity costs — particularly steel — remain elevated through H1 FY27 and TMCV absorbs even 50–100 bps more than it can pass through in price, gross margin will compress and EBITDA margin will follow directly. The 550 bps structural expansion achieved over FY23–FY26 depended partly on stable-to-falling commodities and rising volumes; both conditions are now under pressure simultaneously as FY27 begins.
