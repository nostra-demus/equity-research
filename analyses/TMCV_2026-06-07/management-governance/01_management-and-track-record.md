## 3. Stated Strategy & Targets

Management's stated strategy has four pillars: (1) grow the core domestic CV franchise by deepening product range across all tonnage categories, launching next-generation platforms (including 17 new models in FY26), and expanding the dealer and service network; (2) grow the noncyclical aftermarket and telematics business (Fleet Edge, genuine parts, workshop services) faster than the cyclical vehicle business — management explicitly targets a noncyclical CAGR at 2.7x the cyclical CAGR; (3) grow international exports (52 countries, led by South Asia and Africa) with Indonesia (70,000-unit order now in delivery) and targeted Middle East expansion; (4) acquire Iveco Group N.V. (€3.8 billion all-cash tender offer announced July 30, 2025, targeted to close Q2 FY27) to build a global commercial vehicle group.

Explicit checkable targets set by management as of May 13, 2026:
- FY27 capex: 2%–4% of revenue (same range as FY26 actual of ~₹3,000 crore)
- FY27 EBITDA margin: "Teens" (deliberately corrected from "mid-teens" on call)
- Q1 FY27 India CV TIV growth: "single-digit if not more"
- Net zero emissions target: 2045
- Iveco deal closure: Q2 FY27, subject to France and Spain regulatory approvals

[FQ4 FY26 Earnings Call Transcript, May 13, 2026, prepared remarks and Q&A, p.4–10; FY26 Annual Report, MD&A, p.212–222]

## 5. Management Read

Girish Wagh brings genuine sector depth — 33 years at Tata Motors with 8 years running the CV business before the demerger — and the FY26 operating record confirms his team delivered on the commitments within its control: deleveraging, EBITDA margin expansion, H2 recovery, and noncyclical revenue growth. The CFO Ramanan is similarly embedded, having served as Group Controller in the broader Tata Motors group before taking the CFO seat at TMCV.

The track record as a standalone issuer spans only two quarters, which limits how far promise-vs-delivery can be scored. Within those two quarters, both were beats. The full-year EPS miss versus consensus (−10.7%) was driven by the TMF Holdings subsidiary drag (₹2,478 crore FVTPL loss) and commodity cost absorption — partially a structural issue inherited at demerger, not operational failure by the management team.

The single biggest management signal is the Iveco acquisition: a ₹41,691 crore all-cash bid at 3.3x TMCV's own book equity, funded by a bridge loan on a balance sheet that took three years to get to net cash. The deal rationale is narrative-only, with no synergy targets or post-deal return thresholds disclosed, reversing the very deleveraging that management rightly highlighted as a FY26 achievement. Whether this is disciplined expansion or an empire-building step will only be answerable after deal close and integration — but the absence of checkable financial commitments around the Iveco deal is a weakness in the current record.
