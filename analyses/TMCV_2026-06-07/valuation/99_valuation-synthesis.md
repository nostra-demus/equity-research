# Valuation Module — TMCV (Synthesis)

## Abstract

TMCV is modestly undervalued relative to what its near-peer Ashok Leyland implies — but the apparent deep discount on EV/EBITDA is almost entirely a capital-structure artefact, not a genuine market rebuke of the business. The base-case fair-value range, derived by reconciling the SOTP and own-history multiples methods (the two most reliable for this company), is approximately ₹380–430 per share, roughly 3–17% above the current price of ₹369.15. What the current price embeds is conservative: a reverse-DCF at a 13% discount rate requires only ~1.7% FCF CAGR over ten years to justify the price — well below the 5–7% revenue growth the company has demonstrated and management has guided. The bear-case value on a pre-Iveco standalone basis is approximately ₹300–346, placing the downside at roughly 6–19% from today's price. The single dominant risk is the pending ₹41,691 crore Iveco acquisition, whose financing structure is unresolved; if funded entirely by debt, intrinsic value per share could fall to approximately ₹66, making the true downside binary rather than incremental. On current evidence, the business is priced at fair-to-modest value with a real but deal-contingent downside.

## 3. Reconciliation

The methods produce a wide range of implied values. The DCF (₹147–190/share) is an extreme outlier on the low side. The peer multiples method (₹524–679/share pre-Iveco) is a high outlier. The SOTP (₹346–411) and own-history mean reversion (₹423–465) are the two methods that agree most closely and are most grounded in observable data for this business.

**High-to-low spread:** ₹147 (DCF bear) to ₹679 (peer bull, pre-Iveco) — a spread of 362%, far exceeding the 40% reconciliation threshold. This disagreement is the central finding of this module and must not be averaged silently.

**Reconciliation:**

The DCF is the lowest-confidence method here. Three specific issues push it down: (1) a very high WACC (17.71%) driven by a 1.59 beta estimated from only six months of listed history — this beta is almost certainly overstated for a company with >95% India domestic revenues and a long-standing business franchised through the old Tata Motors group; (2) a normalized EBITDA margin of 10%–11%, which underweights the structural improvement the company has already demonstrated (three-year EBITDA margin expansion of 550 basis points, from ~7.8% to 12.3%); and (3) the normalization of the FY26 working capital inflow, which reduces the FCF base from ₹12,438 crore (company-defined) to ₹6,245 crore — a conservative correction that may be larger than warranted. The DCF intrinsic value of ₹147–190 is flagged as a floor — the lower bound of what the business generates at mid-cycle with conservative assumptions and a high discount rate — not as a central estimate.

The peer multiples range (₹524–679) is the most optimistic because it applies a multiple range (18×–23.5× EV/EBITDA) that partly reflects Ashok Leyland's inflated consolidated EV/EBITDA of 23.5×, itself driven by the captive-finance arm's net debt of ₹46,856 crore. The true pure-play CV peer multiple for Ashok Leyland on a debt-neutral basis is closer to 12×–15×, which aligns with TMCV's current trading level. The peer method is useful for establishing the upper bound but should not be the primary anchor.

The SOTP (₹346–411) is the most reliable for TMCV. It uses a single named comparable (Ashok Leyland's EV/EBITDA of ~12.2× as the floor), applies it to TMCV's Underlying EBITDA (₹10,314 crore), and cross-checks against the current implied multiple (12.5× at ₹369.15). The own-history mean reversion (₹423–465) uses a different lens but produces a comparable midpoint. Together, they bound a tight range of ₹380–430 as the base case.

**Reconciled fair-value range:** ₹380–430 per share (base), ₹300–346 (bear, excluding Iveco debt scenario), ₹520–530 (bull, assuming Iveco risk resolves favorably). Methods broadly agree on the base-case range once the DCF and peer outliers are explained. The disagreement between DCF and SOTP / own-history (₹165 vs ₹390 midpoint) exceeds 40%; this trips the confidence cap (see Section 4).

## 5. Fair-Value Summary

The base-case fair-value range of ₹380–430 per share rests primarily on the SOTP (collapsed to a CV single-multiple read at 12×–14× Underlying EBITDA of ₹10,314 crore) cross-checked against own-history mean reversion (EV/EBITDA mean of ~14.9× implies ₹423–433). These two methods agree within a narrow range and are the most reliable for a single-segment Indian commercial vehicle manufacturer with six months of listed history.

At ₹369.15, the price implies only ~1.7% FCF CAGR over ten years — a conservative assumption the reverse-DCF shows is inconsistent with the company's recent operating trajectory (5–7% revenue growth, 550 basis points of EBITDA margin expansion, ₹12,438 crore FCF in FY26). The implied expectations are achievable from the base business, meaning the current price embeds a meaningful discount for the Iveco uncertainty rather than reflecting a fundamental problem with the CV franchise.

The bear-case value is ₹300–346 — roughly 6–19% below today's price. This is the floor from the SOTP at the reported EBITDA multiple (12× on ₹8,641 crore reported EBITDA, which absorbs the non-cash FVTPL loss) plus net cash. The margin of safety to the bear case is narrow in percentage terms (single digits to low teens), but this bear case excludes the Iveco debt scenario. If the Iveco acquisition closes fully debt-funded at ₹41,691 crore, the equity bridge deteriorates dramatically — the DCF shows an event-risk floor of approximately ₹66/share. This is not a base-case outcome, but it is a modelled, non-speculative outcome contingent on a known pending event. The downside risk score of 72/100 (higher = worse) reflects this binary rather than the gradual decline suggested by the bear-to-current-price spread alone.

The apparent cheapness in EV/EBITDA terms versus peers deserves scrutiny as a potential value trap. The 47% EV/EBITDA discount to Ashok Leyland is almost entirely explained by capital structure (Ashok Leyland's EV is inflated by captive-finance net debt); on a debt-neutral basis the discount narrows to 11%. The RF-OWN-004 amber flag from the governance module is relevant here: the Tata Sons-controlled parent initiated the TMF Holdings merger (which transferred ₹2,418 crore of non-cash losses into the listed CV entity in FY26 alone) and the Iveco acquisition (which consumed three years of deliberate deleveraging with no disclosed financial targets). These are concrete examples of a controlling shareholder taking actions that benefit the group at the margin but impose costs on minority shareholders. Persistent cheapness in this ownership structure is partly structural and may not self-correct through re-rating; the verdict of "Modestly undervalued" is the ceiling under this ownership context.
