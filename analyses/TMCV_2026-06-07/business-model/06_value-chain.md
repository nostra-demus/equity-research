## 2. Input Cost Pass-Through

TMCV's ability to pass rising commodity costs to customers is real but structurally limited. In FY26, material costs held at 67.7% of revenue — exactly flat with the prior comparative period despite higher steel, aluminium, and copper prices — because the company absorbed part of the increase through cost engineering and mix improvement rather than price alone. [FY26 Integrated Annual Report (Ind AS), MD&A, p.216: "Material costs, as a percentage of revenue, broadly remained in line with the previous year despite an increase in input costs, supported by improved realisations and operational efficiencies."]

The company has no disclosed contractual escalator or index-linked pass-through clauses in customer contracts. In practice, it takes selective, measured price increases rather than full pass-through: during Q4 FY26, the company faced a 100 basis point (1 percentage point) variable cost impact from elevated commodity costs and announced a 2% price increase for April 2026 — but explicitly declined to pass on the full increase. [FQ4 FY26 earnings call transcript, May 13, 2026, Girish Wagh: "we have decided to not pass on the entire commodity increases… we don't want to impact the demand momentum."] The company uses forward contracts and options on key commodities for short-term hedging and enters long-term supply agreements with key suppliers for price visibility. [FY26 Integrated Annual Report (Ind AS), Risk Management, p.40] The lag between input cost moves and price action is approximately one quarter, and the pass-through is partial — typically offsetting 40–60% of the commodity spike, with the rest absorbed internally through Value Analysis and Value Engineering (VAVE). [FY26 Integrated Annual Report (Ind AS), Risk Management, p.40: "VAVE is an institutionalised and continuous programme focused on improving product cost structures."]

## 3. Customer Pricing Power

TMCV has moderate pricing power relative to most Indian industrial manufacturers, supported by its 35.7% overall market share (FY26, VAHAN registration data) and its 55% share in HCVs — the highest in a decade. [FY26 Integrated Annual Report (Ind AS), MD&A, p.213] These market positions give it a degree of price-setting credibility in a category where customers buy on total cost of ownership rather than sticker price alone. Average selling prices in the truck segment are rising on a year-on-year basis, which management confirmed is a genuine price effect rather than a mix shift for that segment. [FQ4 FY26 earnings call, May 13, 2026, Girish Wagh: "in trucks, there is a healthy increase in ASPs on a Y-o-Y basis."] EBITDA margins expanded from 7.8% in FY23 to 12.3% in FY26 — a 550 basis point structural improvement over three years — demonstrating that some pricing benefit has been retained. [FQ4 FY26 earnings call, May 13, 2026, G.V. Ramanan prepared remarks: "EBITDA margins have expanded from 7.8% in '23, 13.2% in FY '26. That's nearly 550 bps of structural improvement over 3 years."] However, the commercial vehicle market is characterised as "highly competitive" in the company's own risk disclosure, and management explicitly acknowledged that protecting demand momentum limits how aggressively it can raise prices: "we don't want to completely destroy or disturb the demand growth momentum." [FQ4 FY26 earnings call, May 13, 2026, Girish Wagh, Q&A on commodity pass-through] The blended ASP printed slightly lower in FY26 due to vans and electric bus mix effects, confirming that headline ASP is not a reliable proxy for underlying pricing discipline at the consolidated level.

---

## 4. Economic Control Verdict

**Mixed** — TMCV controls its economics on the downstream side (brand strength, 35.7% market share, growing aftermarket, 1 million+ connected vehicles creating switching costs), but faces structurally constrained bargaining power on the input side (commodities are globally priced, material costs are a fixed 67.7% of revenue, no contractual pass-throughs, and the company explicitly absorbs part of commodity spikes to protect volume). The 550 basis point EBITDA margin expansion over three years shows the downstream advantage is real and compounding, but the company's own admission that full commodity pass-through is "out of question" because it would damage demand confirms that upstream cost exposure remains a persistent structural constraint on how much of the value chain economics the company can actually capture.

---

## 5. The Single Biggest Bargaining Risk

**Upstream commodity costs** — steel, aluminium, and copper together constitute a large share of the 67.7% material cost ratio; if sustained commodity inflation exceeds what VAVE and partial pricing can absorb, EBITDA margins compress directly with no contractual protection.
