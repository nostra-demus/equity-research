## 3. Runway

| Metric | Value |
|---|---:|
| Total committed liquidity (₹ cr) | 16,798 |
| Annual FCF — CFO minus total capex (₹ cr) | 12,733 |
| Near-term uses over next 12 months (₹ cr) | 8,289 |
| **FCF surplus over near-term uses (₹ cr)** | **4,444** |
| FCF as % of near-term uses | 154% |

**Formula:** Annual FCF (₹12,733 cr) minus near-term uses (₹8,289 cr) = annual surplus of ₹4,444 crore.

FCF covers all near-term uses by a factor of 1.54×. There is no finite runway to compute — FCF generation alone exceeds all obligations due in the next 12 months, without drawing a single rupee from the cash or revolver. The liquidity pool of ₹16,798 crore is an additional layer of protection on top of the FCF surplus.

**FCF quality note:** The FY26 FCF (₹12,733 cr computed, ₹12,438 cr company-defined) was verified against the cash flow statement (SEBI LODR, May 13, 2026). The CFO-to-Underlying-EBITDA ratio was 145% in FY26 — significantly above 100% — driven by the deeply negative working capital cycle (−54 days). The earnings-quality agent (analyses/TMCV_2026-06-07/earnings/06_earnings-quality.md) rated earnings quality at 72/100 and found no accrual concerns. The ₹6,657 cr working capital inflow in FY26 was the largest component inflating CFO above EBITDA; this includes ₹3,265 cr of customer advances (contract liabilities) that partially reverse as deliveries occur. The normalized FCF — stripping out the non-recurring portion of the customer-advance build — is approximately ₹10,000–₹11,000 cr. Even at this lower level, FCF still covers all near-term uses comfortably.

**Company-defined FCF cross-check:** The company's own FCF definition (₹12,438 cr) adds interest received (₹506 cr) and investee dividends (₹95 cr), and deducts equity investments in core auto entities (₹138 cr), relative to our (CFO − total capex) measure of ₹12,733 cr. The two figures are close; both are used here interchangeably as the FCF measure, with the more conservative (company-defined ₹12,438 cr) used where a single number is required.

### Seasonality / Peak Liquidity Need (Hard Check)

The FY26 quarterly revenue distribution was approximately Q1 ~18%, Q2 ~23%, Q3 ~26%, Q4 ~31% — heavily H2-weighted, with Q4 accounting for nearly one-third of full-year revenue. This pattern is consistent with India's infrastructure-spending and freight-cycle seasonality. However, only one full fiscal year of audited standalone TMCV data exists. One year is insufficient to confirm this as a structural seasonal pattern rather than a cycle-specific effect (the FY26 H2 was boosted by a recovery from H1 supply-chain disruptions per the earnings call).

The potential peak working capital need — the maximum intra-year cash outflow from working capital build before H2 receipts peak — is not separately quantified in the available filings. Given the negative cash conversion cycle (−54 days), TMCV collects cash from customers before paying suppliers, which limits the severity of a seasonal cash trough relative to a positive-WC business.

**Ruling:** Peak working-capital need not separately disclosed. Given the negative cash conversion cycle and the ₹16,798 cr committed liquidity pool — more than twice total annual near-term obligations — a peak trough that materially threatens liquidity is not plausible on any reasonable assumption. However, this cannot be formally stress-tested until multiple years of intra-year cash-flow data are available.

## 4. Sources and Uses Bridge

As of March 31, 2026, TMCV's internal sources — operating cash flow alone — cover all near-term obligations with ₹4,444 crore to spare. The business generates approximately ₹35–₹41 crore of CFO per day (₹14,981 crore CFO over 365 days). Near-term debt maturities of ₹3,657 crore represent roughly 88–104 days of operating cash inflow. No external access to refinancing markets, asset sales, or revolver drawings is required to meet the next 12 months of obligations under the current structure.

Of the ₹16,798 crore usable committed liquidity pool, ₹13,048 crore is already in hand (cash and liquid investments), and ₹3,750 crore is the undrawn committed revolver. The ₹13,048 crore in-hand liquidity alone exceeds the ₹8,289 crore of near-term uses — meaning that even in a scenario where FCF fell to zero, the existing cash and liquid investments would cover all uses for the next 12 months with ₹4,759 crore remaining.

The split: approximately 57% of liquidity coverage comes from already-in-hand cash (₹13,048 cr ÷ ₹8,289 cr = 1.57× coverage from cash alone). The remaining FCF is an additional layer that does not need to materialize for the near-term picture to be safe.

**Critical forward warning:** This analysis is a snapshot of the pre-Iveco balance sheet. The EUR 3.8 billion (~₹41,691 crore) all-cash Iveco acquisition (targeted Q2 FY27 close) will, if completed, consume the entire liquid pool and draw the committed acquisition facility, converting TMCV from a net-cash entity into a significantly net-debt entity. TMCV's CRISIL/ICRA/CARE AA+ Stable ratings have been maintained on the current structure; S&P has noted the deal could be credit-neutral if integration proceeds to plan. The liquidity runway analysis for FY27 post-Iveco will be a completely different picture — that scenario belongs to the stress-test agent (06).

## 5. Liquidity Read

TMCV's liquidity runway in the current (pre-Iveco) structure is not a finite number of months — annual FCF of approximately ₹12,400–₹12,700 crore covers all near-term uses of ₹8,289 crore with a ₹4,400+ crore surplus, and the existing cash pool of ₹13,048 crore would cover all near-term uses for roughly 19 months even if FCF fell to zero. The single biggest near-term liquidity risk is not the existing debt structure but the Iveco acquisition: a EUR 3.8 billion all-cash deal that, if closed in Q2 FY27, will erase the net-cash position and the liquidity surplus at a stroke, leaving TMCV dependent on the FCF of the combined business (including Iveco's European operations) to service a substantially larger debt load. Until that transaction closes or is withdrawn, the current liquidity position is straightforwardly strong — but the runway clock starts fresh the moment the deal clears.
