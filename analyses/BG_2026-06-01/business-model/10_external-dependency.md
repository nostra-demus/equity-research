# External Dependency Check — BG (Bunge Global SA)

Scope note: Bunge is a global agribusiness — oilseed crushing, refining, grain merchandising, and milling, post the July 2025 Viterra combination. It buys agricultural commodities, processes them, and sells the products. Earnings are the spread (crush margin / merchandising margin), not a fixed selling price. That makes external variables the dominant driver by construction, and management's job is largely to manage exposure around them, not to set them.

This score is INVERTED: higher = WORSE (more dangerous external dependence). All bands below follow that direction.

Caveat: these are point-in-time mark-to-market sensitivities on hedged net positions, not earnings sensitivities to the full crush-margin or biofuel-mandate cycle. The disclosed FX and rate figures look modest precisely because Bunge hedges; the un-hedgeable trend in crush margins and biofuel policy is what actually moves EPS, and that is not captured by a 10% VaR-style table. The clearest earnings sensitivity is in the transcript, not the 10-K: a single quarter's guidance moved from ~$0.80–0.90 to ~$2.00 on the RVO decision and margin environment.

## 3. Classification

**Mostly externally driven.**

The product is a spread, and the spread is set by commodity prices, crush economics, biofuel mandates, weather-driven crop sizes, and trade flows — all explicitly "outside of our control" in the company's own words. The proof is in the numbers, not adjectives:
- Soybean Processing & Refining segment EBIT: $2,222M (2023) → $872M (2024, -61%) → $1,225M (2025, +40%). Swings of this size in a year, on a stable asset base, are margin-environment swings, not management swings. [FY25 10-K, p.36–37]
- Consolidated net income attributable to Bunge: $1,137M (2024) → $816M (2025); diluted EPS $7.99 → $4.91, "primarily due to lower" segment results. [FY25 10-K, p.34]
- Q1 2026 guidance swung >100% (~$0.85 → ~$2.00) in two months on the EPA RVO decision. [Q1 2026 transcript, Q&A]

Management does hold real levers, which is why this is not the worst band: a centralized, VaR/drawdown/volumetric-limited hedging framework overseen by a Board ERM committee and a Management Risk Committee; geographic diversification across northern/southern hemispheres that smooths seasonality; the ability to suspend or reduce production at facilities in weak-demand periods; freight, energy, FX, and rate hedging that demonstrably shrink the disclosed sensitivities. But hedging dampens volatility around the trend — it does not set the trend. When the crush curve inverts or a mandate slips, Bunge's earnings move with it. The levers manage the ride; they do not pick the destination.

## 4. External Dependency Risk Score

**72 / 100** (INVERTED: higher = WORSE).

Band: 61–80 — "Mostly externally driven, limited management levers."

Reasoning: Three of the highest-impact variables — commodity/crush margins, biofuel policy, and weather/crop size — are rated High and are the primary EPS drivers, with a documented >100% single-quarter guidance swing on policy alone and ~2–3x year-to-year segment EBIT swings. That pins the score into the "mostly externally driven" band. It is not pushed to 81–100 (pure pass-through) because Bunge demonstrably uses real, disclosed levers — formal VaR-limited hedging, hemispheric diversification, capacity flexing, and FX/rate/freight hedges that cut the disclosed 10% sensitivities to modest or "not material" — and because the segment EBIT, while volatile, has not gone negative across the 2023–2025 window. The score sits in the lower-middle of the "mostly externally driven" band: dominated by external forces, but with a genuine, institutionalized mitigation apparatus.

## 5. The Single Biggest Lever

Biofuel demand policy (the EPA RVO and equivalent mandates in Brazil/Indonesia/EU): a 20% adverse move in mandated/blended biofuel volumes would compress soy- and canola-oil demand and crush margins across the core Processing & Refining segments — the same mechanism that, in reverse, doubled Q1 2026 guidance from ~$0.85 to ~$2.00 — and would do the most damage to consolidated earnings.
