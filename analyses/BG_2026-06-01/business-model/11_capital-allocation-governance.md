# Capital Allocation & Governance — BG

Bunge Global SA (BG) is a Swiss-domiciled holding company (redomesticated from Bermuda Nov 1, 2023) and one of the four global "ABCD" agribusiness traders. FY2025 was a transformation year: the all-of-Viterra business combination closed July 2, 2025, a $10,617M stock-and-cash deal that lifted total debt from $6,238M to $14,051M and share count from ~134.0M to 193.4M. That single event drives most of the capital allocation and governance picture below. There are no Indian-listing constructs (no promoter pledging). Fiscal year ends December 31. Figures are USD millions unless noted. FCF defined here as cash from operations minus capital expenditures, per CLAUDE.md. Net debt = total debt minus cash, per CLAUDE.md. EBITDA references are management's adjusted figures where labelled.

## 2. Classification

**Standard professional management** — with one elevated, deal-specific governance feature to watch.

The table is dominated by low-to-mid severities. The hard governance triggers are clean: related-party transactions are ≤9% of COGS (far below threshold), the auditor (Deloitte, 24 years) issued an unqualified opinion with no restatement or material weakness, and CEO/CFO have been stable for ~7 years. Capital allocation is being executed by a professional management team running a transparent dividend-plus-buyback program alongside the largest deal in the company's history. The single feature that lifts this above a plain "standard" read is the new ~34% combined stake held by Glencore, CPP Investments and BCI, with two board-nomination seats each for Glencore and CPP — a real concentration of voting and board influence that did not exist before July 2025. It is disclosed, contractual, and not in itself a red flag, but it is a structural change in who can shape the board. The 44% one-year share-count increase and the $7.8B debt step-up are large but are the mechanical cost of a strategic combination management is now deleveraging against (1.9x to 1.6x adjusted leverage), not evidence of habitual value destruction. This does not reach "Governance red flags," and the integration is too young to call "Capital allocation concerns" a proven outcome — but the M&A bet is unproven and the shareholder concentration is material.

## 3. Most Material Signal

The Viterra acquisition and its financing (severity-bearing across three rows: acquisition pattern 45, share dilution 55, debt 45) is the single signal that would change the classification if it deteriorated. Bunge paid $10,617M and issued 65.6M shares (~44% dilution) plus took on $7.8B of incremental debt for a business whose synergies (>$70M realized so far) and integration are barely ten months old, and the purchase-price allocation is still preliminary with $2,659M of goodwill. If integration stalls, synergies under-deliver, or the recently-raised 2026 EPS guide ($9.00–$9.50) proves optimistic, the combination of a stretched balance sheet (a downgrade would raise borrowing costs even without a maturity trigger), a paused buyback, and an impairment of preliminary goodwill would move this from "standard professional management" toward "capital allocation concerns." The linked governance lever is the ~34% Glencore/CPP/BCI bloc with board-nomination rights: if that bloc's interests diverged from minority holders (e.g., on a future transaction or a capital decision), the influence it holds could become a governance concern rather than a passive ownership fact. The disconfirming evidence to watch: synergy run-rate, adjusted leverage trajectory back toward target, and whether Q4-2025's near-total buyback pause ($76,564 in Oct, zero Nov–Dec) extends into a multi-quarter halt.

## 4. Capital Allocation Score /100

**59 / 100** (higher = better).

Average raw severity across the 12 applicable rows (excluding the not-applicable pledging row) is ~35, implying a baseline ~65 on a higher-is-better scale. Two adjustments pull it down to 59: (1) the acquisition/dilution/debt cluster carries genuine, unproven execution risk that a simple average understates for a deal this size closed this recently, and (2) the new ~34% concentrated ownership with board-nomination rights is a structural governance overhang that a clean related-party and auditor record does not offset. The score is held up — not higher — by clean audit history, stable leadership, contained related-party dealing, investment-grade ratings across all three agencies, and a coherent, disclosed deleveraging plan. The number sits in the mixed-to-low-strong band, consistent with "standard professional management executing a large, still-unproven transformation," and is deliberately not in the strong band because the defining capital decision of the year cannot yet be judged a success.
