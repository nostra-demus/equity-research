# Business Model Reality Check — BG (Synthesis)

## Abstract

Bunge Global SA buys oilseeds and grains from farmers, crushes and refines them, and sells meal, oil, grain and specialty products to feed, food and biofuel customers across 50+ countries. It earns a thin spread, not a selling price: Soybean Processing and Refining alone is 51.6% of FY25 net sales and 52.6% of reportable segment EBIT, and the soybean crush spread is the single company-wide swing factor. The clearest positive is scale and a clean audit — a 200-year, four-player oligopoly that tightened when Bunge absorbed Viterra in July 2025. The defining negative is that this scale earns sub-cost-of-capital returns: headline ROIC of 6.7% sits below the company's own 7.2% cost of equity. Verdict: a cyclical, commodity-spread business worth deeper work only with a strong timing edge.

## 3. Reconciliation

The specialists are consistent on the central facts (spread economics, Soybean dominance, commodity dependence, narrow scale moat). Three numerical differences are reconciled below, none of which changes any verdict:

- **Share-count / dilution magnitude.** capital-allocation-governance states share count rose "~44%" (133,964,235 → 193,408,656) and elsewhere "~134.0M to 193.4M" [`11`]. data-triage cites 193,509,080 as of Feb 17, 2026 and 194,018,115 as of Apr 27, 2026 [`00`]. These are not in conflict — they are different measurement dates (year-end vs. cover-date vs. post-quarter). Reconciled view: ~44% year-over-year dilution from the Viterra share issuance is the figure that matters for the capital-structure cap; the later counts reflect ordinary post-deal drift.

- **Soybean segment "profit share."** segment-map reports Soybean as 52.6% of *reportable* segment EBIT but flags that, measured against *total* company EBIT of $1,533M (after the −$796M Corporate drag), the same $1,225M is ~80% [`03` §2]. unit-economics and competitive-map both carry the 52.6% figure [`04`, `08`]. Reconciled view: Soybean is unambiguously the value driver on either basis; the synthesis uses 52.6% of reportable segment EBIT as the headline and notes the ~80%-of-total-EBIT alternative reading. No contradiction.

- **ROIC figure.** Multiple specialists cite the company's headline ROIC of 6.7% and adjusted 8% [`04`, `09`]; moat additionally computes ~5.1% on average invested capital [`09` §3]. The conservative default applies: the read that "returns are at or below the cost of equity" holds across all three figures (6.7% headline and ~5.1% computed both sit below the 7.2% cost of equity; only the CIP-adjusted 8% clears it). Reconciled view: Bunge's disclosed and computed returns do not clearly exceed its cost of capital.

## 4. Note To The Final Synthesizer

- **Strongest business-model positive:** Scale within a four-player oligopoly that just tightened. The 200-year, 50+-country origination-processing-logistics network is genuinely hard to replicate, and the July 2025 Viterra acquisition removed one independent ABCD-tier competitor [`09` §5, `08` §4]. What this MEANS: the structural moat keeps the industry an oligopoly and supports survival, but it walls off Bunge's profits from no one in particular — ADM, Cargill and Louis Dreyfus run the same model.
- **Strongest business-model negative:** Scale that does not earn its cost of capital. Headline ROIC 6.7% (computed ~5.1%) sits below the company's own 7.2% cost of equity; gross margin fell 8.14% → 6.39% → 4.85% over FY23–FY25 [`09` §3, `07` §1]. What this MEANS: this is a survivor, not a compounder — the business clears the disqualifier bar but does not generate excess returns through the cycle, so it earns a "deeper work only with a timing edge" verdict, not a "deeper work" verdict.
- **Most important segment:** Soybean Processing and Refining — 52.6% of reportable segment EBIT, 51.6% of net sales, and the source of the single company-wide swing factor (the soybean crush spread) [`03`, `04`].
- **Cleanest unit-economics read (or why it cannot be derived):** It cannot be derived cleanly. Bunge does not disclose a standalone crush margin per ton; segment revenue and raw-material cost blend the processing book with the lower-margin merchandising book, and management explicitly declines to break it out ("we don't lock in the margin until we have all three legs priced") [`04` §1–3]. The best available proxy — ~$30 segment EBIT per processed ton on ~$885 revenue, ~7.5% pre-tax return on segment assets — points to marginal value creation that is contaminated by pass-through. The single most valuable missing disclosure is a processing-only crush margin per ton.
- **Where the company sits vs named peers on margin / ROIC:** Cannot be ranked. The data pool contains no ADM, Cargill or Louis Dreyfus margin or ROIC figures; the one Louis Dreyfus datapoint (~3.4% EBITDA margin) is an unverified web release and not EBIT-comparable [`09` §3–4]. On its own absolute economics Bunge earns a thin, falling, sub-cost-of-equity return. A peer margin/ROIC export is the highest-value missing input for this module.
- **Main external dependency:** Biofuel demand policy (EPA RVO and equivalent mandates in Brazil/Indonesia/EU), which moved a single quarter's adjusted-EPS guidance from ~$0.85 to ~$2.00 — a >100% swing on a policy decision, not a management action [`10` §2, §5]. Behind it sit commodity/crush margins and weather-driven crop size, both rated High.
- **Most important capital allocation or governance signal:** The Viterra combination and its financing (+44% dilution, debt +$7.8B to $14,051M, $2,659M preliminary goodwill, integration ~10 months old) — the defining capital decision of the year, still unproven, and the reason the capital-allocation score is capped at 50/100. The linked governance overhang is the new Glencore/CPP/BCI ~34% bloc with two board-nomination seats each for Glencore and CPP [`11` §3].
- **Whether any automatic disqualifier triggered:** No. All eight hard disqualifiers are clean [`01`].
- **Biggest missing data point:** A direct peer margin/ROIC export (ADM, Louis Dreyfus) to convert the qualitative "leading processor" claim into a quantified competitive position — without it the moat read is "narrow" by inference, not by measured return superiority [`09` §4–5]. Runner-up: a processing-only crush margin per ton.
- **Whether the business deserves deeper work, and what would change the answer:** It deserves deeper work only with a strong timing edge on the crush/biofuel cycle. What would change the answer toward "low-quality / avoid": (a) the Brazil indirect-tax claims (severity 62) shifting from "possible" to "probable" — $760M of unprovisioned exposure rising $115M in one quarter against $816M of FY25 net income [`12`]; (b) a stalled Viterra integration or goodwill impairment that forces a paused buyback and a downgrade [`11`]; or (c) through-cycle ROIC staying at or below the cost of equity, which would turn the scale advantage into "no moat proven" [`09` §5]. What would change it toward "worth deeper work": a documented, sustained crush spread plus through-cycle adjusted ROIC clearly above the cost of capital.

**Red-flag propagation (severity ≥ 40, per mandate):**
- Brazil indirect-tax claims (severity 62) — surfaced above and below; the single highest-weighted new flag.
- Brazil farmer-financing credit book +75% YoY to $835M + $183M long-term, collateral "may not be sufficient," correlated with the same weather/crop risk that drives the P&L (severity 48) — surfaced in the cross-cutting Brazil node below.
- Net share dilution +44% (severity 55), Glencore/CPP/BCI ~34% bloc with board seats (severity 50), Viterra acquisition pattern (severity 45), debt step-up to $14,051M (severity 45), and working-capital cash absorption (severity 40) from `11` — all carried into the capital-structure cap and the capital-allocation commentary above.
- Flags below severity 40 (divestiture indemnity tail $1.6B max / 38; off-balance-sheet performance guarantees +34% to $2,151M / 34; Level 3 fair-value marks through P&L / 30) are noted here as monitoring items and not individually escalated.

**Cross-cutting pattern to carry forward:** Brazil is an undiversified, compounding risk node. The unprovisioned tax claims ($760M, rising), the farmer-financing book ($835M + $183M, +75%), and the Level 3 fair-value marks (Brazil basis/freight inputs) all sit in one jurisdiction and all worsen in the same scenario — a weak Brazilian crop or weaker real [`12` §4]. The headline "no concentration" customer/geography read partly obscures this tail.

## 5. Simple Summary

- **What it does:** Buys oilseeds and grains from farmers, crushes and refines them, and sells meal, oil, grain and specialty products to feed, food and biofuel customers in 50+ countries.
- **How it makes money:** A thin spread — the gap between exchange-priced inputs and exchange-priced outputs — not a price it sets. Gross margin is ~3.5–4.85%; raw materials track sales nearly one-for-one.
- **Whether each new unit creates value:** Unclear, leaning marginal — ~$30 segment EBIT per processed ton, ~7.5% pre-tax return on segment assets, but company-wide ROIC of 6.7% sits below the 7.2% cost of equity. No standalone crush margin is disclosed.
- **Which segment matters most:** Soybean Processing and Refining — half of sales and reportable EBIT, and the source of the crush spread that drives the whole company.
- **Whether it has a moat, and against whom:** A narrow moat (scale, 50/100) against ADM, Cargill, Louis Dreyfus and Wilmar — but the same scale is shared by those rivals and has not produced superior returns.
- **What external variables it depends on:** Biofuel policy (EPA RVO, Brazil/Indonesia/EU mandates), the crush spread, and weather-driven crop size — all rated High, all outside management's control; a >100% guidance swing happened on one policy decision.
- **Whether capital is allocated well:** Not yet proven. The Viterra deal (+44% dilution, debt +$7.8B) is the defining bet and is barely 10 months old; the score is capped at 50/100 on the capital-structure change, and a new ~34% Glencore/CPP/BCI bloc holds board-nomination rights.
- **Whether it deserves deeper work:** Only with a strong timing edge on the crush/biofuel cycle. Clean audit and clear disclosure, but structurally low-quality, sub-cost-of-capital economics and a compounding Brazil tax/credit/marks risk node cap the optimism.
