# Maturity Wall & Refinancing — BG

**Company:** Bunge Global SA (NYSE: BG). **Reporting currency: US$ millions.** Anchored on the FY2025 10-K (year ended Dec 31, 2025), which carries the only complete year-by-year maturity disclosure, with a Q1 2026 10-Q (Mar 31, 2026) overlay and one post-quarter event. Gross debt and instruments are taken verbatim from `01_capital-structure-and-leverage.md` (gross debt **$14,051M** YE2025 / **$14,553M** Q1 2026).

**Two facts dominate this report — flag once.** (1) The headline "36.8% due within 12 months" is **not** a bond tower — ~$3,883M of it is rolling, self-liquidating working-capital debt (local op-co lines, commercial paper, revolver draws) that turns over continuously with the inventory it funds; the *hard-dated bond/term-loan* maturity inside 12 months is only ~$1,378M, and ~$575M of that was already repaid post-quarter. (2) The company terms-out floating debt into fixed bonds opportunistically — the March 2026 bond-for-revolver swap and the April 2026 repayment of the 2.00% notes both happened after the YE2025 snapshot and both reduced near-term refinancing exposure. The schedule below is shown on the YE2025 disclosure basis (the complete one) and these two post-snapshot moves are called out explicitly.

**Reading the rate exposure.** Two distinct rate stories. (1) **Refinancing step-up on the fixed book:** the existing notes carry a low **3.61%** blended coupon (many were issued in the 2020–2021 low-rate window — 1.00%/2.00%/2.75%/3.20%/3.25% tranches). Each maturing tranche refinances into a ~5.0% market — a **~140 bps step-up**, worth roughly **+$115M/yr of cash interest if the entire $8.3bn fixed book repriced** (it does not all at once — it reprices tranche-by-tranche across 2026–2035, so the drag phases in gradually). Bunge's own March 2026 issuance at a 5.00% blended coupon is the cleanest evidence of where it actually refinances today. The March 2026 bond-for-revolver swap modestly reduced this floating share by terming $600M of revolver into fixed bonds.

## 4. Refinancing Exposure

For the next-24-month maturities. The relevant figure is **not** the full $5,261M within-12m headline but the obligations that genuinely require funding/rollover: ~$3,883M rolling working-capital debt (must be *rolled*, not repaid) + ~$1,378M LTD-2026 (less ~$575M already repaid) + $1,369M LTD-2027 = a hard term-maturity load of roughly **$2.2bn over 24 months** (2026 $1,378M + 2027 $1,369M, net of the $575M April repayment ≈ $2.17bn) sitting on top of a continuously-rolling ~$3.9bn working-capital book.

**Conclusion (3–5 sentences).** The near-term wall is **covered, but it leans on rollover and committed-facility access, not on cash + FCF.** Cash ($839M) plus negative FCF cannot retire the ~$2.2bn of hard 2026–2027 term maturities on their own; the company instead relies on (a) $9,065M of committed, undrawn revolving capacity that does not depend on open public markets, and (b) demonstrated investment-grade bond/CP access (four issuances in ~18 months). Rating posture is **investment grade, stable, and recently affirmed/upgraded post-Viterra** — S&P A- (upgraded Jul-2-2025), Moody's Baa1 (affirmed Jul-28-2025), Fitch BBB+ (affirmed Jul-2-2025) — and the debt agreements carry **no rating-downgrade acceleration trigger** [FY25 10-K, Note 17, lines 2967–2991]. The ~56% floating share means **~$7.8bn of interest cost reprices with rates** (+200bp ≈ +$156M/yr). On balance the refinancing read is **"refinanceable in most markets"** — the committed revolver backstop and IG access cover the near-term wall in most environments, but the structure is *not* self-funded from internal cash flow (FCF is negative), so it is a notch short of "low refi risk."

## 5. Refinancing Read

The wall is short-dated in headline terms (WAM ~2.9 years, 36.8% inside 12 months) but the inside-12-months figure is **~$3.9bn of self-liquidating working-capital debt that rolls, not a bond tower** — the hard term-maturity load is ~$1.4bn in 2026 (of which ~$575M was already repaid in April 2026) and ~$1.4bn in 2027, with the real concentration in **2028 ($2,759M, including $1.55bn of Viterra floating term loans maturing alongside the $3.5bn revolver)**. Refinancing the legacy ~3.61% fixed notes into today's ~5.0% market is a **~140 bps step-up** (worth up to ~$115M/yr of incremental cash interest as the $8.3bn book reprices through 2035), and ~56% of the stack floats, so a +200bp rate move adds ~$156M/yr regardless of refinancing; **the single biggest refinancing risk is the 2028 cluster colliding with a closed-market or downgrade scenario, compounded by the fact that FCF is currently negative — so the company depends on rollover access, not internal cash generation, to clear maturities.**

**Survival under "market closure" (no new unsecured issuance) for the next 12 months — explicit answer:** **Yes, the company survives the next 12 months even if public unsecured markets shut**, on the best available evidence. The reason is the **$9,065M of committed, undrawn revolving credit capacity** [FY25 10-K, Note 17, line 7339], which is contractually committed bank funding — not dependent on issuing new public bonds — and which alone dwarfs the ~$2.2bn of hard 2026–2027 term maturities and can also absorb a roll-failure of the ~$3.9bn working-capital lines. Two assumptions are attached and must be carried to Agent 06: (a) *committed revolver capacity remains drawable* — these facilities are committed by lenders and have **no rating-downgrade acceleration trigger** [FY25 10-K, line 2991], but a covenant breach (max debt-to-capitalization / min current ratio) could impair access — covenant headroom is Agent 04's call; and (b) *the rolling working-capital lines (~$3.9bn) continue to roll or can be migrated onto the revolver* — $2,983M of these are uncommitted bilateral/local lines that a lender could decline to renew, which is the genuine stress channel and why this is "survives in most markets," not "immune." **Inference, not from filings:** the conclusion that the committed revolver fully backstops a 12-month market closure is the analyst's reading of disclosed committed capacity vs. disclosed maturities; the filing does not state a market-closure survival horizon.
