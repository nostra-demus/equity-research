## 6. Seasonality Read

Bunge's seasonality is **not quantifiable from the supplied data pool** — only Q1 FY25 and Q1 FY26 are available, so a multi-year quarterly seasonality table is not possible [01, Section 5]. Management's own FY26 phasing guide — 40% H1 / 60% H2, with Q3/Q4 split ~45/55 — explicitly tells the market that Q2 FY26 is the **smallest quarter** of the year and that the seasonal weight sits in H2 (North American harvest in Q4, South American harvest in Q1 of the following year) [04, Section 2]. Translation: Q2 itself is seasonally light, so the swing factor for the year is not Q2's print — it's whether the H2 forward curves inversion proves correct. Q2 alone cannot validate or invalidate the full-year setup; it's the Q2 guidance update on H2 that matters most.

## 7. Historical Pattern

Bunge has a **structural beat pattern on EPS, miss pattern on revenue, beat pattern on EBITDA** [04, Section 6]. Over the last 9 quarters: EPS Normalized beat in 7 of 9 quarters (78% hit rate); average EPS beat magnitude +31% (skewed by Q1 FY26's +110%); revenue missed in 7 of 9 quarters; EBITDA beat in 7 of 9. This is the classic ag-merchandiser signature where take-rate / spread matters more than topline. FY-level: FY25 EPS beat by 3.1%, FY24 missed by 1.7%, FY23 beat by 6.9% — so the FY beat is real but modest (+3 to +7% in beat years). The synthesizer should weight the EPS beat pattern moderately positive but should not extrapolate the Q1 FY26 +110% magnitude — that was an unusual MTM-aided surge and the Street has now caught up. Going forward the relevant magnitude is single-digit to low-teens, not triple-digit.

## 8. Setup Verdict

**Setup favors beat — but only on the Q2 print, not the full year.**

The single most important factor is the **78% EPS beat hit-rate combined with 100% upward revision breadth on Q2 EPS, EBITDA, and EBIT in the last month** [04, Sections 4–6]: the company has just raised guidance, the Street is following, crush margin tailwinds are still operative through Q2, and seasonality means Q2 is the smallest quarter of the year so the bar to beat is mechanically easier. The single biggest risk that could flip the verdict is the **H2 forward curves "heavily inverted" signal becoming visible in Q2 guidance** — if management uses the Q2 call to flag deteriorating Q3/Q4 crush economics, an in-line Q2 print could still trade as a miss because the Street has FY26 priced near the top of management's range.

## 9. Second-Quarter Look-Ahead

The setup for **Q3 FY26 looks materially harder than Q2**: Q3 and Q4 EPS revisions have been net-DOWN over the last month (2 up vs 4 down for each), forward crush curves are heavily inverted, and management itself flagged "significant uncertainty" in H2 [04, Sections 5, 7; 02, Section 7]. Seasonality also turns against the company in Q3 (the H1/H2 = 40/60 split with Q3/Q4 = 45/55 implies Q3 is ~27% of full-year EPS, making it the **second-largest** quarter — i.e., a more loaded bar). The combination of a more loaded quarter and weaker revision breadth points to a tighter beat path in Q3 even if Q2 prints clean.

## 10. Pre-Mortem

If this beat setup fails, the most likely reason we missed it is that the **Viterra-driven, MTM-aided Q1 surge masked a deteriorating underlying crush spread that the inverted forward curves were warning about** — management's "significant uncertainty" framing on H2 turns out to apply earlier than expected, with bunker fuel persistence in Grain Merchandising and softening soy oil refining premiums (already "not where we were in '22 and '23") compounding into a Q2 print where adjusted EPS comes in at or below guidance midpoint while Street sits near the top end. A secondary pre-mortem is that we underweighted the gap between adjusted and GAAP earnings — if a second consecutive quarter shows >$1 per share of MTM adjustments, the market may begin discounting "adjusted" beats as low-quality.
