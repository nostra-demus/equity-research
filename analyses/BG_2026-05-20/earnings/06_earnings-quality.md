## 2. Cash Conversion Assessment

CFO tracked EBITDA tightly in FY23 (87%) and FY24 (84%) — both above the 70% "healthy" threshold. **In FY25 CFO/EBITDA collapsed to 38%, well below the 50% red-flag line.** The collapse is driven by two factors: (a) larger working capital consumption tied to the Viterra balance-sheet step-up (Viterra brought $5,720M of inventories at close [5], plus working capital build from higher commodity prices), and (b) a $395M net unrealized derivative loss reversal flowing through the operating bridge (these are the same MTM timing differences that management adjusts out of "adjusted EBIT") [1]. Q1 FY26 CFO of $(541)M vs Q1 FY25 of $(285)M [6] confirms the trajectory has not yet reversed; TTM FCF is $(1,161)M [7]. **Cash conversion is now a material concern** — the question for the next 12 months is whether the FY25/Q1 FY26 working capital build is transitory (Viterra integration) or persistent.

Flag check:
- **DSO rising >10% YoY**: Yes — but FY25 DSO rise is largely composition (Viterra carries more credit-bearing customers). - **DPO rising sharply**: Yes — payables doubled, but this scales with inventory turns; AP/Inventory ratio actually *fell* from 42.8% (FY24) to 37.0% (FY25), suggesting Bunge is NOT stretching suppliers; if anything, paying faster relative to inventory.

## 9. Earnings Quality Score

**Earnings Quality Score: 42/100** (band: Material concerns — cash conversion weak AND recurring one-offs)

Single most important reason: **CFO/EBITDA collapsed from 84% (FY24) to 38% (FY25)**, taking FCF from +$524M to −$879M in one year; Q1 FY26 cash burn extended this. Even accepting that much of the deterioration is Viterra-related (working capital step-up), the reported FY25 numbers do not yet convert to cash, and the gap between reported EBIT ($1,533M) and FCF (−$879M) is the largest in the three-year window. Layered on top, management's "adjusted" EBIT for Q1 FY26 is 3x the reported number — a magnitude that means the reader is effectively forced to choose between two materially different earnings figures, and the larger of the two relies on $336M of MTM normalization in a single quarter. The score is held above 40 because (a) SBC is not excluded from adjusted figures, (b) capex ratio is stable, (c) the FY25 working capital damage is at least partially explainable by acquisition timing, and (d) the MTM mechanic, while large, is mechanical and reversible.

## 10. The Single Biggest Quality Concern

The single biggest quality concern is the **persistent gap between Bunge's "adjusted" earnings narrative and the cash that actually arrives**. Management is asking investors to look through (a) MTM timing differences that go both ways but consistently appear, (b) acquisition/integration costs that have been "one-time" for at least four consecutive reporting periods totaling $622M, and (c) a balance-sheet step-up from Viterra that absorbed $502M of working capital in FY25 and another ~$1.7B in Q1 FY26. The aggregate effect is that reported FY25 net income of $843M converted into only $844M of CFO and *negative* $879M of FCF, while management's "adjusted" framework would have the reader believe underlying earnings are inflecting positively (Q1 FY26 adjusted EPS up 1.1%, full-year guidance raised to $9.00–$9.50). The gap between the two views is the largest single risk in this name: if Viterra working capital and integration costs prove sticky rather than transitory, then "adjusted" EBIT becomes a permanent overstatement of repeatable cash earnings. The next 2–3 quarters of CFO data — particularly Q3/Q4 FY26 once seasonal South American harvest cash inflows would normally arrive — are the empirical test.
