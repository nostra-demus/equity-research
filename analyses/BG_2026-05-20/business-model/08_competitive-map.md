# Competitive Map — BG

## 1. Dominant Segment

Soybean Processing and Refining — 51.6% of FY25 net sales ($36.3B) and 52.6% of FY25 segment EBIT ($1,225M). [FY25 10-K, p.37–38]

## 2. Named Competitors

Per Bunge's own FY25 10-K, the soybean-segment key competitors are: ADM, Cargill, Louis Dreyfus, Wilmar, and COFCO International. [FY25 10-K, p.307–309] The three most credible global rivals — comparable scale, same multi-region footprint, named in every BG segment description — are ADM, Cargill, and Louis Dreyfus.

### Competitor A — Archer Daniels Midland Co. ("ADM")

- **Ticker / listing:** NYSE: ADM
- **Where they compete:** Direct overlap in soybean crush (dominant segment), softseed crush, specialty oils, and grain merchandising. ADM is named by Bunge as a key competitor in ALL FOUR reportable segments. [FY25 10-K, p.307, p.336, p.382, p.435] Operates "the largest oilseed processing and refining complex in Europe" per industry research and is one of three firms processing ~71% of US soy crop. [Industry research; Oxfam grain-trader report]
- **Scale:** FY2025 revenue $80.3 billion (fiscal year ended Dec 31, 2025), down 6.2% YoY. [Stockanalysis.com / Macrotrends — ADM revenue series] Bunge FY25 net sales $70.3B — ADM is ~1.14x BG scale, directly comparable.
- **Source named in:** Bunge FY25 10-K, p.307 (Soybean segment), p.336 (Softseed), p.382 (Other Oilseeds), p.435 (Grain Merchandising and Milling).
- **One-line read:** The most direct full-stack competitor — same four-segment value-chain shape, similar regional footprint (heavy US/EU exposure, less Brazil than BG), and ADM is the only publicly listed peer where margin and capital-efficiency comparisons are clean for the downstream moat agent.

### Competitor B — Cargill Incorporated

- **Ticker / listing:** Private (US, Minnesota-headquartered)
- **Where they compete:** Direct overlap in soybean crush, softseed crush, specialty oils, and grain merchandising — named in ALL FOUR Bunge segments. [FY25 10-K, p.307, p.336, p.382, p.435] One of three firms processing ~71% of US soy crop alongside ADM and Bunge; "Bunge and Cargill responsible for more than 30 per cent of all soy exports from Brazil to Europe." [Oxfam / industry research]
- **Scale:** Fiscal 2025 (year ended May 31, 2025) revenue $154 billion, down 3.75% YoY from $160B in FY24 — Cargill's lowest revenue since 2021. [Star Tribune, Aug 2025; Cargill 2025 Annual Report] Roughly 2.2x BG net sales, but Cargill spans meat, animal nutrition, and food-ingredient lines BG does NOT touch, so its agribusiness-only revenue is closer-to BG-scale.
- **Source named in:** Bunge FY25 10-K, p.307, p.336, p.382, p.435.
- **One-line read:** The single largest agribusiness peer globally and the only private member of the "ABCD" — a structural disadvantage for BG investors because Cargill's segment-level economics are not publicly disclosed, so margin benchmarking is partial.

### Competitor C — Louis Dreyfus Company B.V. ("LDC")

- **Ticker / listing:** Private (Netherlands-headquartered, family-controlled)
- **Where they compete:** Direct overlap in soybean crush, softseed crush, and grain merchandising — named in THREE of Bunge's four segments (excluded from Other Oilseeds). [FY25 10-K, p.307, p.336, p.435] Heaviest overlap with BG in South America origination and global oilseed flow.
- **Scale:** FY2024 net sales $50.6 billion (calendar 2024); EBITDA $1.88B; SOR $2.35B. FY2024 capex $1.01B, up from $636M in FY23, "focusing on organic growth and strategic acquisitions" including US/Canada oilseed processing expansion. [LDC press release, 2024 Financial and Sustainability Performance Results; Milling MEA, Apr 2025] Roughly 0.7x BG net sales — meaningfully smaller than ADM/Cargill/BG post-Viterra but still in the same tier.
- **Source named in:** Bunge FY25 10-K, p.307, p.336, p.435.
- **One-line read:** Smallest of the three but the cleanest pure-play agribusiness competitor — no meat or food-ingredient diversification, so its segment economics are a useful but private benchmark for BG's crush spread.

## 3. Competitive Position

**Position vs peers: not disclosed.** Bunge does not publish soybean crush market share figures in its FY25 10-K, and the closest the filing comes is a generic statement that "certain of our competitors have added oilseed processing and refining capacity in response to growing demand," with additional refining-capacity competition from petroleum companies in the US. [FY25 10-K, p.1029–1032] The most useful available proxies: (i) Bunge processed 41.0M MT of soybeans in FY25 vs ADM's disclosed >40M MT in 2023 — order-of-magnitude parity at the global level [FY25 10-K, p.38; industry research]; (ii) Bunge's Q1 2026 commentary cites "incremental crush capacity in Argentina" and notes Bunge has "25% more capacity to run harder" [Q1 2026 transcript, prepared remarks; line 291], implying volume growth ahead but not necessarily share gain because peers (notably ADM and LDC) have also announced capacity additions [LDC 2024 results — US/Canada oilseed expansion]. Without disclosed share data, the read is "Holding, post-Viterra step-change in absolute scale" but this is not provable from filings.

## 4. Competitive Shape

The global oilseed-crush and grain-merchandising market is an **"ABCD" oligopoly** — Archer Daniels Midland, Bunge, Cargill, and Louis Dreyfus collectively control "as much as 90 per cent of the global grain trade" per Oxfam's grain-trader study, with "Bunge, Cargill, and ADM dominat[ing] in all the major exporting countries" and processing roughly 71% of the US soy crop. [Oxfam, *Cereal Secrets*; Green European Journal, *How Agriculture's Big Five Thrive in Crisis*] In Europe, the ABCDs jointly accounted for 55–90% of the crude soybean oil market in 2017 [Oxfam research]. COFCO International is the credible fifth player, having "grown to manage a portion of China's imports" as Chinese policy diversified away from ABCD dependence [Oxfam]. Wilmar is dominant in Asia/palm but a secondary peer in soy. This is a structurally concentrated market — barriers to entry (port terminals, origination networks, river/rail logistics, decades of farmer relationships) are very high, but the four-firm-plus-one structure means none of the players has pricing power; competition is principally based on price, geographic asset placement, and logistics cost. [FY25 10-K, p.293–298, p.330–335, p.429–433]

## 5. Caveat

Three issues weaken this competitive map:

1. **No company-disclosed share data.** Bunge does not publish soy-crush or grain-merchandising market share. Direction of share (gaining/holding/losing) is therefore inferred from capacity-addition language, not measured.
2. **Two of three named peers are private.** Cargill (FY25 $154B) and Louis Dreyfus (FY24 $50.6B) disclose only top-line and limited EBITDA; segment-level crush margin comparisons for the downstream moat agent will be cleanest against ADM (public, segment-disclosed) and partial against the private pair.
3. **The "Viterra reshape" is one year old.** Bunge's segment structure changed in Q3 2025 with the Viterra closing, and Glencore retains shareholder rights post-deal. [FY25 10-K, p.1579–1591] Year-over-year peer comparisons for FY25 are not on a like-for-like basis with FY23/FY24, and Glencore's continuing 16%-ish stake means a major commodity trader is also a Bunge shareholder — not a clean independent peer.

Resolution would require: (a) IHS / S&P Global / Mintec crush-capacity tables by company; (b) USDA / Oil World share tables; (c) ADM segment EBIT margins for direct margin benchmarking in the downstream moat agent.
