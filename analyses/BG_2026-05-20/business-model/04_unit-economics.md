# Unit Economics — BG

## 1. Natural Unit

**The natural economic unit for Soybean Processing and Refining is one metric ton (MT) of soybeans processed (crushed).**

Secondary units in adjacent segments: one MT of softseed crushed (Softseed segment, 10.8M MT FY25); one MT of grain merchandised (Grain Merchandising & Milling segment, 67.2M MT FY25); one MT of refined oil (3.6M MT FY25, a sub-unit within Soybean Processing). [FY25 10-K, p.38, p.39, p.42]

## 3. Value Creation Read

**Insufficient data to conclude clean value creation at the unit level.** The disclosed math shows each crushed ton generated about $43 of gross margin and about $30 of segment EBIT in FY25 [FY25 10-K, p.38], up from a trough of $24/MT in FY24 — but Bunge does not disclose what it costs to install a ton of crush capacity, expected plant life, maintenance capex per ton, or anything resembling a payback. Without the denominator (capital required per ton of capacity), the contribution margin figure cannot be converted into a return on incremental capital per unit. The single most valuable missing disclosure is **per-ton fixed-asset and capex intensity for the soybean processing footprint** — without it, the only honest read is that contribution per ton is positive and improving off a depressed FY24 base, but ~50% below the FY23 normalized level, and the structural value-creation question (does a ton of crush earn its cost of capital) cannot be answered from filings.

What can be said: the per-ton segment EBIT spread is sub-3% on revenue (FY25: $29.9/MT EBIT on $885/MT sales = 3.4% EBIT margin) [FY25 10-K, p.38] — even in a normal year (FY23 at 6.1%) this is a thin-spread commodity processor whose unit economics depend on the crush spread, not on the per-unit cost structure Bunge controls.

## 4. Sensitivity

The single most leveraged input is the **crush spread (sales price per ton of meal+oil minus raw soybean cost per ton)**, which collapses directly to contribution per ton. A 20% move in the crush spread on $43/MT gross margin would change unit gross profit by roughly $50–60/MT (well over 100% of current unit gross margin), versus a 20% move in plant utilization which would move per-ton fixed-cost absorption by single-digit dollars. **Historically the crush spread is the variable that has actually moved most**: FY23 → FY24 → FY25 contribution per ton ran $61.8 → $23.7 → $29.9 [FY25 10-K, p.38], a 52%/40% range entirely driven by spread compression and recovery, not by Bunge's per-ton operating costs (which moved less than 5% per ton year-over-year). In other words, the unit-level value-creation read is dominated by a price/margin variable Bunge does not control, which is the central economic characteristic of a commodity oilseed processor.
