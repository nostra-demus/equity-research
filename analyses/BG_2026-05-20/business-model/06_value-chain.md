Bargaining power bands used:
- **Strong:** Company sets price, dictates terms, has alternatives

## 2. Input Cost Pass-Through

Bunge does NOT have formal pass-through pricing on its core flows: it buys commodities at world reference prices and sells commodities at world reference prices, hedging the spread via derivatives rather than negotiating escalators. The 10-K explicitly states the company is "subject to fluctuations in agricultural commodity and other raw material prices, energy prices, and other factors outside of our control that could adversely affect our operating results" and that energy/diesel cost increases hit operating costs without an obvious offset on the sell side. [FY25 10-K, pp.14–15] Q1 2026 made the limitation visible: a "significant spike in bunker fuel costs" tied to Middle East disruption "impacted" the grain merchandising and milling segment via ocean freight, with no language about recovering that cost from customers — the segment guidance was actually taken DOWN for the full year because of it. [Q1 2026 transcript, Neppl remarks, p.7] Mark-to-market hedge timing also caused a $1.28/share GAAP vs adjusted gap in Q1 2026, evidence that risk management is the lever, not contractual indexation. [Q1 2026 transcript, p.3]

## 3. Customer Pricing Power

Bunge has minimal standalone pricing power: its meal, crude oil, and grain are commodities sold at exchange-traded reference prices ("highly competitive and subject to product substitution," 10-K, p.3, p.4, p.5), and management does not describe price increases — they describe "margins" (crush margins, refining premiums). The Q1 2026 transcript is explicit that the company "doesn't lock in the margin until we have all three legs priced," meaning Bunge is a spread-taker, not a price-setter. [Q1 2026 transcript, Neppl, p.11] Where some pricing latitude exists — branded/refined oils, specialty ingredients (Loders, soy protein concentrate), wheat milling brands — competition is still "based on price, quality, product and service offerings… brand recognition… advertising and promotion" [FY25 10-K, p.4], not pure pricing dominance. No explicit pricing-action examples are disclosed in the last 24 months: management discusses volumes, regional margins, biofuel demand, and farmer selling — never a "we raised price by X% and held volume" line. The most concrete evidence on price-taking is on the sell side: Q1 2026 refining premiums are "not where we were back in '22 and '23" but "pretty resilient" — i.e., prices are set by the spot market, not by Bunge. [Q1 2026 transcript, Neppl, p.9]

## 4. Economic Control Verdict

**Squeezed.**

Bunge is a price-taker on inputs (raw soybeans, softseeds, grain, palm — purchased at world reference prices from atomized farmers who can store on-farm and time their sales [FY25 10-K, p.15]) AND on outputs (meal, oil, grain are "highly competitive and subject to product substitution," with prices set by Chicago/Winnipeg/Matba-Rofex exchanges and global crush margins [FY25 10-K, pp.3–5]). Profit is the algebraic spread between the two, not pricing power on either side. Where Bunge does have leverage — scale, global footprint, multi-region asset network, Brazilian farmer-financing — it manifests as "optionality" and risk-management capability ("our combination of integrated global platform, disciplined risk management… allows us to perform through the cycle" [Q1 2026 transcript, Heckman, p.3]), not as the ability to dictate price to either suppliers or customers. Energy-cost shocks (Q1 2026 bunker fuel from Middle East disruption) flow straight to EBIT [Q1 2026 transcript, p.7], biofuel selling prices are dictated by government policy (EPA RVO, Brazil/Indonesia/EU mandates) [FY25 10-K, p.14], and even Q1 2026 strength was framed as "improved market conditions" and "execution" rather than price action.

## 5. The Single Biggest Bargaining Risk

The combined crush margin (soybean and softseed processing spread): if biofuel mandates ease, refining premiums compress, or processing capacity oversupply returns, Bunge has no contractual mechanism to defend the spread on either side — both legs reprice to spot.
