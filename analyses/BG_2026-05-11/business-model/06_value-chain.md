## 2. Input Cost Pass-Through

There are no contractual cost-pass-through clauses. Bunge buys raw materials (oilseeds, grains) at exchange-quoted commodity prices and sells the resulting meal/oil/grain at exchange-quoted commodity prices, and the gap between them — the crush spread — is itself a market price set by CBOT and equivalent regional boards. Management's own framing of the business is that hedging and back-to-back risk management, not pass-through pricing, is what protects the margin (FY25 10-K, Item 1A, p.25 — "Our risk management strategies may not be effective… exposures may not always be fully hedged"). Energy and freight are explicitly NOT passed through: the Q1 FY26 transcript notes that the bunker-fuel cost spike during the Middle East conflict directly hit Grain Merchandising results because ocean freight margins absorbed the increase (Q1 FY26 transcript, prepared remarks p.3-4 — "lower results in ocean freight, which was impacted by the significant spike in bunker fuel costs"). In Brazil, Bunge does have a structural upstream lever via $835M of prepaid contracts and $455M of secured advances to farmers, which lock in physical supply at a pre-agreed price — but this is supply security, not cost pass-through (FY25 10-K, Note 6, p.110; FY25 10-K, Item 1A, p.22).

## 3. Customer Pricing Power

Bunge has minimal independent pricing power on its core volumes. The 10-K is explicit that "markets for our soybeans, soybean meal, and crude soybean oil are highly competitive and subject to product substitution. Competition is principally based on price" (FY25 10-K, Item 1, p.3) — i.e., the company is a price-taker. The FY23-FY25 EBIT path proves it: Soybean Processing EBIT collapsed from $2,222M in FY23 to $872M in FY24 to $1,225M in FY25 on broadly similar (and rising) volumes, with management attributing the FY24 fall to "lower average sales prices… driven by relative price stabilization" (FY25 10-K, MD&A, p.43) — not to any pricing decision Bunge made. The reverse held in FY25 Softseed: the European sunflower drought lifted prices and the segment booked $11,252M of sales (+62%), but Bunge did not "raise" prices — the market did (FY25 10-K, MD&A, p.44). The one place Bunge can take real price action is specialty ingredients (Tropical Oils & Specialty Ingredients), where higher tropical-oil sales prices in FY25 reflected biofuel-mandate-driven demand pull, not Bunge's pricing strategy (FY25 10-K, MD&A, p.45). The Q1 FY26 CFO commentary on refining premiums — "they're certainly not where we were back in '22 and '23, they've been pretty resilient" (Q1 FY26 transcript, p.8) — confirms even the higher-margin refining leg is set by market spreads, not by Bunge.

## 4. Economic Control Verdict

**Squeezed.** Bunge buys at commodity-board prices it does not set, sells at commodity-board prices it does not set, and has no contractual pass-through on either energy or freight. The FY23→FY24 Soybean EBIT collapse (-61%) on rising volumes (FY25 10-K, MD&A, p.43) and the Q1 FY26 ocean-freight hit from bunker-fuel costs (Q1 FY26 transcript, p.3-4) are direct evidence that earnings move with crush spreads, oil curves, and freight rates — none of which Bunge controls. The only stage with real upstream leverage is Brazilian farmer financing ($835M prepaid + $455M secured advances), which secures supply but not price (FY25 10-K, Note 6, p.110).

## 5. The Single Biggest Bargaining Risk

The downstream biofuel/refiner relationship: Soybean Processing (52% of FY25 sales) increasingly depends on renewable-diesel and biodiesel demand, and that demand is set by U.S. EPA RVO levels, EU/Brazil/Indonesia mandates, and the crude oil price — three things that can move against Bunge overnight with no contractual recourse (FY25 10-K, Item 1A, p.19; Q1 FY26 transcript, prepared remarks p.2-3).
