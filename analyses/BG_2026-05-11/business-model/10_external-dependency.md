## 3. Classification

**Mostly externally driven** — Bunge is fundamentally a commodity midstream/processing business. EBIT is dominated by crush margins (soy, canola, sunflower), which are functions of grain/oil prices, biofuel mandate demand, weather-driven harvest yields, and trade-flow disruptions — none of which management controls. Total EBIT moved from $3,333M (FY23) → $1,792M (FY24) → $1,533M (FY25) primarily on commodity-cycle factors, not on internal decisions [FY25 10-K, MD&A p.41]. Management has real levers (hedging, geographic diversification, integration, capital allocation) and these are non-trivial — that's why this isn't 81–100 — but the marketing line "designed for complexity and change" [Q1 2026 transcript] cannot disguise that the upside guidance hike to $9.00–$9.50 EPS in Q1 2026 was driven by Middle East conflict raising biofuel/crush demand, not by company action.

## 4. External Dependency Risk Score

**75 / 100** *(higher = WORSE)*

Justification: Sits squarely in the 61–80 band ("Mostly externally driven, limited management levers"). Score is not higher because (a) Bunge has genuine scale, hedging discipline, and N/S hemisphere geographic balance that smooth some volatility; (b) FX and interest rate exposures are actively managed and disclosed sensitivities are modest. Score is not lower because crush margins, biofuel policy, weather, and freight rates collectively dominate EBIT direction and management has repeatedly acknowledged earnings move with these.

## 5. The Single Biggest Lever

**Soy crush margins** — driven by the spread between soybean meal/oil prices and soybean input cost, which is itself a function of US/Brazil/Argentina harvests, US RVO biofuel mandates, and US-China trade flows. A 20% adverse move would simultaneously hit the Soybean Processing segment ($1,225M FY25 EBIT, ~80% of reportable segment EBIT) on both sides of the spread and would cascade through the renewable diesel value chain (Bunge Chevron JV).
