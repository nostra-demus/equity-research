# Earnings Sensitivity — BG

## 1. Variable Selection

Variables were selected by ranking the magnitude columns of the consolidated revenue-driver table (`02_revenue-drivers.md`, Section 4) and the consolidated margin-driver table (`03_margin-drivers.md`, Section 5), then prioritising the items that both drivers tag "High" and that the business-model `10_external-dependency.md` flags at Dependency Level "High." The seven items selected are: (1) global oilseed crush spread (single biggest margin and revenue driver per upstream Sections 8/7 of both files), (2) U.S. biofuel policy / RVO + global mandates, (3) crude oil / energy macro (Iran-conflict pull on veg-oil demand), (4) European sunflower seed crop, (5) FX (BRL/CAD/EUR/CNY), (6) interest expense from post-Viterra capital structure ($14.05B total debt), and (7) ocean freight / bunker fuel costs in Grain Merchandising. Mark-to-market timing was deliberately excluded — it is short-term distortion, not a structural sensitivity. Viterra cost synergy delivery was included implicitly inside the crush-spread variable because synergies' magnitude (~$250m run-rate) is smaller than a 1-sigma crush-spread move. The business-model external-dependency output (Section 2) confirms three of the seven (commodity prices, FX, interest rates) with company-disclosed Item 7A sensitivities — those are tagged High confidence below.

## 4. The Single Highest-Sensitivity Variable

**The global oilseed crush spread — chiefly soybean in South and North America — is the variable that moves Bunge's earnings the most by a wide margin.** Its observed range is ~$1.6B at the EBITDA level (FY23 peak Soybean P&R EBIT $2,222m vs FY24 trough $872m), which is more than 2x the next-largest variable and equates to roughly $6.50 of adjusted EPS — i.e. on its own it can move EPS from ~$6 to ~$12. It is **fully external** and unmanageable: per the business-model value-chain output and the margin-drivers Section 8, Bunge is a price-taker on both the soybean input cost and the meal/oil output, and has no contractual cost pass-through. Current direction is favourable (Q1 2026 strength in South America crush, the EPA RVO decision, and stronger meal demand drove the 2026 guidance hike from $7.50–$8.00 to $9.00–$9.50). For the variable to swing to the adverse case in 2026 the system would need at least two of the following: (a) U.S. RVO retreat or expanded small-refinery waivers, (b) collapse in crude oil prices that makes discretionary renewable diesel blending uneconomic, (c) bumper South American 2026 crop crushing regional margins, or (d) China–US trade reset reducing the U.S. soy export pull. Importantly, the forward crush curves are already **heavily inverted** into 2H 2026 — the market is pricing this exact tail.

## 5. Interaction Effects

Material compounding exists. (1) **Crude oil, biofuel mandates, and the oil leg of the crush spread move together** — a sustained crude rally lifts veg-oil demand, supports the RVO economics, and raises the crush-spread oil leg simultaneously. The same three variables would also unwind together if the Iran-conflict premium fades and the EPA softens the RVO. In that downside case, the EBITDA impacts are not independent and would stack toward the lower end of the bear band, not the midpoint. (2) **European sunflower crop and softseed price/volume mix** are mechanically linked — a poor crop raises sun oil prices (helps revenue per ton) but compresses crush volumes and seed availability (hurts processed-volume EBIT), so the net is volume-driven downside. (3) **Bunker fuel and crude oil** are correlated — an extended Middle East crude shock both helps the crush spread and hurts ocean-freight margin in Grain Merchandising, with a net positive for the consolidated EBITDA but mix-shifting between segments. (4) **Interest expense and crush-spread weakness** can compound if crush margins compress in a high-rate environment, accelerating the Net Debt / Adj. EBITDA ratio (already 4.72x at FY25-end) toward levels that could pressure leverage covenants. The pre-Viterra balance sheet would have absorbed a crush-spread downturn; the post-Viterra balance sheet has less room.

## 6. Non-Linear Or Asymmetric Risks

Several material asymmetries exist:

- **Crush-spread downside is wider than upside given current macro setup.** Q1 2026 already reflects a benefit from Iran-conflict crude pull-through and RVO endorsement. Holding both of those constant, further crush-spread upside is bounded by historical FY23 peak levels, whereas the downside path (RVO softening, China trade reset, SA bumper crop) is open-ended toward the FY24 trough. Asymmetry: **bear/bull ratio ~1.3x** ($900m down vs $700m up).
- **Operating deleverage on poor European sunflower crop.** Softseed plant overhead is fixed; a third consecutive poor crop would not only compress margins but force utilisation cuts, with EBIT margin able to drop below 4% — non-linear erosion below a certain utilisation threshold.
- **Leverage covenant proximity.** Net Debt / Adj. EBITDA was 4.72x at FY25-end. A simultaneous crush-spread reversion (–$900m EBITDA) and bunker-fuel persistence (–$100m EBITDA) would push the ratio above 5.5x and could trigger covenant scrutiny on the Viterra-financing facilities. The marginal dollar of EBITDA lost in that range has disproportionate financing-cost consequences. *Inference, not from filings — covenant levels not disclosed in available data.*
- **Adjusted-to-GAAP gap widens non-linearly with mark-to-market timing volatility.** Q1 2026 already showed a $336m MTM swing at Segment EBIT (49% of Adj. Segment EBIT). In a high-volatility commodity tape, GAAP EPS can diverge materially from Adjusted EPS — a 4x gap was already observed in Q1 2026 ($0.35 GAAP vs $1.83 Adjusted). This is a quality-of-earnings asymmetry, not an EBITDA asymmetry, but it affects the headline reaction to any earnings print.
- **FX is asymmetric vs. company disclosure.** Item 7A says 10% FX move is "not material" on net positions, yet realised segment FX P&L has swung +$100m in a single year (Softseed alone, FY25 vs FY24). The hedge math captures the position book; it does not capture the operational FX flowing through segment costs and revenue. **Adverse FX shocks during high-volatility commodity moves can compound.**

## 7. Earnings Volatility Score

**Score: 78 / 100** *(higher = WORSE; band: high volatility — multiple variables with large impact)*

Reason: The single dominant variable (crush spread) alone has a documented ±$800m EBITDA / ±$6.50 EPS swing range — larger than the 2026 EPS guidance midpoint itself — and is fully external. Three of the top four variables are externally driven (crush spread, RVO, crude), with no contractual pass-through, and they tend to move together. The Item 7A disclosed sensitivities (10% adverse commodity move = $131m, ±100bps rates = $78m, FX "not material") are the **position-book** sensitivities and materially understate the structural earnings sensitivity. Score is not 81+ because Bunge has genuine N/S hemisphere geographic diversification and active hedging programs that smooth the tail, and because management's adjusted-EPS series has been less volatile than GAAP would suggest ($12.93 → $13.91 → $13.66 → $9.19 → $7.57 over FY21–FY25 is wide but directional rather than chaotic). Score is not below 70 because the FY23→FY24 Total EBIT decline of $1.5B / 46% on commodity-cycle factors alone demonstrates that the next 12 months' EPS can plausibly land anywhere from ~$5 to ~$12 against a $9.00–$9.50 guidance band.
