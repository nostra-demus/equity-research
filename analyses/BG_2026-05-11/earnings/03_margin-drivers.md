# Margin Drivers — BG

## 1. Segment Decomposition Status

**Multi-segment, decomposition feasible.** Per the cross-module business-model output `analyses/BG_2026-05-11/business-model/03_segment-map.md`, BG operates four reportable segments (recast Q3 2025): Soybean Processing & Refining (51.6% of FY25 revenue, 52.6% of segment EBIT), Softseed Processing & Refining (16.0%/22.4%), Other Oilseeds Processing & Refining renamed Tropical Oils & Specialty Ingredients in Q1 2026 (6.6%/5.1%), and Grain Merchandising & Milling (25.8%/20.0%). Soybean is the dominant segment but no segment exceeds 85%, so consolidated and segment-level analyses are both required. The 10-K provides full per-segment cost decomposition (Raw materials, Industrial fixed, Industrial variable, Depreciation, SG&A) for FY23, FY24, and FY25 in Note 26 — disclosure quality is high. Caveat: comparability is impaired because the Viterra acquisition closed July 2, 2025, so FY25 segment cost stacks include Viterra for only the second half. Pre-Viterra organic margin trend is best read from FY23→FY24 and from quarterly data within 1H 2025. [FY2025 10-K, Note 26, pp.F-77/F-78]

## 3. Gross Margin → EBITDA Margin → EBIT Margin Walk

| Margin Level | FY2025 | FY2024 | Change bps | Main Reason | Evidence |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|---|
| Gross margin | 4.85% | 6.39% | −154 bps | Lower segment realizations: Soybean (lower prices ex Argentina) and Softseed (sunflower drought lifted nets but COGS also spiked); higher raw materials as % of revenue | [FY2025 10-K, p.F-10; MD&A pp.38–40] |
| EBITDA margin (filing-derived = EBIT + D&A) | 3.18% | 4.25% | −108 bps | Same crush-margin compression flowing through; higher SG&A absolute (Viterra integration); partially offset by Viterra revenue addition lifting denominator | [FY2025 10-K, p.37 + p.F-13] |
| EBIT margin (company-defined Total EBIT) | 2.18% | 3.37% | −119 bps | Corporate & Other EBIT widened to -$796m from -$367m: $118m pension settlement loss, $30m impairment, absence of $195m prior-year BP Bunge Bioenergia gain, +Viterra integration costs $223m | [FY2025 10-K, p.37, p.43] |

**Pass-through lag.** From the cross-module value-chain output: BG has **no contractual cost pass-through**. Raw-material movement and selling-price movement are both set by commodity exchanges and the crush-spread "gap" is itself a market price. Energy and freight are explicitly NOT passed through — the Q1 2026 transcript confirms bunker fuel cost spikes absorbed by ocean freight margin in Grain Merchandising [Q1 2026 transcript, prepared remarks p.3-4]. Effective pass-through lag: indeterminate for crush spreads (gap can persist for quarters — FY24 Soybean EBIT fell 61% on rising volumes purely from spread compression); zero pass-through on freight/energy.

## 4. Margin Walk — Which Margin Level Matters Most?

**EBIT margin (and especially Segment EBIT margin) is the most useful primary margin metric for Bunge.** Three reasons. (1) Gross margin is technically calculable but mechanically uninformative for a commodity merchandiser/crusher: roughly 90% of revenue is the raw-material commodity, so any 1–2% move in commodity price relative to selling price swings gross margin disproportionately versus the underlying operating economics. The crush spread — what management actually optimizes against — sits inside the consolidated "Cost of goods sold" line and is not directly observable from gross profit. (2) EBITDA margin is contaminated by mark-to-market timing differences (Q1 2026 MTM adjustment was $336m at Segment EBIT — material relative to $661m Adj. Segment EBIT) and by the rising D&A burden post-Viterra ($703m FY25 → guided $975m FY26 = +39%), which distorts the picture without revealing operating health. (3) Segment EBIT, especially **Adjusted Segment EBIT** that strips MTM timing, is the metric management itself reports and guides on ("Adjusted segment EBIT was $661m in the quarter versus $406m last year" — [Q1 2026 transcript, prepared remarks p.4]), and the underlying segment-EBIT margin (Soybean P&R EBIT margin 3.4% FY25 vs 6.1% FY23) is the cleanest read on crush economics that drive the franchise.

## 8. The Single Biggest Margin Driver

**The global oilseed crush spread — particularly the soybean crush in South America and North America — is the single biggest margin driver.** It is the largest line by far (Soybean P&R is 52% of revenue and 53% of segment EBIT), it has the largest demonstrated swing magnitude (Soybean P&R EBIT collapsed from $2,222m FY23 to $872m FY24 — a $1,350m / 60% drop on rising volumes — purely on crush-spread compression with no contractual offset), and it is the most upstream variable Bunge cannot control. Current direction is **Tailwind** as of Q1 2026: the EPA RVO decision endorsed strong renewable-diesel volumes, the Middle East conflict has lifted crude prices and made discretionary biofuel blending economic, meal demand continues to surprise to the upside on strong meat economics, and South American crush is benefiting from expanded Viterra origination in Argentina. The 2026 EPS guidance was raised from $7.50–$8.00 to $9.00–$9.50 (+19% midpoint) on this single driver complex. However, forward crush curves are deeply inverted into 2H 2026, signalling market uncertainty about durability — adverse moves in any of (a) U.S. RVO retreat, (b) crude oil collapse, (c) bumper South American crop crushing margins, or (d) China–US trade reset reducing U.S. soy demand could compress Soybean P&R margins back toward the FY24 trough. There is no contractual offset and no pricing power: per the cross-module value-chain output, Bunge is a price-taker on both legs of the crush spread.
