# Margin Drivers — BG

## 1. Segment Decomposition Status

The business-model `03_segment-map.md` exists and identifies four reportable segments (Soybean Processing & Refining, Softseed Processing & Refining, Tropical Oils & Specialty Ingredients [formerly Other Oilseeds], Grain Merchandising & Milling) plus Corporate & Other. None is >85% of revenue (Soybean P&R is 51.6% of FY25 revenue [FY25 10-K, p.37; Note 26, p.F-77]), so segment decomposition applies.

Segment-level cost breakdown IS disclosed at a granularity unusual for a US filer: the Q1 2026 10-Q breaks each segment into Raw Materials, Industrial Expenses – Fixed, Industrial Expenses – Variable, Depreciation, and SG&A [Q1 2026 10-Q, Note 19, p.32]. This is far better than the annual 10-K, which discloses only the top-of-funnel: net sales, COGS, SG&A, FX, and EBIT [FY25 10-K, p.38–43]. I use the Q1 2026 segment cost breakdown to anchor cost structure, then layer in annual MD&A commentary on direction.

Major distortion: Viterra (closed July 2, 2025) is in three of the last four quarters but not in prior-year comparatives. Year-over-year margin moves at the consolidated level are partly mix from Viterra, not organic.

The single most useful observation: Raw materials are ~91% of revenue. Bunge's gross margin is a thin spread on a very large pass-through cost base. A 100 bps move in raw-material-cost-to-revenue is mechanically a ~100 bps gross margin move. This is the structural reality of commodity crushing.

**Pass-through lag**: Bunge has NO contractual pass-through on raw materials, energy, or freight [business-model 06_value-chain.md; FY25 10-K, Item 1A, p.25]. The crush spread (oil price + meal price − soybean price) is itself a CBOT-quoted market spread. The Q1 2026 call confirms management's framing: "we don't lock in the margin until we have all three legs priced" [Q1 2026 transcript, p.11], i.e., margins are locked in real time at the moment of trade, not passed through. Bunker fuel costs are NOT passed through to ocean freight customers — they hit Grain Merchandising directly in Q1 2026 [Q1 2026 transcript, p.4]. The implication: input cost changes flow into margin instantly, but Bunge can lay off risk via futures hedging, which converts a price-direction bet into an execution-quality bet.

## 4. Margin Walk — Which Margin Level Matters Most?

**Gross margin is the most useful margin level for Bunge.** Three reasons. First, raw materials are ~91% of revenue [Q1'26 segment data, Note 19, p.32] — gross margin captures the crush spread directly, which IS the business. Second, EBITDA and EBIT contain too many non-cash and one-off items (FY25 had a $118m pension settlement, $30m impairment, and $223m Viterra integration cost dumped into Corp & Other [FY25 10-K, p.43]) which distort the underlying economic picture. Third, gross margin is reported quarterly and directly comparable across segments, while company-defined "Total EBIT" excludes interest and tax but mixes corporate overhead into a single drag line. The trade-off: gross margin is volatile (compressed 329 bps from FY23 to FY25 [FY25 10-K, p.F-10]) and a single quarter is noisy because of mark-to-market timing differences, so it should be read on a TTM basis. Management's own focus is on "adjusted segment EBIT" because that is where their hedging-discipline story lives, but for an outside analyst trying to read structural change vs. spread-cycle noise, gross margin remains the cleanest summary metric.

## 8. The Single Biggest Margin Driver

**The soybean crush spread is the single biggest margin driver.** A reasonable move in soy crush spreads — for example, the FY23→FY24 compression that took Soybean P&R segment EBIT from $2,222m to $872m on rising tonnage [FY25 10-K, MD&A, p.38] — moves consolidated EBIT by ~$1.35bn, roughly 1.9% of FY25 revenue. That single driver swing alone is larger than the entire FY25 Total EBIT ($1,533m) [FY25 10-K, p.37]. Magnitude is unambiguously **High**.

Current direction is **Tailwind**: management raised FY26 adjusted EPS guidance from $7.50–8.00 to $9.00–9.50, an increase of ~20% mid-point, citing "strength of Q1 and the forward curves as we look at the balance of the year," with the soybean and softseed processing segments specifically called out as the contributors [Q1 2026 transcript, p.5]. The favorable EPA RVO decision and elevated crude prices (Middle East conflict) are both pulling soy oil into renewable diesel and supporting the oil-leg of the crush [Q1 2026 transcript, p.3, p.8]. But the forward curves are "heavily inverted" — meaning the spot strength is not expected to persist through H2 2026 [Q1 2026 transcript, p.8] — so management's own guidance reflects 40% H1 / 60% H2 weighting, with H2 dependent on factors (China-US trade, Argentine farmer selling, El Niño, RIN restriction delays) that are visible-but-unknowable.

If the crush spread reverses (oil demand stalls, meal weakens, or bean prices spike on a Northern-hemisphere weather event), the same magnitude move could compress consolidated EBIT by ~$1bn+ — a margin compression that would dwarf every other lever (Viterra synergies, SG&A, freight, FX) combined.
