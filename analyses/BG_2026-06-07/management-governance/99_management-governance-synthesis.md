# Management-Governance Module — BG (Synthesis)

## Abstract

Bunge's professional management team — CEO Greg Heckman and CFO John Neppl, both in seat since 2019 — has delivered on most of what it promised, executing portfolio simplification, meeting annual EPS guidance in three of the last four years, and integrating Viterra ahead of schedule on costs in its first six months. Capital allocation is mixed: the FY2023–FY2024 buyback program meaningfully reduced share count at reasonable prices, but the $10.6 billion Viterra acquisition — financed with new shares and new debt — expanded the share count by 44%, tripled net debt to $12.9 billion, and has not yet proven per-share value creation at less than one year post-close. Incentive alignment leans positive, with ROIC and relative TSR driving the long-term plan, but the annual cash bonus funds off absolute adjusted profit, a size metric that paid out at 175% of target in a year when GAAP EPS fell sharply. Insider ownership is entirely compensation-derived with no open-market purchasing; the biggest governance overhang is the Glencore/CPP bloc (~34% of shares, four board seats including three on the Enterprise Risk Management Committee) acquired as Viterra deal consideration — a structural minority-protection risk that has not yet surfaced as a conflict but creates the conditions for one. Stewardship is standard to mixed: competent and mostly candid professionals running a demanding integration, with the Viterra bet as the defining open question.

## 5. Stewardship Summary

Heckman and Neppl have run Bunge for seven years with no surprise departures, no governance scandals, and a record of meeting their stated annual targets in three of four years — a satisfactory track record for a professional management pair at a large commodity processor. The operational foundation is solid: portfolio simplification executed as planned, and Viterra synergies tracking ahead of schedule in the first six months.

The Viterra acquisition is the one decision that overshadows everything else. At $10.6 billion — more than Bunge's entire standalone market capitalization at the time — funded with 65.6 million new shares and $7.8 billion of new debt, it reversed two years of hard-won per-share improvement in a single transaction. It has been open for less than a year, so a fair verdict requires more time, but the structural costs (44% dilution, 4.4× net debt increase, board-nomination rights conceded to Glencore and CPP) are permanent unless actively reversed through buybacks, deleveraging, and sustained earnings growth.

Incentives point management mostly in the right direction. The long-term plan — which is 77% of CEO total compensation — rewards EPS, return on capital, and relative shareholder return, not absolute profit or deal count. The annual cash bonus, the smaller component, does fund off absolute adjusted profit, and it paid out at 175% of target in a year when GAAP EPS fell 39% and free cash flow turned negative — the predictable consequence of a size-based metric after a major acquisition. This is the single most visible structural misalignment.

The single strongest reason to trust this team is the completed operational turnaround: the 2019–2023 record is documented in audited filings and shows real delivery, not just a pitch. The single strongest reason for caution is that the defining decision of the current era — Viterra — has been bought, paid for, and partially financed with shareholders' capital, but has not yet earned its keep on a per-share basis, while the management letter to shareholders framed FY2025 as a "year of transformation" without mentioning the GAAP EPS decline, the negative free cash flow, or the dilution cost.
