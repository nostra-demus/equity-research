## 5. Minority-Shareholder Protection Read

Bunge's formal governance architecture is shareholder-friendly: independent Chair separate from CEO, 11 of 12 directors independent, all directors elected annually under majority voting, no poison pill, no staggered board, no dual-class stock, a 5%-threshold special-meeting right, and the Swiss-law supermajority requirement for major corporate actions that actually constrains management unilaterally. Related-party transactions are with unconsolidated investees at arm's length, well below the threshold of concern, and there is no promoter or controlling-family group to exploit minority holders.

The real minority-protection risk is specific and structural: Glencore (~17% stake) and CPP Investments (~14% stake) together hold approximately 31% of Bunge shares and have contractual rights to four of twelve board seats. Three of those four nominated directors sit on the Enterprise Risk Management Committee. If Glencore's or CPP's interests — as sophisticated commodity-trading and asset-management entities — diverge from those of minority holders on a future strategic decision (e.g., a new acquisition, capital allocation between segments, or a transaction with Glencore as counterparty), the bloc has both the board representation to shape governance outcomes and, combined with BCI's ~3%, a combined ~34% voting position that could be determinative in a supermajority vote. This is not a current problem; it is a structural overhang that could become one. There is no RF-OWN-004 trigger here (no government control, no listed subsidiary of a value-maximizing parent, no unrelated conglomerate in the §24 Filter 6 sense) — Glencore is a co-strategic investor from the Viterra deal, and both Glencore and CPP are long-term financial and strategic holders, not predatory controllers. The board also added neutral independent directors (Jojo, Browner, etc.) who provide counterbalance.

**Overall verdict: Adequate.** Formal governance is sound and above-average for a US large-cap. Minority holders are protected by the structural safeguards. The Glencore/CPP board-representation bloc is a latent structural risk — it is disclosed, contractual, and currently operating without conflict, but it is the single biggest entrenchment or self-dealing risk: if the two anchor shareholders' interests diverged from minority holders on a major decision, their combined board seats and voting bloc give them material leverage. Until that potential conflict actually surfaces, "adequate" rather than "strong" is the right characterization.

**Biggest entrenchment / self-dealing risk to minority holders:** The four board seats held by Glencore and CPP nominees (with three on the operationally critical Enterprise Risk Management Committee), backed by a combined ~34% voting bloc, create a structural channel through which the two largest shareholders could potentially influence major strategic decisions in ways that benefit them as sophisticated commodity-trading and capital-allocation entities — rather than per-share value maximization for the minority. No such conflict has materialized, but the architecture makes it possible.
